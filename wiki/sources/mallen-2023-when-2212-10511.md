## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature.

This paper studies when large language models should be trusted to answer factual questions from parametric memory alone and when they should rely on retrieval. Using ten GPT-Neo, OPT, and GPT-3 variants on long-tail open-domain QA, the authors show that factual memorization is strongly predicted by subject-entity popularity and relationship type: scaling mostly improves answers about popular entities, while long-tail facts remain difficult. They introduce POPQA, a `~14k`-question benchmark grounded in Wikidata, compare parametric and retrieval-augmented systems, and show that retrieval is most helpful for unpopular entities but can hurt on popular ones. Based on this asymmetry, they propose Adaptive Retrieval, a simple popularity-threshold policy that improves accuracy while lowering retrieval and API cost.

## Problem & Motivation

Large language models exhibit strong closed-book factual recall on many benchmarks, but those benchmarks are often dominated by popular entities and do not reveal where memorization breaks down. The paper asks three concrete questions: how factual memorization varies across entities and relations, how much non-parametric memory complements parametric memory, and whether retrieval can be turned on only when it is likely to help. The motivation is both scientific and practical: if long-tail factual knowledge is poorly memorized, blindly trusting LM answers is unsafe, but always retrieving also increases latency and can inject misleading evidence.

## Method

- **Task setup**: study factual memorization via open-domain QA, where each fact is represented as a triple `(subject, relation, object)` and correctness is measured by exact substring match against any gold answer.
- **Benchmarks**: introduce POPQA with `~14k` questions built from Wikidata triples covering `16` relationship types, and also evaluate on EntityQuestions using the `82%` subset whose subject mention maps uniquely to a Wikidata entity.
- **Popularity signal**: use subject-entity popularity from monthly Wikipedia page views as a proxy for how often a fact is discussed on the web, and analyze performance against `log_10(popularity)` together with relationship type.
- **Model suite**: evaluate `10` prompted LMs without fine-tuning: OPT `1.3B/2.7B/6.7B/13B`, GPT-Neo `1.3B/2.7B/6B/20B`, and GPT-3 `davinci-002/davinci-003`.
- **Prompting**: use the simple template ``Q: <question> A:``; GPT-3 is run in `0-shot` mode to reduce API cost, while GPT-Neo and OPT models use `15-shot` prompting.
- **Retrieval augmentation**: retrieve the `top-1` Wikipedia paragraph offline and concatenate it with the question; compare lexical BM25, dense Contriever, and the parametric context-generation baseline GenRead.
- **Adaptive Retrieval**: choose a per-relationship popularity threshold on a development set; if a question falls below the threshold, use the retrieval-augmented system, otherwise trust parametric predictions from the vanilla LM.
- **Efficiency analysis**: measure not only QA accuracy but also latency and API cost, testing whether selective retrieval reduces overhead relative to always-retrieve pipelines.

## Key Results

- On POPQA, vanilla GPT-3 reaches about `35%` accuracy and GPT-Neo `20B` reaches about `25%`, showing that LMs memorize substantial factual knowledge but remain far from saturated.
- Popularity strongly predicts memorization: GPT-3 `davinci-003` shows roughly `0.4` correlation between accuracy and subject popularity, while GPT-Neo `1.3B` is closer to `0.1`.
- Scaling helps mostly on popular facts; on the `4,000` least popular POPQA questions, GPT-Neo `6B`, GPT-Neo `20B`, and GPT-3 `davinci-003` score only `15%`, `16%`, and `19%` accuracy.
- Retrieval augmentation materially helps on the long tail: Contriever adds about `7%` absolute accuracy on top of GPT-3 `davinci-003`, and a Contriever-augmented GPT-Neo `2.7B` can outperform vanilla GPT-3 on the least popular questions.
- Retrieval is not uniformly beneficial: for the `10%` of questions where retrieval turns a correct GPT-3 answer into an incorrect one, recall@1 is only `0.14` versus `0.42` overall; for the `17%` where retrieval fixes a failure, recall@1 rises to `0.88`.
- Adaptive Retrieval yields the best POPQA result: GPT-3 `davinci-003` combined adaptively with GenRead and Contriever reaches `46.5%` accuracy, `5.3%` above any non-adaptive alternative, while reducing GPT-3 API cost by about half and lowering latency by up to `9%`.

## Limitations

- The analysis is confined to entity-centric factual knowledge and does not establish that the same patterns hold for commonsense or other less structured knowledge types.
- POPQA and parts of the evaluation are synthetic, so the transfer from template-generated questions to naturally occurring QA is not guaranteed.
- Popularity is approximated by Wikipedia page views, a time-sensitive and imperfect proxy for how frequently facts appear in pretraining corpora or on the web.
- Adaptive Retrieval uses a simple heuristic threshold rather than a learned decision rule, so effectiveness may vary across QA pipelines, retrievers, and domains.
- The retrieval setup uses only the `top-1` paragraph for simplicity; stronger or different retrieval stacks could change the measured tradeoff between parametric and non-parametric memory.
