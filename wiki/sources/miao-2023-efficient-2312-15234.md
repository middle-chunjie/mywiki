## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature.

This survey systematizes efficient generative large language model serving from both algorithmic and systems perspectives. Starting from Transformer attention `softmax(QK^T / sqrt(d_k)) V` and autoregressive decoding `y_t = argmax_y P(y | X_{t-1})`, it organizes the design space into algorithmic innovations such as non-autoregressive decoding, speculative decoding, early exit, architectural changes, and compression, alongside system optimizations such as quantization, parallelism, memory management, request scheduling, and kernel design. The paper also compares representative GPU serving stacks including FasterTransformer, FlexFlow-Serve, vLLM, FlexGen, TGI, and TensorRT-LLM, and frames deployment trade-offs through latency and throughput metrics such as TTFT and TPOT. Its main contribution is a broad taxonomy and synthesis of open problems rather than a new serving algorithm.

## Problem & Motivation

Generative LLMs deliver strong task performance, but their deployment cost is dominated by large model weights, autoregressive token-by-token decoding, attention-state growth, and the need to satisfy low-latency, high-throughput service objectives simultaneously. The paper is motivated by the gap between algorithmic advances in LLM capability and the practical difficulty of serving these models efficiently under real workloads. It aims to provide an MLSys-oriented map of the solution space so that researchers and practitioners can reason about where latency, memory, communication, and scheduling bottlenecks arise, and which families of methods address them with what trade-offs.

## Method

- The survey anchors efficient serving in the standard Transformer primitives: self-attention `Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QK^T / sqrt(d_k)) V` and feed-forward blocks `FFN(x) = max(0, xW_1 + b_1) W_2 + b_2`, emphasizing that both attention and FFN dominate compute and memory costs.
- It formalizes autoregressive inference as `y_t = argmax_y P(y | X_{t-1})` with sequence update `X_t = concat(X_{t-1}, y_t)`, then uses this sequential dependency to motivate acceleration methods that either reduce the number of decoding steps or increase per-step parallelism.
- The taxonomy splits prior work into two top-level axes: algorithmic innovation and system optimization. Algorithmic methods include non-autoregressive decoding, speculative decoding, early exiting, cascade inference, architectural redesigns, and compression-oriented approaches.
- For compression and low-precision execution, the paper highlights distillation, structured and unstructured pruning, and practical quantization configurations such as `W8A16`, `W4A16`, `W8A8`, and `W4A4`, with attention to both hardware support and numerical degradation.
- For distributed execution, it reviews tensor and pipeline model parallelism, sequence parallelism, cloud scaling, and decentralized inference, treating communication and runtime cost models as first-class design constraints.
- For memory efficiency, it centers the dynamic growth of the KV cache and surveys paged or token-level allocation, offloading to CPU DRAM / SSD, and cache-aware system architectures for multi-turn or long-context workloads.
- For online serving, it compares request-level versus iteration-level scheduling, continuous or inflight batching, preemption, chunked-prefill schemes, and prefill/decode disaggregation under heterogeneous request mixes.
- For kernel-level performance, it covers kernel fusion, specialized attention kernels for prefill and decode, variable-length batching strategies, and compiler-driven code generation, then analyzes how representative software stacks combine these choices.
- The evaluation lens is explicitly systems-oriented: throughput, latency, TTFT, and TPOT, with total response time decomposed as `TTFT + TPOT * output_length`.

## Key Results

- The survey organizes the field into `2` primary dimensions and at least `8` recurring technique families: decoding, architecture design, model compression, low-bit quantization, parallel computation, memory management, request scheduling, and kernel optimization.
- Table 2 compares `10` representative open-source GPU LLM serving systems: FasterTransformer, FlexFlow-Serve, vLLM, FlexGen, TGI, DeepSpeed-Inference, ZeRO-Inference, LightLLM, MLC-LLM, and TensorRT-LLM.
- The quantization section highlights production-relevant precision regimes `W8A16`, `W4A16`, `W8A8`, and `W4A4`; it also notes that Hopper-class hardware introduces FP8 tensor cores and reports H100 peak throughput figures up to roughly `60x` FP32-level TFLOPS.
- The pruning/compression synthesis reports that recent unstructured methods often reach about `50-60%` sparsity, and that these approaches can be extended to semi-structured `2:4` and `4:8` sparsity patterns for tensor-core acceleration.
- The paper argues that latency should be decomposed as `TTFT + TPOT * output_length`, which makes clear why prompt processing, decode iteration time, and request length distribution must be evaluated separately.

## Limitations

- This is a synthesis paper, not a head-to-head experimental benchmark; it does not reproduce or normalize all systems under a single controlled setup.
- The discussion is GPU-centric and focuses mainly on open-source serving engines, so CPUs, mobile/edge deployments, TPUs, and ASICs receive less detailed treatment.
- Because the metadata is anchored to the `2023` arXiv version in a fast-moving `llm` domain, several framework and systems comparisons may now be outdated.
- The paper itself stresses that optimizations interact nonlinearly: a method that improves memory efficiency or throughput in one workload can worsen latency or scheduling behavior in another.
