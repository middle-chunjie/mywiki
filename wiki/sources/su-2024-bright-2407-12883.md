## Summary

BRIGHT introduces a text retrieval benchmark aimed at cases where relevance cannot be detected by lexical overlap or embedding similarity alone, but instead requires substantive reasoning about the query and candidate documents. The benchmark contains `1,384` queries across `12` datasets spanning StackExchange, coding, and theorem-driven math settings, with corpora drawn from web pages, documentation, problem-solution pairs, and theorem statements. The paper evaluates `13` sparse, dense, open, and proprietary retrievers and shows that performance collapses relative to conventional retrieval leaderboards: SFR drops from `59.0 nDCG@10` on MTEB retrieval to `18.3` on BRIGHT. LLM-generated reasoning traces, LLM reranking, and downstream QA analyses show that explicit reasoning helps, but even the strongest systems remain far from solving reasoning-intensive retrieval.

## Problem & Motivation

The paper argues that standard IR benchmarks mostly test keyword matching or high-level semantic matching, while many real user tasks require a third regime where the query-document connection is only recoverable through deductive, causal, analogical, or analytical reasoning. Examples include locating syntax documentation from a bug description, identifying theorem statements useful for a math problem, or finding web pages that explain a real-world scenario through the same underlying principle. The authors introduce BRIGHT to stress-test retrieval systems on these harder relevance judgments using realistic queries and naturally occurring documents rather than synthetic answer strings.

## Method

- **Task formulation**: for a query `Q`, the retriever ranks a corpus `D = {D_1, ..., D_|D|}` where positives `D_Q^+` are documents judged helpful for solving or explaining the query; the main metric is `nDCG@10`, and the long-context variant uses `Recall@1`.
- **Benchmark composition**: BRIGHT contains `12` datasets and `1,384` queries: `7` StackExchange domains, `2` coding datasets (LeetCode, Pony), and `3` theorem-based datasets (AoPS, TheoremQA-Q, TheoremQA-T). Corpus sizes range from `7,894` documents (Pony) to `413,932` (LeetCode).
- **StackExchange construction**: queries come from accepted or highly upvoted posts; positive documents are URLs cited in answers and then manually verified by multiple annotators. Only unanimously accepted positives are retained. Annotators also mine up to `5` hard negatives per query and split long pages using delimiters such as double newlines or markdown `#`.
- **Coding/math construction**: Pony uses coding instructions as queries and syntax documentation as positives. TheoremQA questions are rewritten with GPT-4 into more applied surface forms while preserving the theorem. For theorem retrieval, the authors first match theorem names and run BM25 over ProofWiki with `k = 10`, then use GPT-4 plus manual checking to validate positives.
- **False-negative control**: in LeetCode, candidate negatives are filtered by topic overlap, excluding `Q'` when `|T(Q) ∩ T(Q')| / |T(Q)| >= 0.5`; in theorem datasets, metadata-based exclusions are used to avoid semantically overlapping solved problems.
- **Reasoning categories**: the benchmark is explicitly organized around deductive, analogical, causal, and analytical reasoning, rather than simple lexical or semantic similarity.
- **Retriever suite**: `13` models are benchmarked, including BM25, small encoders (SBERT, BGE, Instructor-Large), large instruction-tuned models (Instructor-XL, E5-Mistral, GritLM, SFR, gte-Qwen1.5), and proprietary embeddings from Cohere, Voyage, OpenAI, and Google. Context limits range from `512` to `8192` tokens depending on model.
- **Prompted retrieval**: to augment queries, LLMs generate reasoning traces with a three-step prompt: `(1)` identify the essential problem, `(2)` reason step by step about what relevant documents should contain, `(3)` draft an answer. The paper tests GPT-4, GritLM, and Llama-3-70B-Instruct as query rewriters.
- **Reranking and QA**: the authors rerank top `k ∈ {10, 100}` candidates using MiniLM, Gemini, and GPT-4. They also test retrieval-augmented QA with Claude-3.5 Sonnet as both generator and evaluator, comparing closed-book, retrieved-document, and oracle-document settings.
- **Leakage robustness experiment**: GritLM is continually trained with contrastive and language-modeling objectives on StackExchange data using `3,200` QA pairs per domain (`1,538` for Sustainable Living), document chunks of `2048` tokens, batch size `64`, and `10` epochs to test whether in-domain exposure alone improves BRIGHT.

## Key Results

- **Benchmark difficulty**: under original queries, the best average score in Table 2 is Qwen at `22.5 nDCG@10`; BM25 reaches `14.5`, while SFR scores only `18.3` despite `59.0 nDCG@10` on MTEB retrieval.
- **Query augmentation helps but does not solve the task**: LLM-written reasoning traces improve retrieval by up to `12.2` absolute points according to the paper, yet the best reported reasoning-enhanced performance still remains below `30 nDCG@10`.
- **Reranking is mixed**: MiniLM hurts BM25 badly (`14.3 -> 8.3` when reranking top `100`), while GPT-4 reranking improves BM25 from `14.3` to `17.4` at top `10` and improves Google retrieval from `19.5` to `22.6` at top `100`.
- **Downstream QA benefits from stronger retrieval**: on StackExchange QA, Claude-3.5 Sonnet goes from `77.7` closed-book average to `79.6` with Qwen retrieval (`+1.9`), while oracle documents raise the average to `81.8` (`+4.1`).
- **Long-context retrieval stays hard**: in the unsplit long-document setting, the best average `Recall@1` is only `27.8` (Qwen), compared with `11.4` for BM25.
- **Pretraining-style exposure does not fix the benchmark**: continued training of GritLM on StackExchange data changes average BRIGHT performance from `20.5` to `20.4`, supporting the claim that corpus familiarity alone is insufficient without stronger reasoning.

## Limitations

- Relevance judgments remain partly subjective even though the authors use unanimous annotation and domain-expert review for retained positives.
- Several construction steps rely on LLM assistance, including TheoremQA question rewriting and theorem verification, so benchmark artifacts from prompting or model bias are possible.
- The paper demonstrates benchmark difficulty and diagnostic interventions, but it does not introduce a new retriever architecture that closes the gap; even the best settings remain well below practical performance.
- The benchmark is text-only and leaves multimodal reasoning-intensive retrieval as future work.
- The long-context setting reduces the search space and switches metrics to `Recall@1`, so it does not fully cover open-ended web-scale long-context retrieval.
