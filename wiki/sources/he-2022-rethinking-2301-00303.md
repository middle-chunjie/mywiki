## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2022; re-verify against recent literature.

The paper proposes Rethinking with Retrieval (RR), a post-processing inference framework that augments large language models with external knowledge without additional training or fine-tuning. RR first samples diverse chain-of-thought reasoning paths, then retrieves evidence for each decomposed reasoning step, and finally reweights candidate answers by a faithfulness score grounded in the retrieved evidence. The method is evaluated with GPT-3 on commonsense, temporal, and tabular reasoning, using Wikipedia, Wikidata, WordNet, and ConceptNet as task-specific knowledge sources. RR improves both answer accuracy and explanation faithfulness over zero-shot, few-shot, chain-of-thought, and self-consistency baselines, while the analysis shows that decomposition-based retrieval is materially better than retrieving from the original query alone.

## Problem & Motivation

Large language models can produce plausible multi-step explanations while still relying on incomplete, outdated, or incorrect stored knowledge. The authors argue that this creates two failure modes: wrong supporting facts and wrong inference over otherwise relevant facts. Existing retrieval-enhanced approaches often require retriever training, fine-tuning, or architectural changes, which are expensive for very large models. RR is motivated as a lightweight alternative that keeps the base LLM fixed and instead uses external knowledge only at inference time, with the specific goal of making explanations more faithful to evidence rather than merely improving answer accuracy.

## Method

- RR samples diverse chain-of-thought reasoning paths `R_1, ..., R_N`, where each path is `R_i = (E_i, P_i)` with explanation `E_i` and prediction `P_i`; in the main evaluation, self-consistency and RR use `temperature = 0.7`, `max_tokens = 256`, and typically sample `N = 10` outputs.
- Final prediction uses faithful inference: `\hat{P} = argmax_P \sum_{i=1}^{N} 1(P_i = P) f_{\mathcal{KB}}(R_i)`, so answers supported by more faithful reasoning paths receive larger aggregate weight.
- For commonsense reasoning, RR retrieves the top `10` Wikipedia paragraphs per reasoning sentence with BM25 via Pyserini, selects the best paragraph by MPNet cosine similarity, then scores each sentence with an NLI model using entailment and contradiction against the selected paragraph.
- For temporal reasoning, RR links entities in each reasoning sentence to Wikidata, extracts temporal relations from the linked pages, and verbalizes those relations into evidence sentences before applying the same faithfulness-driven reranking.
- For tabular reasoning, RR converts tables into textual premises and augments them with WordNet and ConceptNet relation triples converted into sentences, such as lexical bridges between hypothesis and premise terms.
- The appendix defines task-specific faithfulness functions over MPNet similarity `M(s)`, entailment `E(s)`, and contradiction `C(s)` with thresholds `T_m = 0.5`, `T_e = 0.6`, and `T_c = 0.99`.
- The analysis section studies two stronger variants: fact selection replaces weak facts with better-supported ones from other sampled outputs using greedy clustering over MPNet embeddings, while fact generation asks questions about unsupported entities, answers them with evidence plus UnifiedQA, and rewrites the answers into declarative facts.

## Key Results

- On the main benchmark table, RR beats self-consistency on all three tasks with GPT-3: StrategyQA commonsense reasoning `77.73%` vs. `73.36%`, TempQuestions temporal reasoning `39.05` exact match vs. `37.28`, and InfoTabs tabular reasoning `84.83%` vs. `84.00%`.
- RR also outperforms plain chain-of-thought prompting by `+11.79` points on StrategyQA (`77.73%` vs. `65.94%`), `+5.91` on TempQuestions (`39.05` vs. `33.14`), and `+1.50` on InfoTabs (`84.83%` vs. `83.33%`).
- Decomposition-based retrieval is materially better than retrieving from the original query: `77.73%` vs. `73.36%` on commonsense reasoning and `39.05` vs. `36.69` on tabular reasoning.
- On StrategyQA with gold evidence paragraphs, the stronger RR variants improve both accuracy and explanation faithfulness over CoT: Variant I reaches `78.60%` accuracy and `54.11%` faithfulness, Variant II reaches `78.60%` and `54.54%`, while CoT is `65.94%` and `38.73%`.
- The ablation on tabular reasoning shows that tables already provide most of the gain: background knowledge alone yields `84.75%`, external lexical knowledge alone `79.92%`, and combining them `84.83%`.
- Retrieval choice matters for commonsense reasoning: BM25 reaches `77.73%`, clearly outperforming DPR at `58.52%` and slightly beating BM25+DPR at `77.29%`.

## Limitations

- RR depends on the quality of decomposition generated by chain-of-thought; if the sampled reasoning steps are off-topic, retrieval quality and downstream faithfulness scoring both degrade.
- The paper evaluates a single base LLM family in the main experiments, centered on `text-davinci-002` / GPT-3, so transfer to newer instruction-tuned or retrieval-native models is not established.
- Temporal reasoning still uses a small evaluation slice of `169` implicit temporal questions after filtering, and the overall gain over self-consistency is modest (`39.05` vs. `37.28`).
- The tabular ablation suggests external lexical resources add little beyond table-derived background knowledge, so RR's extra retrieval machinery is not equally valuable in every setting.
- Explanation faithfulness is estimated with proxy models such as MPNet and NLI rather than direct human verification, so the reranking objective can inherit errors from those scorers.
