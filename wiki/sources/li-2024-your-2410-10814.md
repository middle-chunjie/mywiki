## Summary

The paper argues that Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) large language models already contain a useful embedding signal in their router activations, even without extra representation finetuning. Instead of relying only on the decoder hidden state (HS), it extracts per-layer routing weights (RW) from MoE gates and shows that RW captures complementary, more prompt-robust semantic structure. Based on this observation, the authors propose MoEE, a training-free embedding method that combines HS and RW either by concatenating vectors or, more effectively, by summing their cosine similarities with a task-level weight. Across 6 MTEB task types and 20 datasets, MoEE consistently improves over standalone HS or RW on DeepSeekMoE, Qwen1.5-MoE, and OLMoE, with especially strong gains on STS, clustering, and re-ranking.

## Problem & Motivation

Decoder-only LLMs are strong generators, but their hidden states are optimized for next-token prediction rather than holistic sentence representation. As a result, off-the-shelf hidden-state embeddings can lag behind smaller encoder models or require additional finetuning and prompt engineering to become competitive on embedding tasks. The paper asks whether pretrained MoE LLMs can already serve as general-purpose embedding models without extra training. Its core motivation is that MoE routers expose intermediate routing decisions that may preserve input-sensitive semantics omitted by output-oriented hidden states, enabling stronger training-free embeddings.

## Method

- **Hidden-state baseline**: for an input sequence `x = [x_1, ..., x_T]` with final-layer hidden state `H^(L) in R^(T x d)`, the baseline sentence embedding is the last token representation `e_HS = H^(L)_T`; the paper also studies mean pooling and all-layer variants in ablations.
- **Routing-weight extraction**: at layer `l`, the MoE gate outputs routing probabilities `g^(l)(H^(l)) in R^(N^(l))`, with `g_i^(l)(H^(l)) = exp(z_i^(l)) / sum_j exp(z_j^(l))` and `sum_i g_i^(l) = 1`. These probabilities determine each expert's contribution to the sparse FFN computation.
- **Routing-weight embedding**: the paper concatenates routing distributions across all MoE layers to form `e_RW = [g^(1)(H^(1)); ...; g^(L)(H^(L))] in R^(sum_l N^(l))`, using the last token's routing weights because they outperform averaging over all tokens.
- **Complementarity analysis**: RW and HS are compared via k-means clustering, BERTopic topic inspection, prompt-robustness analysis, and STS error analysis to test whether they encode different semantic structure.
- **MoEE (concat)**: one combination strategy concatenates the two embeddings, `e_final = [e_HS; e_RW] in R^(d_HS + d_RW)`, preserving both output-oriented and routing-oriented signals in a single vector.
- **MoEE (sum)**: the stronger variant computes `sim_HS = cosine(e_HS(s_1), e_HS(s_2))` and `sim_RW = cosine(e_RW(s_1), e_RW(s_2))`, then combines them as `sim_final = sim_HS + alpha * sim_RW`, where `alpha` is a task-level weighting hyperparameter.
- **Evaluation setup**: experiments cover 6 task types and 20 MTEB datasets using DeepSeekMoE-16B (`28` layers, `64` experts/layer), Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B (`24` layers, `60` experts/layer), and OLMoE-1B-7B (`16` layers, `64` experts/layer), with and without PromptEOL.

## Key Results

- RW and HS are empirically complementary: clustering overlap is modest with `AMI = 0.29`, `NMI = 0.29`, `Jaccard = 0.06`, and only `45.54%` exact cluster matching.
- RW is more robust to prompt variation: mean prompt-to-prompt correlation is `0.63` for RW versus `0.52` for HS, while cross-family RW-HS correlation is only `0.51`.
- On the no-prompt MTEB average, DeepSeekMoE improves from `35.36` (HS) and `35.91` (RW) to `43.30` with MoEE (sum), a `22.45%` gain over HS; Qwen improves from `35.18` to `42.25`, and OLMoE from `36.26` to `42.69`.
- With PromptEOL, DeepSeekMoE improves from `42.30` (HS) to `53.28` with MoEE (sum), a `25.96%` gain; Qwen reaches `55.04`, and OLMoE reaches the best overall average of `55.16`.
- On STS ablations with DeepSeekMoE, the best MoEE variant reaches `71.75` average over STS12-16, versus `60.40` for last-token final-layer HS and `61.18` for RW alone.
- Representative task gains are large: DeepSeekMoE re-ranking rises from `38.13` (HS, no prompt) to `47.88` with MoEE (sum), and with PromptEOL its STS average rises from `59.66` to `70.66`.

## Limitations

- The method applies only to MoE LLMs with accessible router outputs; it does not transfer directly to dense decoder-only models.
- The strongest variant, MoEE (sum), is a similarity-level combination rather than a single fused embedding vector for all downstream uses, and it depends on the weighting hyperparameter `alpha`.
- Evaluation is restricted to text embedding benchmarks on `3` MoE backbones and `20` MTEB datasets; the paper does not test multilinguality, retrieval-heavy deployment settings, or non-text modalities.
- Prompt sensitivity is analyzed, but PromptEOL remains the main prompting strategy; robustness to broader instruction families is still underexplored.
- The paper does not introduce additional training or architecture changes, so absolute performance may still trail specialized embedding models in some settings.
