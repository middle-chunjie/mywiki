## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature.

Orca studies whether a 13B open model can learn not just the surface style of proprietary assistants but part of their reasoning process. Built on [[llama]], the model is trained with explanation-rich traces from [[chatgpt]] and [[gpt-4]] over a large FLAN-derived instruction mixture. The core idea is "explanation tuning": augmenting standard instruction-response pairs with system prompts, step-by-step justifications, and longer teacher outputs so the student sees richer supervision than vanilla imitation learning. Across open-ended generation, academic exams, and reasoning benchmarks, Orca substantially outperforms prior open 13B chat models such as [[vicuna-13b]], reaches near-parity with ChatGPT on some settings like Big-Bench Hard, but still trails GPT-4 and exposes clear weaknesses in math, long-context reasoning, hallucination, and safety evaluation coverage.

## Problem & Motivation

Earlier small instruction-tuned models such as Alpaca, WizardLM, and Vicuna could often mimic the conversational style of strong proprietary models, but their reasoning and comprehension lagged far behind. The paper argues this gap stems from three issues: weak supervision from plain `⟨query, response⟩` imitation, limited diversity and scale of synthetic instruction data, and over-optimistic evaluation protocols based on GPT-4-as-judge over small prompt sets. Orca is motivated by the hypothesis that richer explanation traces, broader task coverage, and stricter reasoning-heavy evaluation can close more of the capability gap for smaller open models.

## Method

- **Base model**: Orca is a `13B` parameter model built on [[llama]] and tuned to imitate stronger teachers with richer supervision than standard instruction tuning.
- **Training objective**: the paper proposes **explanation tuning**, where each example is `⟨system message, user query, LFM response⟩`; supervision includes explanation traces, step-by-step reasoning, and longer justifications rather than only terse answers.
- **Teacher setup**: responses are collected from [[chatgpt]] over `5M` FLAN-derived prompts and from [[gpt-4]] over a `1M` subset; the second stage uses GPT-4 after a first stage on ChatGPT, framing learning as a curriculum / progressive-teaching setup.
- **Prompt augmentation**: the authors handcraft `16` system messages to elicit different response styles, including detailed answers, explicit reasoning, instruction following, and simple explanations for multiple-choice tasks.
- **Data mixture**: training samples come from [[flan]]-v2 sub-collections with `150K` zero-shot CoT examples, `440K` NiV2 samples, `2.5M` FLAN 2021 samples, and `2M` T0 samples, while Dialogue is skipped due to missing context.
- **Tokenization and packing**: Orca uses the LLaMA BPE tokenizer with an added padding token `PAD`, giving vocabulary size `32,001`; sequences are packed to `max_len = 2048` with average packing factor `2.7` examples per sequence.
- **Loss**: training computes loss only on teacher-generated response tokens, conditioning on the system message and task instruction but not training the model to predict the prompt prefix.
- **Compute**: training runs on `20` [[nvidia-a100]] GPUs (`80 GB`) for `4` epochs on FLAN-5M (`160` hours) and another `4` epochs on FLAN-1M (`40` hours); data collection itself takes roughly `2` weeks for ChatGPT and `3` weeks for GPT-4.
- **Evaluation protocol**: open-ended generation uses GPT-4 rating over Vicuna, Awesome Prompts, and WizardLM prompt sets; reasoning is measured in strict zero-shot form on [[big-bench-hard]] and AGIEval-style exams without exemplars or CoT.

## Key Results

- Open-ended generation: Orca retains `95%` of [[chatgpt]] quality and `85%` of [[gpt-4]] quality under GPT-4-as-judge, improving over [[vicuna-13b]] by `11.3%` relative to ChatGPT-referenced scores and `13.5%` relative to GPT-4-referenced scores.
- AGIEval reasoning: Orca averages `41.7` accuracy versus `29.3` for [[vicuna-13b]], `47.2` for ChatGPT, and `62.0` for GPT-4, yielding a `42.1%` relative improvement over Vicuna while retaining about `88%` of ChatGPT quality.
- Category gains on AGIEval are large on reading-heavy tasks: `57.3` on LSAT-RC and `76.7` on SAT-English, versus Vicuna's `32.7` and `44.2` respectively.
- Big-Bench Hard: Orca reaches `49.7` average versus `48.9` for ChatGPT, `23.3` for [[vicuna-13b]], and `67.4` for GPT-4, corresponding to a `113.7%` relative gain over Vicuna but still a `17.7` point gap to GPT-4.
- The progressive-learning ablation matters: training on `5M` ChatGPT examples plus `1M` GPT-4 examples beats GPT-4-only FLAN-1M training by `4.52` AGIEval points (`41.7` vs `37.18`).
- Safety-related evaluation is directionally positive but incomplete: the paper reports Orca as more truthful than Vicuna on TruthfulQA-MC and less toxic on ToxiGen-style prompting, though the section emphasizes these are limited measurements rather than comprehensive safety validation.

## Limitations

- The paper evaluates primarily zero-shot single-turn settings; multi-turn dialogue, in-context learning, few-shot prompting, and tool-augmented usage are explicitly left untested.
- Orca inherits both architectural limits of [[llama]] and teacher-specific biases from [[gpt-4]] / [[chatgpt]], so explanation tuning can transfer weaknesses as well as strengths.
- Performance remains weak on several hard reasoning regimes, especially math, geometry, long-context comprehension, table understanding, and some world-knowledge tasks.
- The safety analysis is narrow: TruthfulQA-MC and ToxiGen do not cover the full risk surface, and the authors caution against downstream deployment without further analysis.
- The paper itself raises contamination and evaluation-bias concerns, including GPT-4 judge bias and possible benchmark contamination in Big-Bench reports, so some claims depend on imperfect evaluation tooling.
