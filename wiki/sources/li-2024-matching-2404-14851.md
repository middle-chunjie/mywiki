## Summary

This survey frames [[generative-information-retrieval]] as the shift from similarity-based ranking to generation-based access, and organizes the field into [[generative-retrieval]] and [[reliable-response-generation]]. On the retrieval side, it formalizes how generative models map queries to [[document-identifier]] sequences with constrained decoding, then reviews model training, DocID design, dynamic-corpus adaptation, multimodal retrieval, and recommendation. On the response side, it synthesizes work on internal knowledge memorization, [[retrieval-augmented-generation]], [[tool-augmentation]], citation, and personalization. The paper is especially useful because it does not stop at taxonomy: it also compares benchmark results, showing that term-set and multi-view DocIDs are currently stronger than early semantic-ID approaches, and closes with concrete open problems around scalability, latency, dynamic corpora, factuality, and unified retrieval-generation systems.

## Problem & Motivation

Traditional IR is dominated by lexical and dense matching systems that return ranked documents rather than directly satisfying the user’s information need. The authors argue that recent language models make a broader paradigm possible: either retrieve by generating valid document identifiers, or answer directly with grounded and accountable responses. Because research on these two forms had become fragmented across GR, RAG, citation, tool use, and personalization, the survey’s motivation is to provide a unified map of methods, evaluation protocols, bottlenecks, and future directions for next-generation IR systems.

## Method

- The survey first formalizes classical retrieval baselines: sparse matching uses `R(q,d) = Σ_{t in q ∩ d} tf-idf(t,d) · tf-idf(t,q)`, while dense retrieval encodes `v_d = E_d(d)` and `v_q = E_q(q)` and scores with cosine similarity `R(q,d) = cos(v_q, v_d)`.
- It then formalizes [[generative-retrieval]] by defining a bijection `phi(d_i) = d'_i` from documents to DocIDs, a trie-constrained next-token set `V(d'_<i)`, and query-document relevance as generation probability `R(q,d) = P(d'|q; theta) = Π_i P(d'_i | d'_<i, q; theta)`.
- Training of GR is summarized as sequence modeling with cross-entropy loss `L = -Σ_i log P(d'_i | d'_<i, q; theta)`, then expanded into survey dimensions covering model training, architecture, DocID design, incremental learning, downstream adaptation, multimodal retrieval, and generative recommendation.
- The GR section highlights architectural families such as encoder-decoder [[sequence-to-sequence]] models, decoder-centric formulations linked to [[autoregressive-language-model]] behavior, and hybrid designs that combine generative models with dense retrieval through [[residual-quantization]] or clustering-based candidate narrowing.
- For reliable response generation, the paper structures the literature into four routes: internal knowledge memorization, external knowledge augmentation, generation with citation, and personalized information assistance. Within augmentation, it distinguishes retrieval-augmented flows and [[tool-augmentation]] workflows.
- The retrieval-augmentation subsection further breaks [[retrieval-augmented-generation]] into sequential, branching, conditional, and loop-style pipelines, while GR inference is tied to [[beam-search]] and [[constrained-decoding]] over valid DocID prefixes.
- Evaluation is organized across GR metrics (`Recall`, `R-Precision`, `MRR`, `MAP`, `nDCG`) and response-generation metrics, including rule-based scores and perplexity `PPL(W) = exp{-1/N Σ_i log p(w_i | w_<i)}`, plus model-based and human evaluation.

## Key Results

- On MS MARCO 300K, term-set and multi-view GR methods are strongest: TSGen reaches `Recall@1 = 0.384` and `MRR@10 = 0.502`, while DGR reaches `Recall@10 = 0.779` and `MRR@100 = 0.504`, outperforming DPR and often matching or exceeding SPLADEv2 on key metrics.
- On Natural Questions, TSGen attains `Recall@1 = 0.708`, `Recall@10 = 0.889`, and `MRR@10 = 0.771`; NOVO reaches `Recall@10 = 0.897` and `MRR@100 = 0.767`, supporting the paper’s claim that better DocID design matters more than naive scaling.
- On KILT, title and sub-string identifiers scale well on Wikipedia-like corpora: GENRE achieves `FEVER = 0.847`, `AY2 = 0.928`, `WnWi = 0.877`, and `WnCw = 0.706`, while SEAL reaches `NQ = 0.658`, `HoPo = 0.565`, `TQA = 0.715`, and `WoW = 0.527`.
- Early semantic-ID GR can collapse at large scale: the BART semantic-ID baseline on KILT records `FEVER = 0.003` and `AY2 = 0.001`, which the authors use as evidence that numeric DocIDs often memorize seen pairs rather than generalize across the corpus.
- In the cited large-scale GR analysis on the full 8.8M-passage MS MARCO setting, the best reported configuration uses T5-XL (`3B`) with synthetic-query training to reach `MRR@10 = 26.7`, while T5-XXL (`11B`) does not improve further, reinforcing the survey’s argument that scaling alone is insufficient.
- For reliable answer generation, the survey aggregates benchmark evidence that current systems still struggle with truthfulness and safety: TruthfulQA has `817` questions over `38` categories, with best model truthfulness only around `58%` versus `94%` for humans, and SafetyBench contains `11,435` questions over `7` safety categories.

## Limitations

- The paper is broad by design, so many comparisons inherit heterogeneous datasets, corpus sizes, and training settings from prior work; some “best” methods are therefore not strictly apples-to-apples.
- The reliable-response-generation half is taxonomy-heavy and does not provide the same level of aligned experimental comparison that the GR section provides through Tables 3 and 4.
- Several future directions, such as unified large search models and end-to-end retrieval-generation systems, are argued conceptually rather than validated with a single working system.
- Metadata is mildly inconsistent across artifacts: the supplied bib points to arXiv `2404.14851` from `2024`, while the manuscript header uses TOIS-style formatting with `journalyear: 2025` and a placeholder DOI, so the staging page follows the arXiv/bib record.
