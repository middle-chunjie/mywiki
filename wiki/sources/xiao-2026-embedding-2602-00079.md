## Summary

The paper proposes a training-free compression pipeline for unit-normalized embeddings that converts Cartesian vectors into `d - 1` spherical angles before transposition, byte shuffling, and zstd compression. The key observation is geometric: for high-dimensional unit-norm vectors, most spherical angles concentrate near `π/2`, so their IEEE 754 exponents collapse to a dominant value and the high-order mantissa bytes become predictable as well. This reduces entropy beyond what prior lossless floating-point compressors can achieve on raw Cartesian coordinates. Across 26 text, image, and multi-vector settings, the method consistently reaches about `1.5×` compression with reconstruction error below `1e-7`, making it effectively float32-faithful while materially reducing storage for embedding archives, network transport, and retrieval indexes.

## Problem & Motivation

Embedding-heavy retrieval systems store large float32 arrays whose footprint quickly becomes operationally expensive: a 1024-dimensional embedding costs roughly 4 KB, and multi-vector retrievers such as [[colbert]] multiply that by token count. Existing lossless baselines for floating-point data mainly exploit exponent redundancy after transpose and shuffle, but they cannot reduce the near-maximal entropy of mantissa bytes, so compression stalls around `1.2×`. This paper argues that for unit-norm embeddings the representation itself should be changed: the hyperspherical geometry of normalized vectors induces a much lower-entropy angular representation than raw Cartesian coordinates, enabling better compression without learned codebooks or aggressive quantization.

## Method

- **Representation change**: for a unit-norm vector `x ∈ R^d`, store `d - 1` angles instead of `d` Cartesian coordinates. The transform uses `θ_i = arccos(x_i / ||x_{i:d}||_2)` for `i = 1, ..., d - 2` and `θ_{d-1} = atan2(x_d, x_{d-1})`.
- **Compression pipeline**: compute spherical angles `Θ ∈ R^{n × (d - 1)}`, transpose to group the same angular position across vectors, apply byte shuffling to separate exponent and mantissa bytes, then compress with `zstd`. Decompression reverses the byte unshuffle and transpose; Cartesian reconstruction uses cumulative sine products.
- **Why it works**: high-dimensional unit vectors lie on `S^{d-1}`, so most early angles follow a distribution concentrated near `π/2 ≈ 1.57`. Empirically this collapses exponents to value `127` with probability above `0.999`, reducing exponent entropy from about `2.6` to `0.03` bits/byte on `jina-embeddings-v4`.
- **Mantissa savings**: because the angles cluster near `π/2`, the high-order mantissa byte also becomes more predictable. The paper reports a drop from `8.0` to `4.5` bits for the high-order mantissa byte, which contributes roughly another `11%` savings beyond exponent concentration.
- **Numerics and complexity**: intermediate transforms use float64 and final storage uses float32, keeping reconstruction error below `1e-7`, under float32 machine epsilon `1.19e-7`. A backward cumulative norm recurrence reduces transform cost from naive `O(nd^2)` to `O(nd)`.
- **Direct similarity**: cosine similarity can be computed directly from spherical angles via the recurrence `R = cos(θ_{d-1} - φ_{d-1})`, then `R ← cos θ_k cos φ_k + sin θ_k sin φ_k · R` for `k = d - 2, ..., 1`, avoiding full Cartesian materialization.
- **Operational settings**: the implementation is training-free, uses no codebook, and recommends `zstd` level `1` in practice because the ratio stays around `1.52×` while throughput reaches hundreds of MB/s.

## Key Results

- On `jina-embeddings-v4` (`7600` vectors, `2048d`), raw float32 shrinks from `59.38 MB` to `37.59 MB`, improving the best baseline (`49.57 MB`) from `1.20×` to `1.58×`.
- Reconstruction remains essentially float32-faithful: max element-wise error `9e-8`, mean error `2e-8`, and max cosine error `2e-7`.
- Across `26` configurations spanning text, image, and multi-vector embeddings, compression ranges from `1.47×` to `1.59×`, which is a `20%` to `32%` improvement over the transpose+shuffle+zstd baseline.
- For multi-vector retrieval, `jina-colbert-v2` drops from `243.22 MB` raw to `160.48 MB` compressed (`1.52×`, `+26.5%` over baseline), motivating the paper's estimate that a `1M`-document ColBERT index can shrink from roughly `240 GB` to `160 GB`.
- The optimized C implementation reports over `1 GB/s` for the spherical transform alone and, at `zstd` level `1`, total pipeline throughput of `487 MB/s` encoding and `605 MB/s` decoding.
- Dimension and chunking ablations show gains rise with dimensionality and plateau near `1.52×`; chunk sizes `100` to `1000` keep random-access overhead within about `1.8%` to `2.5%`.

## Limitations

- The method is only advantageous for float32 embeddings; on BF16, FP16, FP8, and Int8 the spherical transform expands storage because angle outputs are stored as float32.
- It relies on the unit-norm assumption; the compression mechanism comes from hyperspherical angle concentration and would not directly transfer to arbitrary unnormalized vectors.
- It is not strictly lossless in the bit-exact sense, since `arccos`/`atan2` plus float32 storage introduce bounded `ε`-scale reconstruction error.
- Small chunks or per-vector random access reduce compression notably (`1.35×` at chunk size `1`), so high ratios depend on enough cross-vector context for entropy coding.
- Evaluation focuses on compression statistics and numerical fidelity rather than large end-to-end retrieval benchmarks under production serving constraints.
