## Summary

The paper introduces Agent Data Protocol (ADP), a standardized schema for representing heterogeneous agent-training trajectories and converting them into framework-specific supervised fine-tuning data. ADP models interaction traces as alternating actions and observations, covering API calls, code execution, natural-language messages, text feedback, and web-state observations. Using this protocol, the authors unify 13 public datasets into a 1.3M-trajectory corpus and build converters to OpenHands, SWE-Agent, and AgentLab. Across coding, software engineering, browsing, and tool-use benchmarks, ADP-trained models improve by roughly 20% on average over their untuned baselines, while mixed-domain training also outperforms task-specific-only tuning. The paper’s main contribution is less a new model than a reusable data interface that reduces dataset–agent integration from pairwise engineering to a hub-and-spoke pipeline.

## Problem & Motivation

Public agent fine-tuning data already exists, but it is fragmented across incompatible trajectory formats, action spaces, observation types, and dataset-specific tooling. This fragmentation makes it expensive to combine datasets, hard to compare them systematically, and awkward to adapt them to new agent harnesses. The paper argues that the main bottleneck for academic agent SFT is therefore data standardization rather than a lack of raw demonstrations. ADP is proposed as a common interlingua that preserves task diversity while removing per-dataset, per-agent conversion overhead.

## Method

- **Unified schema**: each trajectory is a `Trajectory(id, content, details)` object, where `content` is an alternating sequence of actions and observations and `details` stores dataset-specific metadata.
- **Action space**: ADP defines three action types: `APIAction(function, kwargs, description)`, `CodeAction(language, content, description)`, and `MessageAction(content)`.
- **Observation space**: ADP defines `TextObservation(source, content)` and `WebObservation(html, axtree, url, viewport_size, image_observation)` to cover both textual and browser-state feedback.
- **Conversion pipeline**: the three-stage workflow is `Raw -> ADP`, `ADP -> SFT`, and automated quality assurance; this separates dataset normalization from harness-specific formatting.
- **Validation**: converters enforce structural checks such as tool-call formatting, proper conversation termination, and a default threshold that at least `80%` of tool calls are paired with an English thought.
- **Scale and training setup**: the released ADP Dataset V1 contains `1.3M` trajectories from `13` datasets spanning coding, software engineering, tool use, and web browsing; experiments fine-tune Qwen2.5 `7B/14B/32B` and Qwen3 `8B` models for `3` agent harnesses.
- **Balanced sampling**: per-dataset sampling uses `m_d = ceil(w_d n_d)` with upsampling or downsampling via `w_d` to prevent very large corpora such as Orca AgentInstruct from dominating training.
- **Efficiency argument**: ADP replaces pairwise conversion cost `Cost_no-ADP(A, D) ≈ A * Σ_i LOC_i(raw->ADP)` with `Cost_ADP(A, D) ≈ Σ_i LOC_i(raw->ADP) + Σ_j LOC_j(ADP->SFT)`, changing the community burden from `O(D x A)` to `O(D + A)`.

## Key Results

- **SWE-Bench Verified, 7B**: SWE-Agent improves from `0.4%` to `20.2%` and OpenHands improves from `2.8%` to `20.4%` when trained on ADP data.
- **SWE-Bench Verified, larger models**: with SWE-Agent, Qwen2.5-14B reaches `34.4%` and Qwen2.5-32B reaches `40.3%`; with OpenHands, the same scales reach `30.6%` and `36.8%`.
- **WebArena**: AgentLab with ADP data reaches `21.0%` at `7B`, `22.2%` at `14B`, and `22.9%` at `32B`, versus `4.5%`, `5.5%`, and `10.9%` untuned.
- **AgentBench OS**: OpenHands improves from `3.5%` to `27.1%` at `7B`, from `2.8%` to `20.8%` at `14B`, and from `27.8%` to `34.7%` at `32B`.
- **GAIA and cross-task transfer**: GAIA rises from `7.3%` to `9.1%` at `7B`; mixed ADP training also beats task-specific-only tuning, e.g. SWE-Bench `10.4%` vs `1.0%` for Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and WebArena `20.1%` vs `16.0%`.
- **Engineering efficiency**: dataset-to-ADP conversion across `13` datasets totals `4892` LOC, while ADP-to-SFT converters average about `77` LOC; for `A = 100` harnesses, the projected cost drops from `489,200` LOC without ADP to `12,592` LOC with ADP.

## Limitations

- ADP is still primarily text- and browser-state-centric; the paper lists multimodal extensions such as images and screen recordings as future work rather than current coverage.
- Empirical evaluation covers only `3` harnesses and `4` benchmarks, so the protocol’s generality is demonstrated broadly but not exhaustively.
- Data quality still depends on the correctness of dataset-specific converters and automated validation; the paper does not report large-scale manual auditing of the unified corpus.
- Reported “generalist” gains remain partly harness-specific because training mixes are filtered by domain, such as excluding web datasets for OpenHands and SWE-Agent.
