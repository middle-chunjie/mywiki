## Summary

This paper proposes In-Context Pretraining, a corpus-side modification to autoregressive LM pretraining that replaces random document concatenation with sequences of semantically related documents. The key idea is to expose the model to cross-document dependencies during pretraining without changing the LM architecture or loss. The pipeline first retrieves nearest-neighbor documents with Contriever plus FAISS approximate search, then builds a non-repeating document order via a greedy graph-traversal approximation to a maximum traveling-salesman-style objective. On LLaMA-style models from `0.3B` to `7B` trained over `306B` CommonCrawl tokens, the method improves in-context learning, reading comprehension, faithfulness under knowledge conflict, long-context reasoning, and open-book QA, while also lowering perplexity in ablations that validate both document linking and semantic deduplication.

## Problem & Motivation

Standard LM pretraining packs randomly shuffled documents into long contexts, so tokens in earlier documents usually provide no predictive signal for later ones. That wastes context-window capacity and fails to train models to reason over multi-document evidence. The paper targets this mismatch by constructing input contexts whose constituent documents are semantically related, so the model repeatedly encounters useful cross-document dependencies during pretraining. The design goal is intentionally minimal: improve contextual reasoning without altering the base LM objective, architecture, or overall training recipe.

## Method

- **Core pretraining change**: replace random document concatenation with ordered sequences of related documents, while keeping the standard autoregressive language-model objective unchanged.
- **Document retrieval**: encode each document `d_i` with Contriever mean pooling to produce `E(d_i)`, then score relatedness with cosine similarity `s(d_i, d_j) = cos(E(d_i), E(d_j))`.
- **ANN infrastructure**: retrieve top-`k` neighbors with FAISS approximate search using product quantization and an inverted-file index; Appendix A.2 reports `768`-dim embeddings, IVFPQ code size `256`, `32768` inverted lists, index size `62 GB`, and `nprobe = 64`.
- **Semantic deduplication**: filter near-duplicate documents using retrieval scores before ordering; the paper treats this as necessary to avoid unstable training caused by copying from highly similar prior documents.
- **Graph construction**: build an undirected document graph `G = (D, L)` where edges connect nearest-neighbor pairs and edge weights are document similarities.
- **Graph traversal / ordering**: approximate a maximum-weight Hamiltonian-style path by starting from a minimum-degree unvisited node, greedily following a high-similarity unvisited neighbor, and jumping to another minimum-degree node when the local neighborhood is exhausted.
- **Context packing**: traverse the final path and segment it into fixed-length contexts for LM pretraining so each document appears only once instead of being repeatedly reused as in `k`NN packing.
- **Base LM setup**: use LLaMA-style decoder-only models at `0.3B`, `0.7B`, `1.5B`, and `7B` parameters with context length `8192`.
- **Training hyperparameters**: pretrain on `235M` CommonCrawl documents / `306B` tokens with AdamW, `β1 = 0.9`, `β2 = 0.95`, cosine learning-rate schedule, and FlashAttention for memory efficiency.
- **Systems cost**: the `7B` model trains on `128` A100 GPUs across `16` nodes with batch size `4M` tokens for `9` days; retrieval search uses batches of `50M` embeddings, `8` GPUs per batch, `32` GPUs total for about `6` hours, and graph traversal takes `12` hours on `20` CPUs.

## Key Results

- **In-context learning**: average accuracy rises from `66.0` (Standard) and `61.8` (`k`NN) to `71.3` (`ICLM`); examples include SST-2 `93.2` vs `83.7` and AGNews `76.0` vs `68.3`.
- **Reading comprehension**: average score improves to `43.2` from `37.6` (Standard) and `36.0` (`k`NN); HotpotQA improves from `10.5` to `21.9`, and SQuAD from `26.3` to `30.3`.
- **Open-book QA / retrieval augmentation**: on Natural Questions, open-book EM improves from `28.5` to `32.2`; on TriviaQA, open-book EM improves from `48.1` to `51.6`. Closed-book performance is flat or slightly worse (`17.0` vs `17.0` on NQ; `48.0` vs `49.3` on TQA).
- **Faithfulness under knowledge conflict**: NQ-Swap improves from `39.6` to `45.8`, and MemoTrap from `48.4` to `56.2`, supporting the paper's claim of better context faithfulness.
- **Long-context reasoning**: SCROLL average improves from `32.5` to `34.1`, including Qasper `36.7` vs `34.2` and GovReport `9.1` vs `8.2`.
- **Ablation on data construction**: perplexity improves from `8.2` (random ordering) to `7.9` (clustering) to `7.3` (final linked ordering), and degrades to `8.3` when semantic deduplication is removed.

## Limitations

- The method adds a substantial preprocessing stage: large-scale retrieval, ANN indexing, deduplication, and graph traversal are nontrivial system costs before LM training begins.
- Quality depends on the retrieval stack; if nearest neighbors are noisy or duplicates are not filtered well, the constructed contexts may be less coherent or may destabilize training.
- The paper evaluates only English web-scale pretraining and LLaMA-style models up to `7B`, so generalization to multilingual corpora, different model families, or much larger scales is not established.
- Gains on long-context reasoning after downstream fine-tuning are positive but modest relative to some zero-shot or prompting gains, suggesting pretraining alone does not fully solve long-context reasoning.
- Open-book performance improves, but closed-book QA is sometimes unchanged or slightly worse, consistent with the authors' note that the method may trade some memorization for better contextual use.
