## Summary

The paper introduces ToRA, a tool-integrated reasoning agent for mathematical problem solving that interleaves natural-language rationales with executable programs and tool outputs. Instead of relying only on chain-of-thought or only on program synthesis, ToRA alternates between rationale generation, tool invocation, execution feedback, and answer finalization. The training pipeline first builds TORA-CORPUS, a 16k-trajectory supervision set synthesized with GPT-4 on GSM8k and MATH, then expands the model's output space through additional sampled and teacher-corrected trajectories. Across 10 math benchmarks, ToRA substantially improves open-source models at every scale; ToRA-CODE-34B reaches 50.8% on MATH, while ToRA-70B achieves the best average score reported for the family and strong out-of-distribution generalization.

## Problem & Motivation

Open-source large language models remained weak on advanced math because rationale-only methods are good at semantic decomposition but brittle on exact computation, while program-only methods are good at execution but weaker at planning, abstraction, and recovery from intermediate errors. The paper targets this gap by training models to interleave reasoning with tool use, so the model can decide when to analyze in language, when to write code for symbolic or numerical operations, and how to continue after observing execution results. The additional motivation for output space shaping is that plain imitation learning from a single valid trajectory per question overly constrains test-time behavior and reduces robustness.

## Method

- **Tool-integrated trajectory format**: For a question `q`, ToRA alternates rationale `r_i`, program `a_i`, and execution output `o_i`, with trajectory `τ = r_1 a_1 o_1 ... r_n`, where `r_n` contains the final `\boxed{}` answer.
- **Generation equations**: Rationale generation follows `r_i ~ P_G(. | p ⊕ q ⊕ τ_{i-1})`; program generation follows `a_i ~ P_G(. | p ⊕ q ⊕ τ_{i-1} ⊕ r_i)`; trajectory update is `τ_i <- τ_{i-1} ⊕ r_i ⊕ a_i ⊕ o_i`.
- **Corpus construction**: GPT-4 is prompted to synthesize interactive tool-use trajectories on GSM8k and MATH. The inference loop uses maximum interaction rounds `n = 3`, greedy decoding first, then nucleus sampling with sample size `10` when greedy decoding fails, keeping up to `4` valid trajectories per question.
- **TORA-CORPUS**: After filtering wrong answers and tool-use errors, the authors keep about `16k` trajectories, covering `98.2%` of GSM8k training questions and `83.1%` of MATH training questions.
- **Imitation learning**: The model is trained with negative log-likelihood on complete trajectories: `M = arg min_M Σ_{q,τ} Σ_{i=1}^{n-1} -log P_M(r_{i+1} a_{i+1} | q, r_1 ... o_i)`.
- **Output space shaping**: To diversify valid behavior, the imitation-learned model samples `64` trajectories per training question. Valid trajectories are retained; invalid trajectories are truncated at prefixes and completed by a teacher model.
- **Teacher correction**: The teacher is `CodeLLaMA-34B` trained on TORA-CORPUS. This process yields `233k` distinct valid sampled trajectories and `69k` corrected trajectories; the final training mixture keeps up to `4` extra trajectories per GSM8k/MATH problem and trains on `69k` annotations.
- **Model/training setup**: The paper fine-tunes `LLaMA-2` and `CodeLLaMA` models from `7B` to `70B` with learning rate `2e-5` by default and `1e-5` for `34B`/`70B`, global batch size `128`, linear schedule with `3%` warm-up, `3` epochs, `DeepSpeed ZeRO Stage 3`, and `Flash-Attention 2`.
- **Inference setup**: Results use greedy decoding, maximum sequence length `2048`, and at most `3` tool executions.

## Key Results

- Across the 10-dataset evaluation suite, ToRA improves over prior open-source baselines by `13%-19%` absolute on average, depending on model scale.
- On MATH, `ToRA-CODE-7B = 44.6%`, which exceeds `WizardMath-70B = 22.7%` by about `22` absolute points and surpasses `GPT-4` chain-of-thought at `42.5%`.
- `ToRA-CODE-34B` is the first open-source model in the paper to exceed `50%` on MATH, reaching `50.8%`, close to `GPT-4 (PAL) = 51.8%`.
- `ToRA-70B` achieves the strongest family-wide average score in Table 2 with `76.9`, including `84.3` on GSM8k, `49.7` on MATH, `67.2` on GSM-Hard, `82.7` on SVAMP, `74.0` on TabMWP, `86.8` on ASDiv, and `93.8` on MAWPS.
- Output space shaping contributes sizable gains: the paper reports average absolute improvements of `+3.4%` on GSM8k and `+4.0%` on MATH, with gains up to `+4.5%` when correction is added on top of sampling.
- On MATH subtopics, tool-integrated reasoning gives especially large gains in Algebra and Number Theory; for example, `ToRA-CODE-13B` reaches `66.7` on Algebra and `55.6` on Number Theory, far above same-scale WizardMath.

## Limitations

- The dominant failure mode is still incorrect reasoning rather than missing computation: human analysis attributes `38%` of sampled failures to reasoning errors.
- Diagram-heavy problems remain difficult; `21%` of sampled failures come from diagram understanding, especially because MATH diagrams are represented textually in Asymptote.
- Tool use is still brittle: `10%` of failures come from inappropriate tool use, `9%` from syntax errors, and `9%` from runtime errors.
- Some problems cannot be cleanly formalized into executable programs and instead require abstract reasoning; the paper labels `3%` of sampled failures as rationale-only errors.
- Training coverage drops on harder problems; for MATH level-5 questions, coverage in TORA-CORPUS is only `68.0%`, which likely limits performance on the longest and hardest examples.
