## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2021; re-verify against recent literature.

The paper studies how to make non-parametric language models practical by reducing the inference cost of `kNN-LM`, whose test-time nearest-neighbor retrieval over massive datastores causes `10x-30x` slowdown versus a purely parametric LM. Using WikiText-103 and Law-MT, the authors decompose efficiency into three levers: adaptive retrieval that skips unnecessary queries, datastore pruning that removes redundant entries, and vector compression via PCA. The analysis shows that a lightweight retrieval adaptor can prune `50%` of searches on WikiText-103 with almost no perplexity loss, greedy merging can compress the datastore to `60%` of its size, and `512`-dimensional PCA can even improve perplexity while accelerating inference. Combining the three yields `6.6x` speedup on WikiText-103 and `5.4x` on Law-MT while retaining comparable quality.

## Problem & Motivation

`kNN-LM` improves token prediction by interpolating a pretrained neural language model with a nearest-neighbor distribution from an external datastore, which is especially useful for rare patterns and domain adaptation. The systems problem is that the datastore is nearly as large as the training corpus and stores high-dimensional dense vectors for each token, so inference must repeatedly run approximate nearest-neighbor search over tens or hundreds of millions of entries. On WikiText-103, the resulting `103M`-entry datastore occupies roughly `200 GB`; on Law-MT, the datastore still occupies `55 GB`. This makes a standard `kNN-LM` much slower than a parametric baseline and creates a deployment bottleneck even when the perplexity gains are substantial.

## Method

- **Baseline formulation**: the model interpolates the parametric LM and datastore-based retrieval score as ``p(w_t|c_t) = \lambda p_{kNN}(w_t|c_t) + (1-\lambda)p_{NLM}(w_t|c_t)``. The datastore stores key-value pairs ``(f(c_t), w_t)`` for all training tokens, and the retrieved distribution uses distance-weighted aggregation over the `k = 1024` nearest neighbors.
- **Approximate search setup**: retrieval uses FAISS with approximate nearest-neighbor indexing over quantized vectors. Because exact distance recomputation from full-precision vectors on disk is too slow, the paper directly uses approximate distances in ``p_{kNN}``, trading a small accuracy drop for usable speed.
- **Adaptive retrieval**: a lightweight MLP predicts context-dependent interpolation weights ``\lambda(c)`` by maximizing ``\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_t \log p(w_t|c_t;\lambda_\theta(c_t)) - a \cdot \lambda_\theta(c_t)`` with an `L1` sparsity term. Input features include the context vector ``f(c)``, LM confidence/entropy, and local count statistics such as `n`-gram frequency and fertility; at inference, low-weight retrievals are pruned by setting ``\lambda(c)=0``.
- **Datastore pruning**: the paper evaluates random pruning, target-aware `k`-means clustering, rank-based pruning, and greedy merging. For compressed entries with multiplicity ``s_i``, the neighbor distribution is corrected as ``p_{kNN}(w=y|c) \propto \sum_{(q_i,v_i)\in\mathcal{N}} \mathbb{I}_{v_i=y} s_i \exp(-d(q_i,f(c)))``.
- **Greedy merging details**: for each datastore record, the algorithm retrieves its local nearest neighbors and merges only nearby entries with the same target token, updating weights ``s_i`` without propagating merges recursively. This keeps compression local and reduces the approximation error that large global clusters can introduce.
- **Dimension reduction**: principal component analysis compresses datastore vectors from `1024` or `1536` dimensions to smaller spaces before indexing. The paper varies the reduced dimensionality and finds that moderate compression, especially `d = 256` or `512`, improves both speed and sometimes perplexity.
- **Experimental setup**: WikiText-103 uses a transformer LM trained on the same corpus with `1024`-dimensional keys and interpolation weight ``\lambda = 0.25``; Law-MT uses a `12`-layer transformer trained on WMT News Crawl with `1536`-dimensional keys and ``\lambda = 0.9``. Benchmarking uses `32` CPU cores for retrieval plus `1` NVIDIA RTX `3090` GPU for neural computation.

## Key Results

- Baseline speed gap: on WikiText-103, the parametric NLM gets `18.66` perplexity at `3847` tokens/s, while `kNN-LM` improves to `16.65` perplexity but drops to `277` tokens/s; exact-distance `kNN-LM` runs at `<1` token/s.
- Domain adaptation gain: on Law-MT, the NLM baseline is `106.25` perplexity at `28K` tokens/s, while `kNN-LM` drops perplexity to `12.32` at `1052` tokens/s, showing the value of datastore retrieval despite the latency cost.
- Adaptive retrieval: pruning `50%` of retrievals on WikiText-103 yields `16.67` perplexity at `530` tokens/s, nearly `2x` faster than vanilla `kNN-LM` (`277` tokens/s) with negligible quality loss.
- Datastore pruning: greedy merging with retention rate `n = 0.6` reaches `16.86` perplexity and `446` tokens/s on WikiText-103, outperforming `k`-means and rank-based pruning; random pruning is unexpectedly competitive, while `k`-means can underperform the NLM baseline at aggressive compression.
- Dimension reduction: PCA to `512` dimensions gives `16.40` perplexity and `991` tokens/s on WikiText-103, about `3.6x` faster than vanilla `kNN-LM` while slightly improving perplexity.
- Combined system: on WikiText-103, combining adaptive retrieval, greedy merging, and PCA achieves `16.67` perplexity at `1835` tokens/s (`6.6x` speedup over `kNN-LM`); on Law-MT, the combined method reaches `12.29` perplexity at `5708` tokens/s (`5.4x` speedup).

## Limitations

- The paper is empirical rather than theoretical: it provides heuristics and ablations, but does not give a formal account of when pruning or PCA should preserve nearest-neighbor quality.
- Adaptive retrieval is much less effective in the true domain-adaptation regime, where the datastore carries novel domain knowledge and only `10%` of retrievals can be removed at a `0.1` perplexity cost.
- Several pruning methods are computationally heavy to build or weak in accuracy: token-aware `k`-means is expensive and can distort distances, while rank-based pruning does not beat simpler baselines.
- The evaluation is limited to two datasets and decoder-only transformer LMs; the conclusions may not transfer directly to larger contemporary LLMs, alternative retrievers, or different memory architectures.
- The approximation stack is cumulative: FAISS quantization, approximate distances, pruning, and PCA can each alter the retrieval distribution, so the best settings remain validation-dependent rather than universally robust.
