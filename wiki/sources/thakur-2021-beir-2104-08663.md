## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2021; re-verify against recent literature.

BEIR introduces a unified zero-shot retrieval benchmark for measuring how well information retrieval systems generalize beyond their training distribution. The benchmark covers `18` English datasets across `9` task families, spanning corpora from `3.6K` to about `15M` documents and broad variation in query and document lengths. The paper evaluates `10` retrieval systems from lexical, sparse, dense, late-interaction, and re-ranking families under a common interface and metric. Its core empirical message is that strong in-domain performance on MS MARCO does not reliably predict cross-domain robustness: BM25 remains a hard baseline, docT5query and ColBERT generalize better than many dense bi-encoders, and cross-encoder re-ranking is strongest overall but computationally expensive.

## Problem & Motivation

Neural retrieval models were often evaluated in narrow, homogeneous settings, usually on a single training and test distribution such as MS MARCO or QA-heavy benchmarks. That makes it hard to separate genuine semantic generalization from benchmark-specific fitting. The paper targets this gap by constructing a broad zero-shot benchmark over diverse domains and retrieval tasks, then asking which architectural choices remain robust under domain and task shift. A second motivation is tooling: existing datasets were scattered across formats and evaluation protocols, making controlled comparison unnecessarily difficult.

## Method

- **Benchmark scope**: construct BEIR with `18` English evaluation datasets over `9` retrieval tasks, including bio-medical IR, QA, tweet retrieval, news retrieval, argument retrieval, duplicate-question retrieval, entity retrieval, citation prediction, and fact checking.
- **Selection criteria**: choose datasets to maximize task diversity, domain diversity, task difficulty, and annotation diversity, rather than optimizing for any single application setting.
- **Unified data interface**: standardize every dataset into `(corpus, queries, qrels)` so the same evaluation code can run across corpora with sizes from `3.6K` to `~15M` documents.
- **Domain-diversity analysis**: quantify pairwise dataset overlap with weighted Jaccard similarity, `J(S,T)=sum_k min(S_k,T_k) / sum_k max(S_k,T_k)`, where `S_k` and `T_k` are normalized unigram frequencies.
- **Primary metric**: use `nDCG@10` as the common retrieval metric because it is rank-aware and supports both binary and graded relevance labels; also report `Recall@100` and define capped recall `R_cap@k` for datasets with more than `k` relevant documents.
- **Document truncation**: for all neural retrievers, keep only the first `512` word pieces of each document to match Transformer input limits.
- **Lexical baseline**: run BM25 with Anserini using Lucene defaults `k = 0.9` and `b = 0.4`, indexing title and passage as separate fields when titles exist.
- **Sparse / dense / interaction baselines**: evaluate DeepCT, SPARTA, docT5query, DPR, ANCE, TAS-B, GenQ, ColBERT, and `BM25+CE`; notable settings include SPARTA with `30K`-dim sparse vectors and `2,000` non-zero entries, docT5query generating `40` synthetic queries per document, ANCE trained for `600K` steps, GenQ generating `5` queries per document with `top-k = 25` and `top-p = 0.95`, and ColBERT trained for `300K` steps with `max_len = 300` and FAISS depth `100`.
- **Re-ranking setup**: use a `6`-layer MiniLM cross-encoder to rerank the top `100` BM25 hits, providing the strongest but most expensive comparison point.
- **Bias analysis**: on TREC-COVID, measure annotation incompleteness with Hole@10 and manually annotate `980` missing query-document pairs to estimate how pooling bias affects lexical vs. non-lexical systems.

## Key Results

- BEIR covers `18` datasets and `9` retrieval tasks; query lengths range from about `3` to `192` words and document lengths from about `11` to `635`, making it substantially more heterogeneous than prior zero-shot retrieval benchmarks.
- BM25 is much weaker than neural methods in-domain on MS MARCO, but it remains a robust zero-shot baseline and beats many more complex systems on average under distribution shift.
- Relative to BM25 average zero-shot performance, DeepCT is `-27.9%`, SPARTA `-20.3%`, DPR `-47.7%`, ANCE `-7.4%`, TAS-B `-2.8%`, GenQ `-3.6%`, while docT5query is `+1.6%`, ColBERT `+2.5%`, and `BM25+CE` `+11%`.
- `BM25+CE` outperforms BM25 on `16/18` datasets and ColBERT on `9/18`; docT5query beats BM25 on `11/18`, showing that document expansion and interaction-heavy scoring transfer better than many dense bi-encoders.
- TAS-B is the strongest dense retriever overall, outperforming ANCE on `14/18` datasets and DPR on `17/18`, but still underperforming BM25 on difficult shifts such as TREC-COVID and Touché-2020.
- On TREC-COVID, Hole@10 is only `6.4%` for BM25 and `2.8%` for docT5query, but `14.4%` for ANCE and `31.8%` for TAS-B, indicating that the annotation pool is biased toward lexical systems.
- After manually adding `980` missing TREC-COVID judgements, ANCE improves from `0.654` to `0.735` nDCG@10 and ColBERT from `0.677` to `0.735`, while BM25 moves only from `0.656` to `0.668`, supporting the paper's claim that benchmark annotations can structurally disadvantage non-lexical retrieval.

## Limitations

- The benchmark is English-only, so it does not test multilingual or cross-lingual retrieval robustness.
- Most neural comparisons assume `512` word-piece truncation, which weakens conclusions for long-document retrieval.
- The setup focuses on text-only retrieval and omits common practical signals such as recency, authority, click feedback, or other multi-factor ranking features.
- BEIR emphasizes broad zero-shot generalization, so specialized task-specific systems may look worse than they would in their native domains.
- The paper shows that relevance pooling can introduce lexical bias, meaning leaderboard scores on some datasets may partially reflect annotation artifacts rather than pure model quality.
