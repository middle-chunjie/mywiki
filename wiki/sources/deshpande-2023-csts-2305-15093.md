## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature.

This paper introduces [[conditional-semantic-textual-similarity]], a reformulation of [[semantic-textual-similarity]] where two sentences are scored relative to an explicit natural-language condition instead of an implicit, underspecified notion of meaning. The authors build the C-STS-2023 benchmark with 18,908 verified quadruplets `{s_1, s_2, c, y}` derived from image-caption corpora and multi-stage crowd annotation, then evaluate both fine-tuned sentence encoders and prompted LLMs. Strong legacy STS models transfer poorly: the best fine-tuned encoder, SimCSE-Large in a [[bi-encoder]] setup, reaches only Spearman `47.5`, while GPT-4 peaks at `43.6` under few-shot prompting. The core claim is that explicit conditions reduce annotation ambiguity and expose finer-grained failures in sentence understanding than standard STS benchmarks.

## Problem & Motivation

Standard [[semantic-textual-similarity]] collapses many possible comparison aspects into a single scalar judgement, forcing annotators to implicitly choose latent weights over different semantic dimensions. The paper formalizes this ambiguity as an unconditional score `sum_i w_i sim_{c_i}(s_1, s_2)` over hidden conditions `c_i`, where the weights `w_i` differ across annotators. C-STS makes the comparison aspect explicit by supplying a natural-language condition `c`, so the benchmark tests whether models can judge similarity with respect to a specific property such as color, motion, or number of objects. This makes the task both less subjective and more diagnostic for [[sentence-embedding]] models and LLMs used in evaluation-heavy NLP settings.

## Method

- **Task definition**: each example is a quadruplet `{s_1, s_2, c, y}` with two sentences, a free-form natural-language condition `c`, and a Likert similarity label `y in {1,2,3,4,5}`.
- **Ambiguity framing**: unconditional STS is modeled as `sum_i w_i sim_{c_i}(s_1, s_2)` with latent aspect weights `w_i`; C-STS instead evaluates a single conditioned similarity `sim_c(s_1, s_2)`.
- **Sentence-pair mining**: candidate pairs are sourced from MS COCO 2014 train (`~83k` images) and Flickr30K (`~31k` images). Images are encoded with CLIP-ViT, top-`k` neighbors are retrieved (`k = 64` for COCO, `k = 128` for Flickr30K), then captions are paired through Cartesian products across matched images.
- **Filtering for ambiguous-but-relevant pairs**: pairs are filtered by bag-of-words IOU, SimCSE cosine distance `d_text`, sentence-length ratio, and minimum length. Thresholds are COCO: `IOU <= 0.12`, `d_text >= 0.4`, `ratio >= 0.7`, `length >= 50`; Flickr30K: `IOU <= 0.2`, `d_text >= 0.4`, `ratio >= 0.7`, `length >= 48`.
- **Crowdsourcing pipeline**: annotation proceeds in three stages: worker qualification, condition writing, and condition verification plus scoring. The qualification step selects `271` Mechanical Turk workers. Annotators write one high-similarity and one low-similarity condition per sentence pair, then separate workers verify relevance and assign final Likert labels.
- **Encoder baselines**: the paper studies [[cross-encoder]], [[bi-encoder]], and [[tri-encoder]] formulations. Cross-encoders jointly encode `(s_1, s_2, c)` and regress a scalar. Bi-encoders score `-cos(f_theta(s_1; c), f_theta(s_2; c))` with Siamese encoders. Tri-encoders encode each item separately, then compute `cos(h(c; s_1), h(c; s_2))` where `h: R^(2d) -> R^d` is either an MLP or Hadamard-style composition.
- **Training objectives**: beyond MSE regression, the paper defines a quadruplet margin loss `Quad(p_1, p_2, n_1, n_2) = max(lambda + cos(n_1, n_2) - cos(p_1, p_2), 0)` using paired high- and low-similarity conditions; models are trained with `MSE`, `Quad`, or `Quad + MSE`.
- **Fine-tuning hyperparameters**: sweep over `batch_size = 32`, `epochs = 3`, learning rates `{1e-5, 3e-5}`, linear LR schedule, `warmup_ratio = 0.1`, weight decay `{0, 0.1}`, pooler with or without MLP, and 3 evaluation seeds for the best configuration.
- **Prompted LLM baselines**: Flan-T5, Flan-UL2, Tk-Instruct, ChatGPT-3.5 (`gpt-3.5-turbo-0301`), and GPT-4 (`gpt-4-0314`) are evaluated with `0`, `2`, or `4` in-context examples and short/long instructions; outputs are parsed into floats with at most `20` generation tokens.

## Key Results

- Dataset construction yields `20,000` initial instances and `18,908` retained examples after quality assurance, split `60:15:25` into train/validation/test.
- Annotation quality is reasonably strong: Fleiss' kappa is `0.61` on a 100-example sample with three annotations; average sentence and condition lengths are `12.6` and `5.3` words.
- Zero-shot transfer from classic STS is extremely weak: SimCSE-Large bi-encoder gets only Spearman `1.9` on C-STS despite `88.1` on STS-B; DiffCSE-Base gets `0.9` on C-STS versus `84.4` on STS-B.
- Among fine-tuned sentence encoders, the best test result is SimCSE-Large in a bi-encoder configuration with Spearman `47.5 ± 0.1` and Pearson `47.6 ± 0.1`; DiffCSE-Base bi-encoder reaches `43.4 ± 0.2`, and the best cross-encoder reaches `43.2 ± 1.2`.
- The proposed tri-encoder variants do not win empirically: the best tri-encoder result is SimCSE-Large with Spearman `35.3 ± 1.0`, well below the best bi-encoder.
- For prompted generative models, GPT-4 is strongest, reaching Spearman `43.6` with short instructions and 4-shot prompting (`42.6` at short 2-shot; `43.1` at long 4-shot). Flan-T5-XXL peaks around `30.6`, and GPT-3.5 remains far behind at roughly `15-17`.

## Limitations

- The dataset is sizable for evaluation but still modest for training robust [[sentence-embedding]] models; the paper explicitly notes that more data would likely help.
- Sentence pairs come only from MS COCO and Flickr30K captions, so the benchmark is grounded in English image-caption language rather than broader textual domains such as dialogue, scientific writing, or search queries.
- Each sentence pair is annotated with one high-similarity and one low-similarity condition, which gives targeted supervision but only sparsely samples the much larger space of plausible comparison aspects.
- The prompting experiments are limited to models and API versions available in early 2023, so the reported LLM gap should not be extrapolated to newer instruction-tuned or reasoning-oriented systems without re-running the benchmark.
