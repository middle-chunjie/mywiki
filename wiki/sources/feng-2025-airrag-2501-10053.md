## Summary

AirRAG proposes a training-free retrieval-augmented generation framework that expands complex QA reasoning into a tree instead of a single iterative chain. The method defines five reusable actions, uses Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to compose them into multiple reasoning trajectories, and applies self-consistency plus answer verification to select a final response. Its main claim is that complex retrieval-heavy questions benefit from explicit strategic planning, broader solution-space exploration, and compute allocation targeted at the most diversity-sensitive actions. Across multi-hop and open-domain QA benchmarks, AirRAG consistently outperforms vanilla RAG, iterative retrieval, and several agentic baselines, while remaining lightweight enough to plug into different LLMs and retrieval settings without additional model training.

## Problem & Motivation

Standard RAG and many iterative or agentic variants still search within a narrow chain-like reasoning space. For complex QA, a single query is often insufficient, weak intermediate steps can derail later retrieval, and smaller instruction-tuned LLMs struggle to autonomously plan multi-step search well. The paper targets this failure mode by making reasoning actions explicit, allowing branching exploration over alternative plans, and scaling inference-time computation in a controlled way rather than relying on one fragile reasoning trace.

## Method

- **Action space**: AirRAG defines five reasoning actions: system analysis `A_1` (SAY), direct answer `A_2` (DA), retrieval-answer `A_3` (RA), query transformation `A_4` (QT), and summary-answer `A_5` (SA). AirRAG-Lite simplifies this to `{A_3, A_4, A_5}` for better efficiency.
- **Tree-based generation**: the root state is the original question `s_0`; the policy is modeled as `\pi(a|s) = LM(a|s)`, and the next state is built by concatenating prior steps with the current action, `s_i = Concat(s_{0:i-1}, a_j)`.
- **Search rule**: node selection uses UCT, `UCT(s, p) = Q(s, a) / N(s) + w * sqrt(log N_p(s) / N(s))`, where `Q(s, a)` is the backpropagated reward, `N(s)` is node visit count, and `w` trades off exploration vs. exploitation.
- **Backpropagation and rewards**: when a terminal node reaches depth `d` or yields a final answer, the leaf reward `Q(s_d, a_d)` is propagated upward by `Q(s_i, a_i) = Q(s_i, a_i) + Q(s_d, a_d)`. With `n_all = 1`, SA yields a single answer node; with `n_all = 3`, clustered answer confidence is used as reward.
- **Inference scaling knobs**: the framework scales computation through retrieved document count, maximum effective context length `L_max`, number of rollouts, and number of per-action output sequences `n`. The paper studies `L_max` up to `128k` tokens and rollout counts up to `32`.
- **Sampling configuration**: default generator sampling is `top-p = 0.8`, `top-k = 50`, `temperature = 0.7`. For diversity-sensitive actions, especially SAY and QT, the paper also uses `q_div = 1.0`, i.e. `top-p = 1.0` and `temperature = 1.0`.
- **Pruning and deduplication**: AirRAG deduplicates state outputs per action and removes duplicate reasoning paths across rollouts, retaining only one valid path for repeated state sequences to reduce redundant inference.
- **Verification**: final-answer aggregation is tested with Jaccard-score clustering, embedding-similarity clustering, SA-based self-refinement, and a process-supervised reward model. The paper reports that SA and the reward model outperform simple averaging or lightweight self-consistency metrics.
- **Implementation details**: retrieval uses `multilingual-e5-base` over a December 2018 Wikipedia corpus. In scaling experiments, the maximum tree depth is `d = 10`; reward-model tuning uses `8,000` QA pairs per dataset and more than `156,000` reasoning paths generated with `rollouts = 32`, `n = 4`, and `q_div = 1.0`.

## Key Results

- On **Qwen2.5-14B**, AirRAG reaches average `66.1` F1 / `62.4` Acc across NQ, TriviaQA, HotpotQA, MuSiQue, and 2Wiki, versus vanilla RAG at `55.2` / `51.0` and IterDRAG at `57.6` / `52.7`.
- On **Qwen3-235B (thinking mode)**, AirRAG achieves `72.9` average F1 / `70.7` Acc, beating IterDRAG's `64.6` / `60.9` and Search-o1's `64.6` / `62.3`.
- Under a **32k** test-time budget with Qwen2.5-14B, AirRAG obtains `68.7` F1 / `64.2` Acc averaged over HotpotQA, MuSiQue, and 2Wiki, compared with IterDRAG at `57.1` / `51.4`; AirRAG-Blender further improves this to `69.9` / `67.1`.
- On **HotpotQA / MuSiQue / 2Wiki** at `L_max = 128k`, AirRAG reaches `83.3 / 78.0`, `43.5 / 36.5`, and `82.3 / 80.5` (F1 / Acc), showing strong gains on multi-hop settings, though MuSiQue remains substantially harder than the other two datasets.
- Compute allocation matters: on the three multi-hop datasets, baseline AirRAG with `n_all = 1` gets `62.9` F1 / `58.8` Acc; increasing all actions to `n_all = 3` yields `63.4` / `62.1`, while focusing diversity on SAY and QT plus `q_div = 1.0` improves to `65.1` / `63.9`.
- Efficiency is worse than simpler baselines but still manageable: at `L_max = 8k`, average end-to-end time on HotpotQA is `12.752s` for AirRAG versus `6.703s` for IterDRAG and `3.828s` for vanilla RAG; retrieval time rises to `3.453s` with `3.84` retrievals per sample.

## Limitations

- The method depends on substantially more inference-time search than vanilla or iterative RAG, so its latency and token cost grow quickly with rollout count, branching, and context budget.
- The best compute-allocation policy is hand-derived from extensive experiments rather than learned online, so deployment still requires manual tuning of `n`, rollout count, and diversity settings.
- Experiments are concentrated on QA benchmarks and Wikipedia-based retrieval; the paper does not establish comparable gains for broader agent tasks, other corpora, or non-QA downstream objectives.
- The larger search space also introduces noisy or redundant trajectories, making answer selection more important and motivating an additional reward model whose training requires expensive path generation and labeled correctness signals.
