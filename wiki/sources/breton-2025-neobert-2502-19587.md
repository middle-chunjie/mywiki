## Summary

NeoBERT is a `250M`-parameter encoder-only Transformer that modernizes the BERT family with current architectural choices, larger-scale web pretraining, and a controlled embedding evaluation protocol. Relative to BERT and RoBERTa, it keeps `d_model = 768` for plug-and-play compatibility but deepens to `28` layers, replaces absolute positions with RoPE, uses Pre-RMSNorm and SwiGLU, trains on RefinedWeb, and extends context from `1,024` to `4,096` tokens through a two-stage schedule. The paper ablates ten cumulative design variants on GLUE, then compares pre-trained backbones under a unified contrastive-learning setup on MTEB to isolate the effect of pretraining. NeoBERT4096 reaches `89.0` average on GLUE, `51.3` average on MTEB under identical fine-tuning, and `66.60` with contextual document embeddings while remaining faster than ModernBERT on long sequences.

## Problem & Motivation

The paper argues that encoder-only models remain crucial for retrieval, classification, and embedding-heavy NLP workloads, but BERT-style backbones have lagged behind decoder models in architecture, data freshness, and training methodology. Recent embedding leaders often win through sophisticated fine-tuning rather than stronger pre-trained encoders, making it hard to attribute gains to the backbone itself. NeoBERT is motivated by the need for an up-to-date, open, compact encoder that preserves BERT's deployment ergonomics while incorporating modern Transformer design, larger and newer corpora, longer context, and a fair evaluation framework that separates pretraining improvements from downstream tuning tricks.

## Method

- **Backbone scaling**: NeoBERT keeps `d_model = 768` and `12` attention heads for compatibility, but increases depth to `28` layers for roughly `250M` parameters and a more favorable depth-to-width ratio than BERT-style large models.
- **Modernized Transformer blocks**: it replaces post-normalization with Pre-RMSNorm, uses RoPE instead of absolute positional embeddings, and swaps GELU for SwiGLU while shrinking the intermediate size by `2/3` to keep parameter count controlled.
- **Pretraining corpus**: the model is trained on RefinedWeb (`600B`-token corpus) rather than older curated mixtures like Wikipedia + BookCorpus, aiming for broader and more current coverage.
- **Two-stage context extension**: stage 1 trains for `1M` steps on sequences up to `1,024` tokens (`2T` theoretical tokens); stage 2 adds `50k` steps and raises max length to `4,096`, sampling RefinedWeb / RefinedWeb1024+ / RefinedWeb2048+ with probabilities `0.2 / 0.4 / 0.4`.
- **Masked language modeling objective**: NeoBERT drops next-sentence prediction and uses MLM only, with masking rate increased to `20%` and a `100%` mask replacement scheme instead of BERT's `80/10/10`.
- **Optimization**: AdamW with `β1 = 0.9`, `β2 = 0.95`, `ε = 1e-8`, `weight_decay = 0.1`, linear warmup for `2,000` steps to peak `lr = 6e-4`, then cosine decay to `10%` of peak, plus gradient clipping at max norm `1.0`.
- **Training scale**: the batch size is `2M` tokens; appendix details report training on `8` H100 GPUs for `1,050,000` steps and roughly `6,000` GPU-hours.
- **Systems optimizations**: the implementation uses DeepSpeed with ZeRO, xFormers fused operators, no biases, dimensions aligned to multiples of `64`, and FlashAttention to reduce memory and attention overhead.
- **Ablation protocol**: ten cumulative variants `M0` to `M9` are fully trained for `1M` steps each, isolating the effect of architecture, data, tokenizer, optimizer, masking, packing, scale, depth-width ratio, batch size, and long-context training.
- **Embedding evaluation**: to compare pre-trained backbones fairly, all models are fine-tuned for `2,000` steps with the same contrastive objective `` `L = -log exp(s(q,d+)/τ) / (exp(s(q,d+)/τ) + Σ exp(s(q,d-)/τ))` ``, cosine similarity, `τ = 0.07`, in-batch negatives, and task-specific instructions on `9M` positive pairs.

## Key Results

- On GLUE development, NeoBERT4096 reaches `89.0`, exceeding BERTlarge (`82.1`), NomicBERT2048 (`84.0`), and ModernBERT base (`88.5`), while NeoBERT1024 scores `88.8`.
- In unified MTEB fine-tuning, NeoBERT4096 achieves `51.3` average, ahead of the strongest listed baseline (`49.1` for BERTlarge) and above ModernBERT (`45.0`) and NomicBERT2048 (`47.1`).
- With contextual document embeddings, NeoBERT reaches `66.60` on MTEB, beating ModernBERT (`65.68`) and NomicBERT (`65.00`) to rank first among English models under `400M` parameters as of April 2025.
- The biggest positive ablation effects on GLUE come from switching to RefinedWeb (`+3.6%` relative, `M2`) and scaling model size from `120M` to `250M` (`+2.9%`, `M7`); the largest drops come from LLaMA tokenizer replacement (`-2.1%`, `M3`) and naive sequence packing (`-2.8%`, `M6`).
- Long-context training extends usable context from `1,024` to `4,096` tokens; the paper reports NeoBERT1024 already generalizes near `3,000` tokens and NeoBERT4096 models sequences up to roughly `6,000` tokens in appendix pseudo-perplexity tests.
- On efficiency, NeoBERT reaches `17.2k` tokens/s at length `4,096` versus `11.7k` for ModernBERT base, a reported `46.7%` speedup despite having about `100M` more parameters.

## Limitations

- The empirical study is almost entirely English-centric: GLUE, MTEB-English, and the pseudo-perplexity analysis on English Wikipedia do not establish multilingual robustness.
- Strong embedding performance still depends on downstream contrastive fine-tuning or CDE; the paper itself notes that out-of-the-box pre-trained encoder evaluation on MTEB is inconclusive.
- Some retained design choices have negative small-scale ablation effects, including AdamW plus cosine decay (`-0.5%`) and the `20%` masking rate (`-0.7%`), so the justification partly relies on extrapolation rather than direct large-scale verification.
- The dominant gains come from larger and newer web data, and the broader-impact section explicitly notes that retraining on fresher corpora will likely be necessary as pretraining data ages.
- The paper focuses on discriminative NLP and embedding tasks; it does not test multilingual transfer, non-text modalities, or generative workloads where encoder-decoder or decoder-only models may remain preferable.
