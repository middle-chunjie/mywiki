## Summary

RAGEval introduces a schema-driven pipeline for automatically generating scenario-specific evaluation datasets for [[retrieval-augmented-generation]] systems. Given seed documents, it extracts a domain schema, generates diverse configurations, synthesizes documents, and then produces question–reference–answer (QRA) triples with associated keypoints. Three novel generation metrics—[[completeness-metric]], [[hallucination-metric]], and [[irrelevance-metric]]—are grounded in extracted factual keypoints and measure semantic coverage rather than surface-level overlap. A multilingual Chinese–English benchmark, DragonBall, is released covering finance, law, and medical domains (6,711 questions). LLM-based scoring of the proposed metrics achieves absolute agreement with human annotators within 1.67% (Fleiss' κ = 0.77), and the framework's generated documents consistently outperform zero- and one-shot baselines in safety, clarity, conformity, and richness.

## Problem & Motivation

Evaluating RAG systems in specialized domains requires datasets with domain-specific factual content, yet existing benchmarks (HotpotQA, TriviaQA, MS MARCO, Natural Questions) lack sufficient domain coverage and rely on coarse metrics like ROUGE-L and BLEU that measure lexical overlap rather than factual accuracy. Manual annotation is prohibitively expensive, and real-world data raises privacy concerns. LLM-based evaluation approaches like RAGAS and ARES suffer from stability and comparability issues. A scalable, automated framework for constructing scenario-specific evaluation sets with stable, fine-grained metrics is lacking.

## Method

The RAGEval pipeline follows the sequence: `S → C → D → (Q, A) → R → Keypoints`.

**Schema Summary (S)**
- A schema is an abstract template of key domain elements extracted from seed documents using GPT.
- Schemas are domain-specific (e.g., finance: sector/org/metrics; law: case law/statutes; medicine: symptoms/treatments).
- Schemas undergo iterative human refinement to balance comprehensiveness and generalizability; keys are normalized to generic field names (not instance-specific strings) to enable universal code processing.

**Configuration and Document Generation (C → D)**
- Configurations `C` assign concrete values to schema fields, using a hybrid approach: rule-based methods for structured data (ensuring factual consistency), LLMs for complex narrative fields.
- GPT-4o converts factual configurations into structured domain narratives (e.g., medical records with patient info + treatment plan; financial reports with multi-section outline).
- A hierarchical mechanism handles complex multi-section documents; standardized company profiles are pre-generated and injected for consistency.

**QRA Generation (Q, A, R, Keypoints)**
- Questions `Q` are conditioned on configurations `C` and documents `D`; GPT-4o generates initial answers `A` with 7 question types: Factual (FQ), Summarization (SQ), Multi-hop Reasoning (MRQ), Information Integration (IIQ), Numerical Comparison (NCQ), Temporal Sequence (TSQ), Unanswerable (UQ).
- References `R` are extracted from documents by retrieval prompt; answers are iteratively refined to align with references (removing unsupported content, supplementing missing facts).
- Keypoints (3–5 per answer) are extracted with in-context learning prompts as concise factual representations.

**Evaluation Metrics**
- **Retrieval Recall**: `Recall = (1/n) Σᵢ 𝟙(M(Gᵢ, R))` where `M` returns true iff all sentences in ground-truth reference `Gᵢ` appear in at least one retrieved passage in `R`.
- **Effective Information Rate (EIR)**: `EIR = Σᵢ|Gᵢ ∩ Rₜ| / Σⱼ|Rⱼ|` — proportion of retrieved content that is relevant (sentence-level intersection).
- **Completeness**: `Comp(A, K) = (1/|K|) Σᵢ 𝟙[A covers kᵢ]` — fraction of keypoints semantically covered by the generated answer.
- **Hallucination**: `Hallu(A, K) = (1/|K|) Σᵢ 𝟙[A contradicts kᵢ]` — fraction of keypoints contradicted.
- **Irrelevance**: `Irr(A, K) = 1 − Comp(A, K) − Hallu(A, K)` — fraction of keypoints neither covered nor contradicted.

**DragonBall Dataset**: `512` default chunk size, `TopK = 5`, max query length `128` tokens; BGE-M3 used as default retriever; generation batch size `5`, max `512` new tokens; ChatGPT temperature `0.2`, TopP `1.0`; FlashRAG inference pipeline with vLLM backend.

## Key Results

- **Document quality**: RAGEval outperforms zero-shot and one-shot baselines in >85% of cases across richness, clarity, and safety in both CN and EN.
- **QRA quality**: Human review scores ~4.94 (Finance/CN), ~4.81 (Law/CN), ~4.76 (Medical/CN) out of 5; combined score-4+5 proportion ≥95% across all scenarios.
- **Generation (GPT-4o best)**: Completeness 79.13% (CN), 69.36% (EN); Hallucination 12.10% (CN), 13.79% (EN).
- **Best open-source (small)**: MiniCPM3-4B achieves Completeness 75.74% (CN) with Hallucination 13.78%, competitive with GPT-3.5-Turbo (75.40% CN).
- **Scaling law validated**: Qwen1.5-14B-chat outperforms Qwen1.5-7B-chat on both Completeness and Hallucination within family.
- **Metric gap**: Baichuan-2-7B-chat scores highest Rouge-L (38.30% CN) yet only 60.25% Completeness; GPT-4o scores best Completeness at 79.13% but low Rouge-L (21.30%), demonstrating that lexical metrics systematically misrank models.
- **Retrieval models**: BM25 achieves Recall 74.21% (CN) and Completeness 71.89% (CN), surpassing dense retrievers; attributed to keyword-matching alignment with queries in the benchmark.
- **TopK effect**: Increasing TopK from 2→5 boosts Completeness by 27.87% (CN); 5→8 yields only 2.76% marginal gain, indicating diminishing returns.
- **Per-query type sensitivity**: FQ optimal at 256-10; MRQ at 512-5; NCQ at 128-20, demonstrating no universal configuration.
- **Human-LLM alignment**: Absolute metric difference 1.67%; Fleiss' κ = 0.7686.

## Limitations

- Document and answer generation relies on LLMs (GPT-4o), which can introduce hallucinations despite prompt engineering and iterative refinement.
- Using advanced closed-source models is costly; open-source alternatives provide a lower-cost path but may degrade dataset quality.
- Coverage limited to three domains (finance, law, medicine); generalization to other specialized domains (e.g., scientific literature, clinical trials) is left as future work.
- The schema construction step still requires human refinement iterations, limiting full automation.
