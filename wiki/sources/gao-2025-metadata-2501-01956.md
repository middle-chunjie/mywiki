## Summary

This paper proposes Metadata Conditioning then Cooldown (MeCo), a pre-training recipe that prepends document metadata, mainly absolute source domains such as `en.wikipedia.org`, during the first `90%` of training and then removes metadata for the final `10%` cooldown. The core claim is that metadata provides useful grouping signals over heterogeneous web corpora, improving data efficiency without extra model-side computation. Across `600M` to `8B` Llama-style models and three corpora (`DCLM`, `RefinedWeb`, `C4`), MeCo consistently improves downstream evaluation. On a `1.6B` model trained on `160B` DCLM tokens, it matches the average downstream score of a standard `240B`-token baseline, implying about `33%` less data and compute. The paper also shows that metadata-conditioned models can be steered at inference time with real or fabricated URLs.

## Problem & Motivation

Web-scale pre-training corpora mix documents with very different styles, quality levels, and communicative goals, yet standard language-model pre-training treats them as undifferentiated next-token examples. The authors argue that this discards a readily available supervision signal: document metadata, especially source URLs from CommonCrawl-derived corpora. Their goal is to use such metadata to make pre-training more data-efficient and to induce controllable behaviors, while avoiding the deployment-time failure mode where a model depends on metadata being present at inference. MeCo is designed to capture the benefits of source-aware conditioning during training, then recover normal metadata-free usage through a cooldown stage on plain text.

## Method

- **Training recipe**: MeCo runs two stages over the same pre-training schedule: metadata-conditioned training for the first `90%` of tokens, then a standard-data cooldown for the final `10%`. Inputs use a template like `URL: en.wikipedia.org\n\n[document]`.
- **Loss masking**: the model conditions on metadata tokens but the cross-entropy objective is computed only on document tokens, not on the `URL:` prefix or metadata span, because supervising those tokens slightly hurt downstream performance in preliminary experiments.
- **Metadata choice**: the main metadata `c` is the document's absolute domain name extracted from its URL, e.g. `en.wikipedia.org` from `https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Gates`; ablations also test full URLs, suffixes, hashed URLs, and model-generated topic labels.
- **Cooldown mechanics**: the cooldown stage inherits model weights, optimizer state, and the same cosine-decay learning-rate schedule from stage 1, resuming at the `90%` point of the schedule rather than restarting optimization.
- **Backbone and tokenization**: all experiments use a Llama-style `[[transformer]]` with the Llama-3 tokenizer. Models span `600M`, `1.6B`, `3B`, and `8B` parameters; the main `1.6B` model uses `24` layers, hidden size `2048`, intermediate size `5504`, `16` heads, and head dimension `128`.
- **Optimization**: training uses AdamW with `β_1 = 0.9`, `β_2 = 0.95`, learning rate `3e-3` (`5e-4` for `8B`), weight decay `0.033` (`0.1` for `8B`), batch size `4M` tokens, `5%` linear warmup, cosine decay to `10%` of peak LR, and packed sequence length `8192`.
- **Data handling**: when packing documents, each sequence is forced to start at a fresh document boundary even if this discards some tokens; the authors also disable [[cross-document-attention]], reporting a `25%` training speedup for the `1.6B` model and better baseline downstream results.
- **Evaluation**: downstream performance is measured with `[[olmes]]` plus TruthfulQA, using `5`-shot prompting by default and `1,000` sampled examples per task. The main aggregate metric is the average over `10` tasks rather than validation [[perplexity]].
- **Inference-time steering**: after MeCo training, prompts can be conditioned with real or synthetic URLs such as `www.factquizmaster.com` or `en.wikipedia.org`, enabling task-specific performance gains and lower toxicity without retraining.

## Key Results

- On the main `1.6B` / `160B`-token DCLM setup, MeCo improves the `10`-task average from `55.7` to `56.7`, matching a standard model trained on `240B` tokens (`56.7`) with about `33%` less data and compute.
- Relative to the standard `160B` baseline, MeCo gains `+1.4` on ARC-c (`42.7 -> 44.1`), `+5.2` on OBQA (`46.0 -> 51.2`), `+2.2` on WinoGrande (`62.0 -> 64.2`), and `+3.3` on TruthfulQA (`35.2 -> 38.5`), while losing `1.0` on CSQA and `1.6` on SIQA.
- Conditional inference further improves MeCo from `56.7` to `57.2` average, whereas the same URL prompting barely changes the standard model (`55.7 -> 55.8`).
- URL choice matters: in zero-shot evaluation on the MeCo `1.6B` model, `www.factmonster.com` beats `boards.4chan.org` by `7.3` points on CommonsenseQA (`60.9` vs `53.6`) and by `4.6` on OBQA (`52.4` vs `47.8`).
- The two-stage recipe is important: training on `100%` URL-conditioned data drops the `10`-task average to `50.3`, while MeCo reaches `56.7`; direct `90% URL + 10% standard` mixing is weaker at `56.4`.
- Alternative metadata mostly preserve gains: hashed URLs match MeCo exactly at `56.7`, model-generated topics reach `56.6`, and full URLs slightly improve to `56.8`; URL suffixes degrade to `56.2`, indicating that grouping granularity matters.
- MeCo improves across model scales (`600M`, `1.6B`, `3B`, `8B`) and across corpora (`C4`, `RefinedWeb`, `DCLM`). The main `1.6B` / `160B` configuration takes about `1536` H100 GPU-hours, roughly `2` days on `32` H100s.

## Limitations

- The paper reports single-run pre-training experiments because full replication is expensive; although appendix variance estimates are low (`±0.1` average across three resumed baseline runs), the main claims are not backed by full multi-seed studies.
- All experiments use English corpora and Llama-style autoregressive models, so transfer to multilingual settings or other architectures is untested.
- The mechanism behind metadata gains remains unresolved: the authors only hypothesize better domain prioritization or more structured corpus representations.
- Model-generated topic metadata is informative but impractical here, costing roughly `1,500` GPU-hours, comparable to pre-training the main `1.6B` model itself.
- The work studies pre-training and prompt-time conditioning, but not the interaction between metadata conditioning and later post-training or alignment stages.
