## Summary

This paper argues that long chain-of-thought reasoning can be distilled into a standard instruct model with surprisingly little data and minimal parameter updates. Using `17k` long-CoT demonstrations from DeepSeek-R1 or QwQ-32B-Preview, the authors fine-tune Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct via full supervised fine-tuning or LoRA and obtain large gains on math and coding benchmarks. The central claim is not merely that distillation works, but that the decisive property of successful demonstrations is their global structure: reflection, backtracking, and self-validation arranged in a logically coherent sequence. Perturbing local content, including wrong answers or corrupted numbers, hurts far less than disrupting the ordering and continuity of reasoning steps, suggesting that long-CoT learning is primarily a structural imitation problem.

## Problem & Motivation

Large reasoning models such as o1-style systems and DeepSeek-R1 exhibit strong performance by producing long chains of thought with reflection, revision, and self-checking, but it is unclear what training signal actually teaches this behavior. Existing reasoning models are often closed or expensive to reproduce, so the paper asks whether strong long-CoT behavior can be distilled cheaply into an open model, and which properties of the demonstrations matter most. The authors focus on separating local content correctness from global reasoning organization to determine whether reasoning improvements come from factual/step-level correctness or from learning the structural pattern of coherent multi-step deliberation.

## Method

- **Student/teacher setup**: use `Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct` as the student, with `DeepSeek-R1` and `QwQ-32B-Preview` as teacher models that generate long-CoT demonstrations.
- **Training data curation**: collect `12k` math and `5k` coding problems (`17k` total), selecting harder prompts from AMC/AIME, Numina-Math, OlympiadBench-style math subsets, APPS, and TACO; verify correctness by exact-match checking for math and execution for code.
- **Difficulty filtering**: use `GPT-4o-mini` to score prompt difficulty and keep math prompts above roughly `Level 3` and olympiad prompts above roughly `Level 8`, plus all AIME/AMC problems.
- **Fine-tuning recipe**: train with `Llama-Factory`, batch size `96`, learning rate `1e-5`, warm-up ratio `0.1`, and linear decay under next-token prediction loss; LoRA uses the same recipe except learning rate `1e-4`.
- **Parameter-efficient setting**: evaluate LoRA with ranks such as `r = 64`, showing that updating under `5%` of parameters can match or exceed full supervised fine-tuning on several reasoning benchmarks.
- **Data-efficiency study**: vary the amount of R1 data from `4k` to `64k` samples to test how quickly long-CoT behavior emerges.
- **Content perturbation study**: preserve long-CoT structure while corrupting local contents via wrong-answer traces, random digit replacement at rates `20%`, `50%`, `70%`, `100%`, and removal of reasoning-keyword sentences at the same rates.
- **Structure perturbation study**: segment `4,618` correct traces into reasoning steps using `Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct`, then modify `33%`, `67%`, or `100%` of steps through deletion, insertion, or shuffling while leaving the final solution block intact.
- **Evaluation**: report results on `Math-500`, `AIME 2024`, `AMC 2023`, `OlympiadBench`, and `LiveCodeBench`, plus non-reasoning benchmarks (`MMLU`, `ARC-C`, `IEval`, `MGSM`) and comparison against oracle `Best-of-N` sampling.

## Key Results

- With `17k` R1 samples, the fine-tuned model reaches `90.8` on Math-500, `56.7%` on AIME 2024 (`+40.0`), `85.0%` on AMC 2023 (`+17.5`), `60.3%` on OlympiadBench (`+12.7`), and `57.0%` on LiveCodeBench (`+8.1`), broadly competitive with `o1-preview` (`44.6%` AIME, `59.1%` LiveCodeBench).
- Scaling R1 data from the base model average `54.2` to `69.4` with only `16k` samples shows that a relatively small supervised set is enough to recover most of the gain.
- LoRA remains strong: with `7k` QwQ samples and `r = 64`, LoRA reaches `40.0%` on AIME 2024 versus `33.3%` for full SFT, while using far fewer trainable parameters.
- Training on entirely wrong-answer traces still yields average accuracy `63.1`, only `3.2` points below training on fully correct traces (`66.3`), indicating robustness to incorrect local content.
- Corrupting `70%` of digits still gives average accuracy `62.0`, but corrupting `100%` of digits collapses performance to `2.7`, implying some floor of local semantic integrity is still necessary.
- Removing `100%` of reasoning-keyword sentences still leaves average accuracy at `63.0`, only `3.3` below the correct-trace baseline.
- Structural corruption is much more damaging: shuffling `67%` of steps drops AIME 2024 from `40.0` to `26.7`, deleting `100%` of steps drops average accuracy to `49.5`, and inserting incoherent steps at `100%` drops it further to `44.5`.
- On non-reasoning tasks, distilled models largely retain base-model capability; for example, R1-LoRA scores `75.5` on MMLU and `78.4` on IEval versus the base model's `74.1` and `78.7`, avoiding QwQ's severe IEval/MGSM degradation.
- Long-CoT fine-tuning performs comparably to oracle `Best-of-N` sampling with roughly `2` to `16` samples, depending on the student model.

## Limitations

- The main student model is `Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct`; cross-model transfer is tested, but the core structural claims are still established primarily on one strong student architecture.
- The causal account is behavioral rather than mechanistic: the paper shows sensitivity to structure perturbations, but does not explain which internal representations or training dynamics encode that structure.
- The strongest structure-vs-content analysis is run on a `4,618`-trace math subset, so the conclusion is less directly validated for broader domains beyond math-heavy reasoning.
- Teacher quality and data curation remain important; the method is cheap relative to RL, but it still depends on access to high-quality long-CoT teacher traces and reliable correctness filtering.
- Comparison against reinforcement learning and other post-training pipelines is limited, so the paper does not fully establish when simple distillation should replace RL-based reasoning training.
