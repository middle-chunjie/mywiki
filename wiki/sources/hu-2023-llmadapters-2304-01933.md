## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature. LLM-Adapters presents a unified framework and empirical study for adapter-based parameter-efficient fine-tuning of open-access large language models, focusing on LLaMA, BLOOMz, and GPT-J. The paper compares Prefix-Tuning, Series Adapter, Parallel Adapter, and LoRA across fourteen reasoning benchmarks and isolates which insertion locations and hyperparameter settings work best. It also constructs two task-specific supervision sets, Math10K and Commonsense170K, to study both in-distribution and out-of-distribution behavior. The central result is that relatively small LLMs with a small number of trainable adapter parameters can approach or exceed much larger closed models on some reasoning tasks, especially when the fine-tuning data matches the downstream distribution.

## Problem & Motivation

Full-model fine-tuning of large language models is expensive in compute and storage, which makes practical adaptation difficult for many users of open-access LLMs. Prior PEFT work had produced multiple adapter families, but it remained unclear which adapter type, placement location, and hyperparameter setting worked best once these methods were applied to modern LLM backbones rather than smaller pretrained encoders. The paper addresses that gap by building a single framework for multiple adapter families and then running a controlled comparison on math and commonsense reasoning tasks, with explicit attention to both in-distribution and out-of-distribution transfer.

## Method

- **Unified framework**: LLM-Adapters integrates four representative PEFT families into open-access LLMs: Prefix-Tuning, Series Adapter, Parallel Adapter, and LoRA, with experiments on `LLaMA-7B`, `LLaMA-13B`, `BLOOMz-7B`, and `GPT-J-6B`.
- **Prompt / prefix formulation**: prompt-based PEFT augments attention with learned prefix states, written as `H_o = Attn(H_i W_Q, [P_K; H_i W_K], [P_V; H_i W_V])`, where `P_K, P_V in R^{L x d}` and `L` is the number of virtual tokens.
- **LoRA formulation**: reparameterized updates use `H_o = H_i W_0 + H_i DeltaW = H_i W_0 + H_i B A`, where `r << d` is the low-rank bottleneck; the paper treats LoRA rank `r` as a key search variable.
- **Series adapter formulation**: a sequential bottleneck module is inserted as `H_o <- H_o + f(H_o W_down) W_up`, with bottleneck dimension `bn` controlling adapter capacity.
- **Parallel adapter formulation**: a parallel bottleneck branch is inserted as `H_o <- H_o + f(H_i W_down) W_up`, so the adapter path runs alongside the original sublayer rather than after it.
- **Training data construction**: the authors build `Math10K` by combining GSM8K, MAWPS, MAWPS-single, and `1,000` AQuA examples, then use ChatGPT with zero-shot chain-of-thought prompts to synthesize rationales and filter incorrect answers. They also build `Commonsense170K` from BoolQ, PIQA, SIQA, HellaSwag, WinoGrande, ARC-e, ARC-c, and OBQA using task-specific templates.
- **Benchmarks**: evaluation covers `14` datasets, split into `6` arithmetic-reasoning datasets and `8` commonsense-reasoning datasets.
- **Optimization**: all PEFT runs use batch size `16` and `3` epochs; Prefix-Tuning uses learning rate `3e-2`, while Series Adapter, Parallel Adapter, and LoRA use `3e-4`.
- **Placement search**: Series Adapter is tested after attention, after MLP, or after both; Parallel Adapter and LoRA are inserted in attention, MLP, or both attention and MLP; Prefix-Tuning is excluded because its placement is fixed.
- **Hyperparameter search**: Prefix-Tuning sweeps virtual tokens `vt in {10, 20, 30, 40}`; Series and Parallel adapters sweep bottleneck size `bn in {64, 128, 256, 512}`; LoRA sweeps rank `r in {4, 8, 16, 32}`.

## Key Results

- On math reasoning, `LLaMA-13B + LoRA` reaches `65.4%` average accuracy across six datasets versus `70.4%` for `GPT-3.5`, while surpassing GPT-3.5 on `MultiArith (94.8 vs 83.8)`, `AddSub (87.3 vs 85.3)`, and `SingleEq (89.8 vs 88.1)`.
- On commonsense reasoning, `LLaMA-13B + Parallel Adapter` achieves the best average score at `81.5%`, exceeding `ChatGPT` at `77.0%` by `4.5` points.
- Placement matters materially: on `LLaMA-7B` math reasoning, Series Adapter performs best after the `MLP` layer at `59.5%`, Parallel Adapter performs best in `MLP` at `61.7%`, and LoRA performs best when inserted into both attention and MLP at `60.0%`.
- The best configuration search finds `vt = 10` for Prefix-Tuning (`42.0%` average), `bn = 256` for both Series Adapter (`59.5%`) and Parallel Adapter (`61.7%`), and `r = 32` for LoRA (`61.9%`).
- The paper argues that in-distribution supervision is especially powerful: with `Commonsense170K`, `LLaMA-13B` adapters outperform stronger closed baselines, while math gains on OOD sets remain uneven because complex datasets such as `GSM8K` and `AQuA` still lag GPT-3.5.

## Limitations

- The study is limited to relatively small open-access backbones up to `13B`; it does not test whether the same design conclusions hold for larger models such as `33B` or `65B`.
- The evaluation is concentrated on arithmetic and commonsense reasoning, so the conclusions may not transfer directly to other task families such as generation, retrieval, or multimodal instruction following.
- Math10K rationales are synthesized with ChatGPT, which may introduce teacher-specific biases into both the fine-tuning data and the resulting comparison.
- The work compares single adapter families but does not explore adapter composition or hybrid PEFT pipelines, leaving a large design space untested.
