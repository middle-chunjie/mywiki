## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature.

ALCUNA introduces [[knowgen]], a structured procedure for generating durable new knowledge by recombining ontology-style attributes and relations of existing biological entities, and uses it to build a benchmark of `84,351` questions over `3,554` artificial organisms. The benchmark separates [[knowledge-understanding]], [[knowledge-differentiation]], and [[knowledge-association]], allowing evaluation of whether [[large-language-model]]s can absorb, distinguish, and reason with unseen knowledge rather than merely recall memorized facts. Experiments on [[chatgpt]], [[alpaca-7b]], [[vicuna-13b]], and [[chatglm-6b]] under [[zero-shot-learning]], [[few-shot-learning]], and [[chain-of-thought-prompting]] settings show that even the strongest model struggles, especially when reasoning must connect new and internal knowledge. The paper argues that synthetic, structured benchmarks provide a model-agnostic way to stress-test LLM behavior under continual knowledge change.

## Problem & Motivation

Existing NLP and LLM benchmarks mostly test knowledge that may already appear in model pretraining corpora, so high scores can conflate memorization with real generalization. The paper targets a harder and practically important setting: models encountering genuinely new knowledge at inference time, including facts introduced through changing environments or retrieval systems. The authors argue that a useful benchmark for this setting should be sufficient in scale, model-agnostic with respect to training-data leakage, and long-lasting rather than quickly becoming outdated. They therefore avoid closed evaluation and temporal news collection, and instead construct artificial but plausible biological entities whose properties are new to every model while still remaining embedded in an existing knowledge system.

## Method

- **Knowledge formalization**: represent an entity `e` as attribute triplets `T_A(e) = {(e, a, v)}` and relation triplets `T_R(e) = {(e, r, e')}`; a class `C` groups similar entities and common class-level properties.
- **Parent and sibling selection**: choose a parent entity `e^p` from class `C`, define siblings `sib(e^p)`, and split the parent's attribute/relation triplets into heredity, variation, and dropout subsets via `RandomSplit`.
- **Heredity**: preserve class-common and parent properties so the new entity remains plausible within the class, e.g. `T_R(\tilde{e}) <- T_R(C) ∪ T_R^h` and `T_A(\tilde{e}) <- T_A(C) ∪ T_A^h`.
- **Variation of attributes**: for numeric values, perturb with Gaussian noise `\tilde{v} = v + N(0, v / 10)`; for non-numeric values, copy an attribute value from a sibling entity to create novelty without leaving the class manifold.
- **Variation of relations**: for a relation triplet `(e^p, r, e')`, replace the object with a sibling of `e'` so the new entity acquires a related but novel relational profile.
- **Extension and dropout**: sample extra properties `T^e = RandomSample(T(E^{psb}))` from siblings and remove some parent properties, yielding a mix of inheritance, mutation, deletion, and augmentation.
- **Entity naming**: construct names by recombining subwords from related entity names using GPT-2 tokenization; the example `ALCUNA` is derived from `Alpaca` and `Vicuna`.
- **Benchmark construction**: instantiate the method on [[encyclopedia-of-life]] taxonomic data, excluding overly high taxonomic levels and filtering parents with fewer than `3` properties; the resulting benchmark centers on biologically plausible [[artificial-entity]] instances.
- **Question generation**: use [[chatgpt]] to generate `5` templates per property group for multiple-choice, fill-in-the-blank, and Boolean questions; one-hop questions target direct properties, while relation chains produce [[multi-hop-reasoning]] questions.
- **Evaluation setup**: test [[chatgpt]], [[alpaca-7b]], [[vicuna-13b]], and [[chatglm-6b]] in zero-shot and few-shot settings; few-shot uses `3-5` examples per task type, and CoT appends `"Let's think step by step."` or includes worked rationales in demonstrations.
- **Filtering for fair comparison**: retain only questions whose prerequisite existing knowledge can be recalled by each model, then evaluate on the intersection so KD/KA comparisons focus on behavior under new knowledge rather than missing background knowledge.

## Key Results

- The benchmark contains `84,351` questions about `3,554` artificial entities, with `11.75` property triplets and `25.39` siblings per artificial entity on average.
- Automatic question generation is reasonably accurate under manual inspection: `98%` correctness for one-hop questions and `95%` for multi-hop questions on `100` sampled examples each.
- In Few-Shot-CoT, overall average scores are `74.11` for [[chatgpt]], `47.73` for [[vicuna-13b]], `38.02` for [[alpaca-7b]], and `37.64` for [[chatglm-6b]].
- [[knowledge-association]] is consistently the hardest setting: in Few-Shot-Vanilla, KA scores are `41.52` (ChatGPT), `27.63` (Alpaca), `30.10` (Vicuna), and `27.47` (ChatGLM).
- Adding reasoning-chain entities to context improves KA performance, especially for ChatGLM (`27.47 -> 44.43`), supporting the claim that cross-linking new and internal knowledge is the main failure mode.
- Structured JSON knowledge outperforms natural-language input on all tested open models, e.g. average `42.74 vs 31.92` for Vicuna and `42.56 vs 20.54` for ChatGLM.

## Limitations

- The implementation is demonstrated only on biological data from [[encyclopedia-of-life]], so cross-domain validity is argued conceptually rather than empirically established.
- The evaluation set of models is small and mixed between closed and open systems, limiting conclusions about broader scaling trends.
- Fair KD/KA comparison requires model-specific filtering based on recoverable background knowledge, which shrinks the benchmark and complicates reuse on future models.
- The paper reports an internal count inconsistency: Section 3.4 lists KU/KD/KA sizes that do not sum to the stated total `84,351`, while Table 6 implies a larger KD subset.
- Question templates are generated automatically by [[chatgpt]]; despite high spot-check quality, some prompt-induced artifacts or phrasing biases may remain.
