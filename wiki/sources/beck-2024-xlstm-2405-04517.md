## Summary

xLSTM revisits LSTM-style recurrence at modern language-model scale by replacing standard gates and memory cells with two new variants: sLSTM, which adds exponential gating, a normalizer state, and structured memory mixing, and mLSTM, which replaces scalar memory with a matrix memory updated by a gated covariance rule and admits a parallel formulation. These cells are wrapped in residual xLSTM blocks and stacked into pre-LayerNorm language models. Across synthetic state-tracking tasks, SlimPajama pretraining, long-context extrapolation, downstream reasoning benchmarks, and PALOMA domain evaluation, xLSTM is consistently competitive with or better than Transformer, state-space, and recurrent baselines. The main claim is that modernized LSTMs can regain relevance for large-scale language modeling without giving up linear-time sequence processing.

## Problem & Motivation

The paper asks whether LSTMs can become competitive again in large language modeling if their main historical weaknesses are fixed instead of abandoning recurrence entirely. The authors identify three bottlenecks in vanilla LSTM: it cannot easily revise earlier storage decisions, it compresses memory into scalar cell states with limited capacity, and it relies on hidden-to-hidden memory mixing that prevents full parallelization. These issues matter especially for long-context language modeling, rare-token prediction, state tracking, and scaling to modern LLM training regimes. xLSTM is proposed as a redesign that keeps the favorable recurrent inductive bias and linear-in-context behavior of LSTMs while borrowing residual backbones, normalization, and scaling practices from contemporary LLMs.

## Method

- **sLSTM recurrence**: replaces the classic hidden state computation with normalized exponential-gated memory, `c_t = f_t c_{t-1} + i_t z_t`, `n_t = f_t n_{t-1} + i_t`, `h_t = o_t (c_t / n_t)`. The input gate uses `i_t = exp(\tilde{i}_t)`, while the forget gate can be `sigma(\tilde{f}_t)` or `exp(\tilde{f}_t)`.
- **Stabilized exponential gates**: introduces a stabilizer state `m_t = max(log f_t + m_{t-1}, log i_t)` so the effective gates become numerically safe reparameterizations `i'_t = exp(\tilde{i}_t - m_t)` and `f'_t = exp(log f_t + m_{t-1} - m_t)` without changing the forward result.
- **New memory mixing for sLSTM**: extends scalar memory to multi-cell / multi-head form with recurrent matrices `R_z, R_i, R_f, R_o`. In the appendix block design, sLSTM uses `4` heads, optional causal convolution of window size `4`, GroupNorm, and a gated MLP with projection factor `4/3`.
- **mLSTM matrix memory**: replaces scalar memory by a matrix state `C_t = f_t C_{t-1} + i_t v_t k_t^T`, with normalizer `n_t = f_t n_{t-1} + i_t k_t` and retrieval `h_t = o_t ⊙ (C_t q_t / max(|n_t^T q_t|, 1))`. Keys are scaled by `1/sqrt(d)` via `k_t = (1/sqrt(d)) W_k x_t + b_k`.
- **Parallel mLSTM formulation**: because mLSTM removes hidden-hidden memory mixing, the recurrence can be rewritten into a sequence-parallel matrix form with gate matrix `D' = exp(\tilde{D} - max \tilde{D})` and hidden states derived from `\tilde{C}' = (QK^T / sqrt(d)) ⊙ D'`.
- **Residual xLSTM blocks**: sLSTM uses a post-up-projection residual block similar to Transformer FFN placement, while mLSTM uses a pre-up-projection block closer to state-space models. The mLSTM block uses causal convolution kernel size `4`, a learnable skip connection, externalized output gating, GroupNorm, and down-projection after an up-projection factor of `2`.
- **Architecture assembly**: xLSTM models stack these blocks in a pre-LayerNorm residual backbone. The paper denotes mixtures as `xLSTM[a:b]`, where `a:b` is the ratio of mLSTM to sLSTM blocks; e.g. `xLSTM[7:1]` means `42` mLSTM blocks and `6` sLSTM blocks in a `48`-block network.
- **Experimental hyperparameters**: the 15B-token comparison matches GPT-3-scale models at roughly `~408M-409M` parameters with embedding dimension `1024` and `24` residual blocks. The 300B-token study trains `125M`, `350M`, `760M`, and `1.3B` models with training context length `2048` and evaluates extrapolation up to `16384`.

## Key Results

- On SlimPajama after `15B` training tokens, `xLSTM[1:0]` reaches validation perplexity `13.43` and `xLSTM[7:1]` reaches `13.48`, beating Mamba (`13.70`), HGRN2 (`14.32`), Llama (`14.25`), GPT-3 (`14.26`), and all other compared baselines at similar `~408M-423M` scale.
- On the `300B`-token study at `1.3B` scale, `xLSTM[1:0]` obtains SlimPajama perplexity `8.89`, versus Mamba `9.14`, Llama `9.44`, and RWKV-4 `9.83`. `xLSTM[7:1]` also remains competitive at `9.00`.
- On downstream evaluation at `1.3B`, `xLSTM[1:0]` achieves average accuracy `58.48`, narrowly ahead of Mamba (`58.41`) and clearly above Llama (`56.99`) and RWKV-4 (`54.78`). At `760M`, `xLSTM[1:0]` reaches average accuracy `56.12`.
- The best ARC numbers are not always from xLSTM, but xLSTM wins the vast majority of tasks across model sizes. For example at `350M`, `xLSTM[7:1]` reaches `71.16` on PIQA and `56.61` on ARC-E while keeping SlimPajama perplexity `11.37`.
- On PALOMA, `xLSTM[1:0]` is reported to have lower perplexity than Llama, Mamba, and RWKV-4 on `568 / 571` domains (`99.5%`), while the text notes that `xLSTM[1:0]` is generally stronger than `xLSTM[7:1]` on these language-modeling domains.
- For long-context extrapolation, models are trained at context length `2048` and tested up to `16384`; the paper reports that xLSTM maintains the lowest perplexity profile among the compared large models over this extrapolation range.

## Limitations

- mLSTM improves capacity through a `d x d` matrix memory and covariance-style updates, but the paper explicitly notes that this state is computationally expensive even if wall-clock cost stays manageable on GPUs.
- sLSTM retains hidden-hidden memory mixing and is therefore not fully parallelizable; the authors report that even with a custom CUDA kernel it is typically less than `2x` slower than mLSTM rather than equally efficient.
- The paper states that current mLSTM CUDA kernels are still under-optimized and are about `4x` slower than FlashAttention or the scan used in Mamba.
- The empirical evidence is concentrated on next-token language modeling, synthetic tasks, Long Range Arena, PALOMA, and lightweight downstream reasoning benchmarks; the paper does not validate xLSTM on instruction tuning, dialogue alignment, or multimodal settings.
- Most large-scale claims are made on English text corpora such as SlimPajama, Wikitext-103, and PALOMA domains, so cross-lingual and non-text generalization remain open.
- The paper notes additional engineering sensitivity: forget-gate initialization must be chosen carefully, and the fixed-size matrix memory could become overloaded at contexts longer than the `16k` range tested here.
- The work is an arXiv preprint, and some headline claims about scaling behavior and architectural superiority over Transformers / SSMs are extrapolated from experiments topping out at `1.3B` parameters rather than frontier-scale deployments.
