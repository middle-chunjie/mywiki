## Summary

This paper studies datastore size as a third scaling axis for retrieval-based language models, alongside model parameters and pretraining tokens. The authors build MassiveDS, a `1.4412T`-token open datastore spanning eight domains, and introduce a reordered pipeline that makes trillion-token datastore scaling experiments computationally feasible by indexing and retrieving once, then applying filtering, subsampling, and reranking on the retrieved pool. Across language modeling and downstream QA benchmarks, larger datastores improve performance monotonically without clear saturation, and the resulting retrieval-based systems are often more compute-efficient than LM-only baselines. The paper's main claim is practical: external non-parametric memory should be treated as a first-class component in language-model scaling and efficiency trade-offs.

## Problem & Motivation

Standard LM scaling laws focus on parameter count and pretraining data, but retrieval-based LMs also depend on how much external data is available at inference time. Prior work typically used small or single-domain datastores, often Wikipedia-scale, leaving it unclear whether retrieve-in-context systems continue to benefit from much larger and more diverse memory. The paper addresses this gap by asking whether scaling the inference-time datastore improves language modeling, downstream QA, and compute-optimal trade-offs, and whether such studies can be run without the prohibitive cost of rebuilding trillion-token indexes for every experimental variant.

## Method

- **Datastore construction**: build MassiveDS with `1,441.2B` tokens from `8` domains: Books (`26.3B`), STEM (`97.7B`), Encyclopedia (`31.9B`), Forum (`20.2B`), Code (`52.8B`), Math (`14.1B`), Biomedical (`6.5B`), and General Web (`1191.7B`).
- **Retrieve-in-context setup**: study retrieval-based LMs that prepend retrieved documents to the LM input rather than changing the LM architecture; documents are chunked into at most `256` words.
- **Retriever**: use Contriever-MSMARCO as a dense retriever, with each document encoded into a vector and searched by inner-product similarity over a flat FAISS index.
- **Evaluation protocol**: retrieve top `k = 3` documents, concatenate them in reverse rank order, and run `5`-shot prompting for downstream tasks; language modeling uses `1024`-token windows with `512`-token stride and treats the first half as retrieval prefix.
- **Efficient scaling pipeline**: retrieve a large pool `K = 1000` once per query, then apply deduplication, decontamination, reranking, and Bernoulli subsampling with datastore fraction `` `p ∈ {0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1}` `` and seeds `` `s ∈ {100, 101, 102}` ``.
- **Filtering details**: deduplicate retrieved documents with `13`-gram Jaccard similarity `>= 80%`; remove short chunks with fewer than `13` words; decontaminate downstream tasks with `13`-gram Jaccard `>= 80%` and language modeling with either `13`-gram Jaccard `>= 80%` or contiguous overlap of at least `32` tokens.
- **Compute accounting**: compare retrieval-based and LM-only scaling under training-time compute using `` `FLOPs_pretrain ≈ 6 N_LM D_pretrain` `` and `` `FLOPs_datastore ≈ 2 N_retriever D_datastore` ``, exploiting the fact that datastore construction is much cheaper than full LM pretraining.
- **Model families**: evaluate Llama-2 (`7B`, `13B`), Llama-3 (`8B`), Pythia (`1B`, `2.8B`, `6.9B`, `12B`), and OLMo (`1B`, `7B`) to study both direct scaling curves and compute-optimal frontiers.

## Key Results

- MassiveDS improves Llama-2 7B over the LM-only baseline on all reported main tasks in Table 3: TriviaQA `77.0` vs `64.1`, Natural Questions `34.6` vs `26.6`, MMLU `49.3` vs `45.8`, and MedQA `39.4` vs `36.6`.
- Retrieval also improves language modeling perplexity for Llama-2 7B: RedPajama drops from `4.09` to `3.50`, and S2ORC drops from `7.18` to `6.57`.
- On knowledge-intensive QA, a Llama-2 7B model with retrieval from fewer than `100B` tokens can outperform LM-only Llama-2 13B and LM-only Llama-3 8B on TriviaQA and Natural Questions.
- The proposed pipeline reduces the compute needed for datastore-scaling studies by more than an order of magnitude while remaining equivalent to the naive pipeline with high probability.
- Compute-optimal frontiers favor retrieval-based systems: for a fixed training budget, offloading some knowledge storage to the datastore yields better Pareto-optimal performance than training LM-only models.
- Retriever ablations on a `10%` MassiveDS sample show similar performance across dense retrievers, with Contriever reporting RedPajama PPL `4.2210`, NQ `0.3321`, and MMLU `0.4922`, supporting its use as the default retriever.

## Limitations

- The full scaling study uses only one retriever in the main experiments because swapping retrievers requires re-embedding and re-indexing the entire datastore.
- Compute-optimal analysis is limited to model families with released intermediate checkpoints, so the frontier estimates are approximate rather than results from matched from-scratch training runs.
- MassiveDS is broad but may still miss the right in-domain sources for harder reasoning-heavy tasks such as MMLU and MedQA, where gains are weaker than on factual QA.
- Downstream evaluation is concentrated on short-answer or multiple-choice QA; the paper does not test long-form generation, agentic use, or stronger mathematical reasoning regimes.
- Training-time compute is analyzed explicitly, but inference cost from longer contexts and retrieval search is left for future work.
