## Summary

CR-Planner frames reasoning with retrieval as a planning problem over alternating sub-goal and execution decisions. At each step, a large generator proposes rationales, queries, or retrieved documents, while fine-tuned critic models score both which sub-goal to take next and which sampled execution to keep. The critics are trained from [[monte-carlo-tree-search]] trajectories so they estimate the long-term value of local reasoning and retrieval actions rather than only surface plausibility. Using GPT-4o as the generator, LoRA-tuned reward-model critics, and BM25 retrieval, the method improves competitive programming, theorem-driven math, and reasoning-heavy domain retrieval over CoT, RAG, Chain-of-Knowledge, and Reflexion baselines, suggesting that explicit planning can make reasoning and retrieval mutually corrective instead of mutually brittle.

## Problem & Motivation

The paper targets tasks where both multi-step reasoning and external knowledge matter, but naive combinations of [[chain-of-thought]] and [[retrieval-augmented-generation]] often fail. The authors isolate two coupled failure modes: an early reasoning mistake can corrupt later steps, and a bad query or bad retrieved document can inject irrelevant evidence that derails the whole trajectory. This is especially acute in competitive programming, theorem-driven math, and complex domain retrieval, where the model must decide not only what to think next, but also when retrieval is necessary and which retrieved content is worth trusting.

## Method

- **Planning formulation**: cast solving as an MDP `(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}_s, \mathcal{P}, \mathcal{R}, T)` where state `s_t` is the full action-observation history `(o_0, a_0, ..., a_{t-1}, o_t)` and actions depend on whether the current node is a sub-goal state or an execution state.
- **Sub-goal selection**: a sub-goal critic chooses among `Reason`, `GenQuery`, and `Retrieve` with policy `a_t = \arg\max_{a \in \mathcal{A}_{s_t}} \mathcal{R}(s_t, a)`, explicitly deciding whether to continue reasoning, generate a search query, or retrieve evidence.
- **Execution selection**: once a sub-goal is chosen, CR-Planner samples candidate rationales, queries, or documents and scores them with specialized execution critics `g^e_{\textsc{Rationale}}`, `g^e_{\textsc{Query}}`, and `g^e_{\textsc{Doc}}`; execution-state routing uses a separate sub-goal critic `g^g`.
- **Transitions**: state updates use `f_{gen}` for rationale/query generation, `f_{retr}` for retrieval, and `f_{rule}` for deterministic sub-goal transitions, giving `s_{t+1} = (s_t, a_t, o_{t+1})`.
- **Data collection via [[monte-carlo-tree-search]]**: MCTS uses UCB1 `\frac{v_i}{n_i} + c \sqrt{\frac{\ln n_p}{n_i}}` to balance exploration and exploitation, then backpropagates simulated final-answer rewards so each local action inherits a long-term value signal.
- **Critic training**: from sibling observations under the same parent, the authors build chosen-vs-rejected pairs for each observation type and train separate critics with [[pairwise-ranking-loss]] so they learn preference ordering rather than token generation.
- **Model choices**: the generator is GPT-4 (`gpt-4o-2024-05-13`); critics are LoRA-tuned `Skywork-Reward-Llama-3.1-8B` sequence classifiers, and the first logit is used as the reward score.
- **Sampling and retrieval setup**: during training-data collection and inference, rationale/query execution uses `temperature = 0.7` and samples `3` candidates per step for efficiency; retrieval uses [[bm25]] over task-specific corpora, and final retrieval for StackBio/StackEcon returns top `10` documents for nDCG@10 evaluation.
- **Design intent**: the framework separates a strong general generator from smaller domain-specific critics, making planning plug-and-play across base models without fine-tuning the generator itself.

## Key Results

- **USACO competitive programming**: CR-Planner reaches `17.59` overall Pass@1 versus `10.10` for Standard, `12.38` for CoT, `8.47` for RAG, `8.14` for CoK, and `13.36` for Reflexion.
- **Harder USACO splits**: on Gold and Platinum, CR-Planner gets `14.29` and `14.29`, while CoT gets `4.76` and `0.00`; with Reflexion, CR-Planner further improves to `22.80` overall.
- **TheoremQA-Math**: accuracy rises to `53.40`, compared with `39.81` for Standard, `41.75` for CoT, `44.17` for RAG, and `45.15` for CoK.
- **Reasoning-heavy retrieval**: on StackBio / StackEcon, CR-Planner achieves `29.51` / `22.80` nDCG@10, versus `19.20` / `14.90` for BM25 and `20.82` / `17.45` for CoK.
- **Retrieval matters when guided well**: on USACO, removing retrieval drops performance from `17.59` to `14.33`, showing that retrieval helps only when the planner can control when and how it is used.
- **Transfer across generators**: CR-Planner improves Claude-3.5 from `9.12` to `13.68` and Llama-3.1 from `7.49` to `10.10`, though the gains are smaller than with GPT-4-based trajectories.

## Limitations

- The method depends on high-quality MCTS-generated trajectories and gold-answer supervision; collecting these signals is more expensive than plain prompting.
- Critic transfer is imperfect: critics trained on GPT-4 trajectories help Claude-3.5 and Llama-3.1, but the gains shrink, suggesting generator-specific bias in the learned reward models.
- Retrieval errors are still a live failure mode; the paper explicitly shows that bad queries or irrelevant documents can mislead downstream reasoning.
- Performance saturates around `3` execution samples, implying that critic guidance alone cannot overcome generator or retriever quality ceilings.
- The retrieval stack stays lexical (`[[bm25]]`) and the evaluation focuses on three task families, so the paper does not test whether the same planning recipe remains optimal with stronger retrievers or broader domains.
