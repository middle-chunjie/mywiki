## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature.

Ram et al. propose In-Context Retrieval-Augmented Language Models, a frozen-LM grounding framework that avoids architectural changes by prepending retrieved passages directly to the model input. The paper shows that this simple reading mechanism already yields large perplexity gains across WikiText-103, RealNews, and three The Pile subsets, and that task-aware reranking further improves results. With BM25 retrieval, gains often match moving to a `2x-3x` larger language model; with predictive reranking, a GPT-2 345M system can outperform vanilla GPT-2 1.5B on WikiText-103. The study also extends the same idea to zero-shot open-domain QA, showing that frozen LLaMA models benefit substantially from retrieved documents without LM fine-tuning.

## Problem & Motivation

Prior retrieval-augmented language modeling systems such as RETRO usually modify the LM architecture, add cross-attention-style reading modules, and require retraining, which makes deployment difficult for off-the-shelf or API-only models. This paper asks whether a much simpler design can recover a substantial fraction of RALM gains: keep the autoregressive LM frozen, retrieve relevant passages from an external corpus, and inject them only through the prompt context. The motivation is pragmatic as much as algorithmic: if frozen LMs can be grounded effectively through input concatenation alone, retrieval augmentation becomes cheaper to deploy, easier to study in isolation, and more compatible with existing general-purpose models.

## Method

- **Base formulation**: standard autoregressive modeling uses `p(x_1,...,x_n) = ∏_i p_theta(x_i | x_<i)`; In-Context RALM replaces this with `p(x_i | [R_C(x_<i); x_<i]])`, where the retrieved document is concatenated before the prefix and the LM weights remain frozen.
- **Document reading mechanism**: the framework prepends a single retrieved passage to the LM input with no architectural modification, no adapter, and no further LM training; if the retrieved passage plus prefix exceed the context window, tokens are dropped from the beginning of the prefix to fit the model limit.
- **Retrieval stride**: retrieval can be performed every `s` tokens, giving `p(x) = ∏_j ∏_i p_theta(x_{s·j+i} | [R_C(x_<=s·j); x_<(s·j+i)])`; smaller `s` improves perplexity but increases runtime. The paper uses `s = 4` as the practical default, while noting that more frequent retrieval is better.
- **Retrieval query length**: instead of querying with the entire prefix, the retriever uses the last `l` tokens `q_j^{s,l}`; BM25 works best at `l = 32`, while dense retrievers prefer `l = 64`, showing a contextualization-versus-recency tradeoff.
- **Experimental setup**: all LMs are evaluated with maximum sequence length `1024`; retrieval corpora are chunked into non-overlapping `100`-word passages and truncated to `256` tokens when prepended. For WikiText-103, the dev/test articles are removed from the 2018-12-20 Wikipedia corpus to avoid contamination.
- **Models and retrievers**: generation models include GPT-2 (`110M-1.5B`), GPT-Neo / GPT-J (`1.3B-6B`), OPT (`125M-66B`), and LLaMA (`7B-33B`). Off-the-shelf retrieval compares BM25 against BERT mean pooling, Contriever, and Spider; sparse BM25 is both strongest and cheapest in the zero-shot setting.
- **Zero-shot reranking**: given top-`k = 16` BM25 candidates, the reranker scores documents with LM likelihood over recent prefix tokens `y'`, selecting `argmax_i p_phi(y' | [d_i; x_<=s·j-s'])`; the best setting is `s' = 16`, and even GPT-2 110M works as a reranker for larger GPT-2 generators.
- **Predictive reranking**: a RoBERTa-base classifier learns `p_rank(d_i | x_<=s·j) = exp(f(x,d_i)) / Σ_i' exp(f(x,d_i'))` and is trained with loss `-log Σ_i p_rank(d_i | x_<=s·j) · p_theta(y | [d_i; x_<=s·j])`; training uses `300,000` WikiText-103 examples, `10,000` steps, peak learning rate `1e-5`, and batch size `32`.
- **Downstream QA setting**: for zero-shot [[open-domain-question-answering]], the paper uses [[dpr]] retrieval and prepends the top `2` retrieved documents to frozen LLaMA prompts.

## Key Results

- **BM25 alone gives large LM gains**: on WikiText-103, GPT-2 Small improves from `37.5` to `29.6` word perplexity, GPT-2 Medium from `26.3` to `21.5`, GPT-2 Large from `22.0` to `18.1`, and GPT-2 XL from `20.0` to `16.6`.
- **Reranking adds another layer of gains**: GPT-2 Small goes from `29.6` with BM25 to `28.6` with zero-shot reranking and `26.8` with predictive reranking; GPT-2 XL goes from `16.6` to `16.1` to `15.4`.
- **Equivalent scaling gains**: across corpora, In-Context RALM often matches the performance of a `2x-3x` larger vanilla LM; the paper highlights that GPT-2 345M + predictive reranking beats vanilla GPT-2 1.5B on WikiText-103.
- **Large-model effect persists**: the authors report that BM25-grounded OPT `6.7B` matches vanilla OPT `66B` on WikiText-103 / RealNews trends, and LLaMA-13B improves from `8.5` to `7.6` word perplexity on WikiText-103.
- **BM25 beats dense retrievers**: among off-the-shelf retrieval methods, sparse BM25 outperforms BERT mean pooling, Contriever, and Spider while being cheaper to run.
- **Frozen-LM QA also benefits**: on Natural Questions, LLaMA-13B improves from `12.0` to `31.0` EM with retrieved documents; on TriviaQA it improves from `54.8` to `60.1`. LLaMA-33B rises from `13.7` to `32.3` on NQ and from `58.3` to `62.7` on TriviaQA.

## Limitations

- The reading mechanism is intentionally minimal: it prepends only one passage for LM experiments and never adapts LM weights, so it may underuse retrieval compared with learned reader architectures.
- Better grounding comes from more frequent retrieval, but low `s` increases retriever calls and prefix re-encoding cost, leaving a clear latency-performance tradeoff unresolved.
- Predictive reranking is no longer training-free: it needs target-corpus examples and is trained only on WikiText-103 in the paper.
- Evidence is concentrated on perplexity and zero-shot QA; the paper argues grounding should help factuality, but this work does not provide a broad human evaluation of factual correctness.
- As a 2023 preprint in a fast-moving [[large-language-model]] / retrieval area, its comparisons predate later long-context readers, production RAG systems, and stronger retrievers.
