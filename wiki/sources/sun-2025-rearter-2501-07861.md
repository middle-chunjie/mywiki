## Summary

ReARTeR proposes a retrieval-augmented reasoning framework that combines post-training and test-time scaling for multi-step QA. Its core idea is Trustworthy Process Rewarding: a `Process Reward Model (PRM)` supplies scalar step scores, while a `Process Explanation Model (PEM)` turns low-score steps into natural-language critiques that drive refinement. The paper also tries to make step-level rewards more reliable by balancing PRM training data, reducing early-step bias with a temporal-difference look-ahead update, and aligning PEM explanations with PRM preferences. For open models, ReARTeR further performs step-level offline reinforcement fine-tuning using MCTS-collected preference data. Across five multi-hop benchmarks, it consistently outperforms standard RAG, RAG-CoT baselines, and CR-Planner with both GPT-4o-mini and LLaMA-3.1-8B generators.

## Problem & Motivation

The paper targets a gap between standard retrieval-augmented generation and genuinely strong multi-step reasoning. Existing RAG-CoT pipelines can interleave retrieval and thought generation, but they do not fully exploit inference-time search or post-training optimization. Recent PRM-based methods help with test-time scaling, yet the paper argues that they remain untrustworthy in three ways: scalar scores are not explanatory, PRM training data is biased toward easy examples, and shallow reasoning steps receive noisy reward estimates. In addition, simply plugging an off-the-shelf LLM into RAG leaves reasoning ability under-optimized. ReARTeR is motivated by the claim that RAG needs both reliable step-level credit assignment and explicit optimization of reasoning trajectories.

## Method

- **Framework structure**: the policy `\pi_{\theta}` contains a generator `G` and retriever `E`, while ReARTeR adds a PRM `R` and PEM `C`. Each reasoning step is `e_t = [q_t, j_t, d_t, r_t]`, where `q_t` is a sub-query, `j_t` is a retrieval indicator, `d_t` is retrieved evidence if needed, and `r_t` is the generated thought.
- **Test-time candidate search**: at step `t`, the model samples `M = 3` candidate reasoning steps and scores them with `R(s_t, e_t) \in (0,1)`. The highest-scoring candidate `\hat{e}_t` is accepted directly if `\hat{r}_t > \tau`, with `\tau = 0.5`; otherwise the system enters refinement.
- **Explanation-guided refinement**: when `\hat{r}_t \le 0.5`, PEM generates a critique `c_t = C(s_t, \hat{e}_t, \hat{r}_t)`, and the policy refines the step as `e_t = \pi_{\theta}(s_t \mid \hat{e}_t, c_t, \hat{r}_t)`. The reasoning horizon is capped at `T = 5` steps.
- **PRM supervision construction**: for each question, the method performs `N = 5` rollouts and computes a Monte Carlo score `MC = (1/N) \sum_{n=1}^{N} correct(a_n)`. It uses OmegaPRM-style annotation when `0 < MC < 1`, switches to a stronger generator when `MC = 0`, and discards all-`1` / irrecoverable all-`0` cases because they are non-discriminative.
- **PRM training objective**: the paper binarizes each supervision label as `y_i = 1[MC_i > 0.5]` and trains the PRM with cross-entropy, `L_prm = -(1/M_r)\sum_i [y_i log R(s_i,e_i) + (1-y_i) log(1-R(s_i,e_i))]`.
- **Reducing early-step bias**: for shallow reasoning nodes, ReARTeR applies a TD-style look-ahead update `r_t <- r_t + \alpha (r_{t+1} - r_t)`. The simulation stops when the reward change falls below `\beta = 0.05` or the look-ahead limit `H = 3` is reached.
- **PEM alignment**: PEM is aligned to the PRM via off-policy preference learning. If a refined step improves the PRM score (`r_t^2 > r_t^1`), the explanation is labeled positive; otherwise negative. PEM is then optimized with KTO, using a dynamic loss-aversion weight `\lambda_U = \lambda_0 exp(r_1 - r_2)` for negative examples.
- **Post-training scaling**: for weaker open models, ReARTeR first performs warm-up supervised fine-tuning on strong-generator traces while masking retrieved document tokens `d_t`, then iteratively gathers step-level preferences with [[monte-carlo-tree-search]] and updates the generator with KTO-based offline reinforcement.
- **Implementation details**: experiments use Top-`1` retrieval per reasoning step for iterative methods, Top-`3` retrieval for single-pass baselines, `e5-base-v2` as retriever, `skywork-reward-llama-3.1-8b-v0.2` as the PRM backbone, `Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct` as PEM, and FlashRAG as the experimental framework.

## Key Results

- With `GPT-4o-mini` as generator, ReARTeR reaches `ACC_R / ACC_L = 0.554 / 0.534` on `2WikiMultiHopQA`, `0.496 / 0.544` on `Bamboogle`, `0.468 / 0.506` on `HotpotQA`, `0.296 / 0.302` on `MuSiQue`, and `0.772 / 0.772` on `StrategyQA`.
- Against the strongest explicit test-time baseline `CR-Planner`, ReARTeR improves `ACC_L` by `+0.056` on `2WikiMultiHopQA` (`0.534` vs `0.478`), `+0.020` on `Bamboogle`, `+0.090` on `HotpotQA`, `+0.040` on `MuSiQue`, and `+0.028` on `StrategyQA`.
- With `LLaMA-3.1-8B`, ReARTeR achieves `0.470 / 0.364` on `2WikiMultiHopQA`, `0.438 / 0.484` on `Bamboogle`, `0.424 / 0.434` on `HotpotQA`, `0.244 / 0.252` on `MuSiQue`, and `0.724 / 0.724` on `StrategyQA`, outperforming standard RAG on all five datasets.
- Ablations show every major component matters: removing refinement lowers GPT-4o-mini performance to `0.522 / 0.466` on `2WikiMultiHopQA`; removing TD look-ahead lowers it to `0.524 / 0.490`; replacing PEM with PRM scores alone yields `0.532 / 0.484`.
- The training pipeline is non-trivial in scale: the paper reports `M_r = 167,716` PRM training examples, `M_e = 769` PEM preference examples, `M_w = 548` warm-up examples, and an average of `M_u = 27,822` preference examples per offline-reinforcement iteration.

## Limitations

- The paper is a preprint and the metadata inside `paper.md` still contains placeholder ACM publication boilerplate, so venue-level details are not production-ready.
- Several core components rely on strong external models, including `GPT-4o` for hard PRM data collection and proprietary `GPT-4o-mini` in one evaluation setting, which limits reproducibility and cost transparency.
- Post-training experiments are restricted to `LLaMA-3.1-8B`; the paper does not verify whether the same offline reinforcement pipeline scales cleanly to substantially larger open models.
- The authors report only up to three offline-reinforcement iterations and explicitly note resource constraints, so the claimed scaling trend is suggestive rather than fully established.
- The reported PEM training set size (`M_e = 769`) is much smaller than the PRM dataset, leaving open questions about alignment stability and generalization across domains beyond the five QA benchmarks.
