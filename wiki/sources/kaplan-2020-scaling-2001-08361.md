## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2020; re-verify against recent literature.

Kaplan et al. study how autoregressive Transformer language-model loss scales with non-embedding parameter count, dataset size, and training compute. Using decoder-only models trained on WebText2, they show that cross-entropy follows remarkably stable power laws over multiple orders of magnitude, while depth/width trade-offs matter much less than overall scale. The paper also fits joint laws for overfitting and training dynamics, deriving compute-optimal allocations of model size, data, batch size, and steps. A central conclusion is that, under a fixed compute budget, it is more efficient to train much larger models on comparatively modest data and stop well before convergence. This paper became the canonical empirical basis for later large-language-model scaling work.

## Problem & Motivation

The paper asks which factors dominate language-model performance once architecture is fixed to a modern Transformer family: model size, dataset size, training compute, or finer hyperparameter choices such as width, depth, and head count. The motivation is practical and predictive. If loss obeys smooth regularities, researchers can extrapolate performance, estimate overfitting risk, choose batch sizes, and allocate compute more rationally instead of relying on ad hoc sweeps. The authors also want to understand whether better language modeling primarily comes from architectural novelty or from predictable scaling of parameters, data, and optimization budget.

## Method

- **Model family**: decoder-only Transformer language models on WebText2 with byte-pair encoding, vocabulary `n_vocab = 50257`, and context length `n_ctx = 1024`.
- **Scale sweep**: trained models from `N = 7.68e2` to `1.5e9` non-embedding parameters while varying dataset size, context length, depth, width, feed-forward dimension, and attention heads.
- **Parameterization**: non-embedding model size is approximated as `N ≈ 2 d_model n_layer (2 d_attn + d_ff) = 12 n_layer d_model^2` under the standard choice `d_attn = d_model` and `d_ff = 4 d_model`.
- **Training setup**: default runs use `2.5e5` steps, batch size `512 x 1024 = 2^19` tokens, Adam for most models, Adafactor for models above `1B` parameters, and a learning-rate schedule with `3000` warmup steps followed by cosine decay.
- **Primary scaling laws**: fit `L(N) = (N_c / N)^{alpha_N}`, `L(D) = (D_c / D)^{alpha_D}`, and the joint overfitting law `L(N, D) = [ (N_c / N)^{alpha_N / alpha_D} + D_c / D ]^{alpha_D}`.
- **Training-dynamics law**: model loss at finite training time is fit as `L(N, S_min) = (N_c / N)^{alpha_N} + (S_c / S_min)^{alpha_S}`, where `S_min = S / (1 + B_crit(L) / B)` adjusts observed training to an equivalent large-batch minimum-step regime.
- **Compute model**: training compute is estimated as `C ≈ 6 N B S`; critical batch size is modeled by `B_crit(L) ≈ B_* / L^{1 / alpha_B}` with `B_* ≈ 2e8` tokens and `alpha_B ≈ 0.21`.
- **Compute-optimal allocation**: combine the fitted laws to derive `N ∝ C_min^0.73`, `B ∝ C_min^0.24`, `S_min ∝ C_min^0.03`, and one-epoch data usage `D ∝ C_min^0.27`.
- **Overfitting threshold**: finite-data experiments support `D \gtrsim 5 x 10^3 N^0.74` as a rough regime where overfitting remains small near convergence.

## Key Results

- Loss scales smoothly with model size as `L(N) = (N_c / N)^{alpha_N}` with fitted values `alpha_N ≈ 0.076` and `N_c ≈ 8.8 x 10^13` non-embedding parameters.
- Dataset-limited performance follows `L(D) = (D_c / D)^{alpha_D}` with `alpha_D ≈ 0.095` and `D_c ≈ 5.4 x 10^13` tokens.
- The joint fit to overfitting uses `alpha_N = 0.076`, `alpha_D = 0.103`, `N_c = 6.4 x 10^13`, and `D_c = 1.8 x 10^13`, and empirically supports the rule `D \gtrsim 5 x 10^3 N^0.74`.
- Critical batch size is largely model-size independent at fixed loss and is fit by `B_crit(L) ≈ 2 x 10^8 / L^{1 / 0.21}`; near convergence for the largest runs it is roughly `1-2M` tokens.
- Compute-efficient training scales as `N ∝ C_min^0.73`, `B ∝ C_min^0.24`, and `S_min ∝ C_min^0.03`, so additional compute should mostly buy larger models rather than many more serial steps.
- Appendix-B analysis estimates that training about `10%` above converged loss can reach the same loss with `7.7x` fewer parameter updates, `2.7x` more parameters, and about `65%` less compute than a near-converged (`2%`) regime.

## Limitations

- The scaling laws are empirical fits without a satisfying theoretical derivation; the authors explicitly say they do not know when the laws should break.
- The smallest-data regime is poorly fit: at around `2 x 10^7` tokens an epoch is only about `40` updates, and behavior appears qualitatively different.
- Extrapolation of `B_crit(L)` outside the observed loss range is uncertain, yet it materially affects conclusions about optimal data parallelism and wall-clock time.
- The compute estimate `C ≈ 6 N B S` ignores context-length-dependent terms, so very long-context regimes may not follow the same compute scaling.
- Results are tied to English web text (WebText2) and decoder-only Transformer language models; the paper argues for universality but does not establish it across domains or modalities.
- The conjectured breakdown point around `C* ~ 1e4` PF-days, `N* ~ 1e12`, `D* ~ 1e12`, and `L* ~ 1.7` nats/token is highly uncertain and sensitive to fitted exponents.
