## Summary

This paper introduces Precise Information Control (PIC), a claim-level formulation of faithful long-form generation in which a model must write only from a provided set of verifiable claims. The authors build PIC-Bench, an eight-task benchmark spanning six full-PIC and two partial-PIC settings, and evaluate open and proprietary LMs with automatic claim extraction and claim verification. They then train PIC-LM, an 8B model derived from Llama 3.1 8B Instruct via PIC-formatted supervised fine-tuning and length-normalized direct preference optimization on weakly supervised preference pairs created by dropping context claims. Even under idealized inputs, strong models still omit claims or hallucinate unsupported content frequently; PIC-LM substantially improves PIC metrics and downstream factual generation in retrieval-augmented and self-verification pipelines.

## Problem & Motivation

The paper studies faithfulness hallucination in long-form generation: outputs that introduce information not supported by the supplied input context. The authors argue that this failure mode remains serious even when the user explicitly provides the information that should appear in the answer. Existing factuality work often checks consistency against external knowledge or uses coarse holistic judgments, but that does not directly measure whether a model can obey user-specified information constraints. PIC reframes the problem as exact information control over verifiable claims, asking whether a model can include all and only the intended claims in full-PIC settings, or only a relevant subset in partial-PIC settings. This matters for summarization, biography generation, literature synthesis, and other high-stakes cases where updated contextual evidence should override parametric memory.

## Method

- **PIC task formulation**: each input is an instruction `\mathcal{I}` and a claim set `C = {c_1, ..., c_n}`. The model produces a response whose extracted claim set is `C'`. Full PIC requires exact coverage, while partial PIC requires `C' \subseteq C` with no unsupported additions.
- **Claim-level evaluation**: support is defined as semantic equivalence between a candidate claim and some claim in the source set. The paper measures precision for partial PIC, recall over source claims, and `F_1@K` for full PIC with `K = |C|`.
- **PIC-Bench**: the benchmark contains `1.2K` examples across `8` long-form tasks, with `6` full-PIC tasks and `2` partial-PIC tasks. Representative datasets include EntityBios, PopBios, AskHistorians, ELI5, ExpertQA, FACTS Grounding, and XSUM.
- **Evaluation pipeline**: outputs are decomposed into verifiable claims with an LLM-based claim extractor, then each claim is checked by an LLM-based verifier against the input claims. In the appendix, the implementation uses GPT-4o mini for both extraction and verification.
- **SFT stage**: PIC-LM starts from [[llama-3-1-8b-instruct]] and is supervised fine-tuned on PIC-formatted long-form instruction data drawn from No Robots, FLAN, CNN/DailyMail, biography generation, and long-form QA. After filtering, the SFT mixture contains `2906` full-PIC and `1501` partial-PIC samples. Training uses batch size `128`, learning rate `1e-5`, weight decay `0.1`, and `2` epochs.
- **Preference construction**: for each sample, the method drops a random subset of claims to form `C_perturb`, generates `y_perturb` from the SFT model, and scores original versus perturbed responses with a normalized log-probability drop `z = \sigma((\log p(y_orig^(L) | \mathcal{I}) / L) - (\log p(y_perturb^(L) | \mathcal{I}) / L))` computed on the last `L = 20` tokens.
- **Preference tuning**: the final model is trained with length-normalized DPO using thresholds `\tau = 0.5` for full PIC and `\tau = 0.3` for partial PIC. DPO uses batch size `128`, learning rate `1e-6`, weight decay `0.1`, `\beta = 5`, and `1` epoch.
- **Practical integration**: beyond PIC-Bench, the paper plugs PIC-LM into claim-based RAG and a chain-of-verification pipeline to test whether stronger claim fidelity transfers to downstream factual generation.

## Key Results

- On PIC-Bench, perfect performance remains rare even for strong frontier models: the proportion of perfect outputs stays below `30%` in full PIC and below `70%` in partial PIC.
- Relative to [[llama-3-1-8b-instruct]], PIC-LM raises average full-PIC performance from `69.1%` to `91.0%` `F_1`, and average partial-PIC precision from `73.6%` to `93.3%`.
- PIC-LM beats the strongest open baseline in both settings: `91.0%` vs. `82.5%` average `F_1` in full PIC, and `93.3%` vs. `82.5%` average precision in partial PIC.
- In downstream RAG on [[asqa]], PIC-LM improves exact-match recall from `52.5%` to `61.5%` in the standard regime and reaches `65.9%` in the oracle regime.
- In the self-verification pipeline, PIC-LM raises birthplace factual precision from `65.9%` to `86.0%` and improves [[qampari]] from `13.5%` to `22.6%` `F_1@5`.
- Human validation reports `97.5%` LLM-human agreement for claim-extraction faithfulness, `89.6%` for decontextualization, and `81.0%` agreement for claim verification labels.

## Limitations

- PIC assumes that the input claims are already well formed, non-empty, non-contradictory, and relevant to the instruction; this is a strong prerequisite that often does not hold in raw real-world inputs.
- The benchmark is intentionally focused on tractable claim control and does not cover creative generation, multi-step reasoning, or coding/math-heavy settings.
- Evaluation sets are relatively small, generally under `250` samples per task, because claim extraction and verification are expensive.
- Even PIC-LM does not eliminate faithfulness hallucination: its perfect full-PIC rate is only `28.0%`, so omission and unsupported-claim errors remain common.
- Stronger adherence to user-provided claims can reduce abstention behavior, which the authors note may increase risks when the supplied context is toxic, counterfactual, or otherwise unsafe.
