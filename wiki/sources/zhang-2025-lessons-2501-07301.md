## Summary

This paper is an empirical study of the pitfalls in building Process Reward Models (PRMs) for mathematical reasoning. The authors from Alibaba's Qwen Team identify two major failure modes: (1) Monte Carlo (MC) estimation for step annotation is noisy because it conflates value estimation with correctness labeling, yielding PRMs that score well on Best-of-N (BoN) but fail at actual step-error localization; (2) BoN evaluation alone inflates PRM scores because policy models frequently produce correct-answer-but-wrong-process solutions that tolerant PRMs reward. They propose a consensus filtering mechanism combining MC estimation with LLM-as-a-judge, retaining only ~40% of samples where both methods agree. The resulting Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-7B/72B models achieve state-of-the-art performance on both BoN and the step-level benchmark PROCESSBENCH, with the 7B model reaching 73.5 average F1 vs. 56.5 for the prior best open-source PRM.

## Problem & Motivation

PRMs for mathematical reasoning promise fine-grained process supervision but face two compounding challenges. First, human annotation of reasoning steps is prohibitively expensive, pushing researchers toward automated MC estimation — which the authors find introduces substantial noise by trying to assess current-step correctness via future-outcome probability. Second, the dominant evaluation metric (BoN) does not expose this noise, because PRMs that tolerate flawed-but-correct-answer solutions can score deceptively high. The paper is motivated by negative preliminary results: MC-trained PRMs failed to beat majority voting and badly underperformed human-annotated models on step-error identification, despite using far more data.

## Method

**Data Construction**
- Collected ~500k queries with gold answers; generated 6–8 diverse responses by mixing outputs from `Qwen2-Math-Instruct` and `Qwen2.5-Math-Instruct` (7B and 72B).
- Steps split by `\n\n` delimiter; MC step labels produced by 8 independent completions per step.
- Hard label: step is positive if *any* completion reaches the correct answer (`threshold = 0` is optimal); soft label: proportion of correct completions.
- **LLM-as-a-judge**: same 860k query-response pairs annotated by `Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct` step by step.

**Consensus Filtering**
- Retain instances only where both MC estimation and LLM-as-a-judge agree on error-step locations; ~40% of data is preserved.
- Filtered data achieves comparable PROCESSBENCH performance to full LLM-as-a-judge at 40% of the cost; BoN performance is also maintained.

**Model Architecture**
- Initialized from `Qwen2.5-Math-7B-Instruct` / `Qwen2.5-Math-72B-Instruct` SFT models.
- Original LM head replaced with a scalar-value head (two linear layers).
- Loss: cross-entropy on the last token of each step for binary classification (hard labels).

**Evaluation**
- BoN (`prm@8`): 7 benchmarks (GSM8K, MATH, Minerva Math, GaoKao 2023 En, OlympiadBench, College Math, MMLU STEM) with `Qwen2.5-Math-7B-Instruct` as policy.
- PROCESSBENCH: step-level F1 for identifying first erroneous step (error and correct accuracy reported separately).
- Also evaluates PRM-guided greedy search@8.

**Key Design Insights**
- MC estimates step value (future probability), not step correctness — these are fundamentally different learning targets.
- In BoN, the minimum step score is the effective ranking key; PRMs that concentrate minimum scores on the final answer step are effectively acting as ORMs.
- For MC-trained PRMs, the `last` scoring strategy outperforms `product` / `minimum`; for LLM-as-a-judge or human-annotated PRMs, `product` / `minimum` are preferable.

## Key Results

- **Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-7B BoN avg@8**: `67.6%` vs. best prior open-source PRM `64.9%` (Qwen2.5-Math-7B-PRM800K), and vs. `maj@8 = 66.2%`.
- **Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-72B BoN avg@8**: `69.3%` vs. `Qwen2.5-Math-RM-72B = 68.9%` (ORM baseline).
- **Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-7B PROCESSBENCH avg F1**: `73.5` vs. `56.5` for Qwen2.5-Math-7B-PRM800K (human annotation) and `46.5` for LLM-as-a-judge alone.
- **Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-72B PROCESSBENCH avg F1**: `78.3`, surpassing GPT-4o-0806 (`61.9`) and competing with `o1-mini` (`87.9`).
- Consensus filtering on 860k samples preserves ~340k; this smaller filtered set matches full LLM-as-a-judge on PROCESSBENCH and outperforms on BoN.
- Hard labels after consensus filtering substantially outperform soft labels; soft labels are noisier for MC estimation where correctness should be deterministic.
- ORM `Qwen2.5-Math-RM-72B` surprisingly achieves `38.9` avg F1 on PROCESSBENCH, surpassing many open-source PRMs, highlighting step-decomposition as a simple ORM baseline.

## Limitations

- Significant gap remains between best PRM (prm@8) and oracle (pass@8): avg 69.3% vs. 74.7%, indicating room for improvement.
- Best practices for using PRMs in reinforcement learning (online RL) remain unexplored.
- Efficient utilization of high-quality human annotation data alongside automated methods (e.g., weakly supervised expansion) is under-explored.
- PRM-guided greedy search offers only marginal gains over BoN; better search strategies (DFS with backtracking, combined reward+value models) are left for future work.
- The annotation comparison is limited to mathematical reasoning; generalization to other step-structured domains (e.g., code, science) is not studied.
