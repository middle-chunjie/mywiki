## Summary

The paper proposes NSA, a natively trainable sparse attention mechanism for long-context large language models that combines three branches: compressed coarse tokens, query-dependent selected blocks, and a local sliding window. Its central claim is that sparse attention only becomes practically useful when algorithm design, GPU memory access, and training-time differentiability are co-designed. NSA therefore uses blockwise token selection induced from compression attention scores, shared selections within GQA groups, and Triton kernels that load queries by group and fetch continuous KV blocks. On a 27B-parameter MoE backbone, the method matches or exceeds dense attention on general benchmarks, improves long-context and reasoning evaluations, and reports large efficiency gains that grow with sequence length.

## Problem & Motivation

Standard softmax attention becomes the dominant latency bottleneck in long-context LLMs because its cost grows with sequence length and decoding remains heavily constrained by KV-cache memory access. Prior sparse attention methods often reduce theoretical computation without delivering end-to-end speedups on modern hardware, and many are inference-only because their selection procedures are non-differentiable or impractical to train through.

NSA is motivated by closing both gaps simultaneously. The paper argues that useful sparse attention for real systems must be hardware-aligned across prefilling and decoding, while also remaining trainable enough to replace dense attention during pretraining and downstream adaptation. This leads to a hierarchical design that preserves global context, local precision, and stable gradient flow.

## Method

- **Overall sparse remapping**: replace dense `Attn(q_t, k_:t, v_:t)` with `Attn(q_t, \tilde{K}_t, \tilde{V}_t)`, where branch outputs are combined as ``o_t^* = \sum_{c \in {cmp, slc, win}} g_t^c · Attn(q_t, \tilde{K}_t^c, \tilde{V}_t^c)`` and `g_t^c` is produced by an MLP plus sigmoid gate.
- **Compression branch**: build coarse keys and values by mapping overlapping blocks with learnable compression, using block length ``l = 32`` and stride ``d = 16``. This branch supplies cheap global scanning over the full history.
- **Selection branch**: compute compression attention scores ``p_t^cmp = Softmax(q_t^T \tilde{K}_t^cmp)`` and reuse them to induce block importance scores for finer blocks. NSA selects top-`n` blocks with selected block size ``l' = 64`` and ``n = 16`` active blocks, then concatenates the corresponding original keys and values.
- **Shared sparse selection for GQA/MQA**: when KV caches are shared, block scores are aggregated within a group as ``p_t^slc' = \sum_h p_t^{slc,(h)}``, forcing consistent selection across heads and reducing duplicate KV fetches during decoding.
- **Sliding-window branch**: preserve recent local context with ``\tilde{K}_t^win = k_{t-w:t}``, ``\tilde{V}_t^win = v_{t-w:t}``, and window size ``w = 512``. Separate branches prevent local patterns from shortcutting learning in the compression and selection paths.
- **Backbone and training setup**: experiments use a `27B`-parameter / `3B`-active Transformer-MoE model with `30` layers, hidden size ``2560``, `64` attention heads, `4` GQA groups, ``d_q = d_k = 192``, ``d_v = 128``, and DeepSeekMoE with `72` routed experts, `2` shared experts, and top-`k = 6` routing.
- **Data and adaptation**: both dense and NSA variants are pretrained on ``270B`` tokens of `8k`-length text, then continued on `32k` sequences with YaRN for long-context adaptation. Reasoning post-training uses SFT on ``10B`` tokens of `32k` mathematical traces distilled from DeepSeek-R1.
- **Kernel design**: NSA implements sparse selection kernels in Triton using group-centric query loading, continuous sparse KV block fetches, and an inner loop over selected blocks where kernel block size satisfies ``B_k | l'``. Compression and sliding-window branches remain compatible with FlashAttention-2 style kernels.

## Key Results

- On general benchmarks, NSA improves average score from `0.443` to `0.456` over Full Attention, outperforming dense attention on `7/9` metrics; examples include GSM8K `0.520` vs `0.486` and DROP `0.545` vs `0.503`.
- On LongBench, NSA reaches the best average score `0.469`, beating Full Attention `0.437` and Exact-Top `0.423`; notable gains include HPQ `0.437` vs `0.350`, 2Wiki `0.356` vs `0.305`, PassR-en `0.905` vs `0.830`, and LCC `0.232` vs `0.163`.
- On 64k-context needle-in-a-haystack retrieval, the paper reports perfect accuracy across all positions.
- On AIME 24 after reasoning SFT, NSA-R outperforms Full Attention-R at both context limits: `0.121` vs `0.046` at `8k`, and `0.146` vs `0.092` at `16k`.
- Efficiency grows with context length: at `64k`, NSA reports up to `9.0x` forward and `6.0x` backward speedup over Triton FlashAttention-2, and decoding analysis estimates `11.6x` speedup with effective token access reduced from `65536` to `5632`.

## Limitations

- The paper is an arXiv preprint rather than a peer-reviewed conference version, so some empirical claims have not yet been externally vetted.
- Most experiments use one `27B`-parameter backbone; cross-architecture evidence remains limited despite discussion of MQA/GQA compatibility.
- The decoding section reports expected speedup from memory-access reduction, but does not provide the same breadth of end-to-end serving benchmarks as the training and prefilling analysis.
- Several baseline sparse methods are inference-only, so reasoning post-training comparisons are limited to Full Attention rather than a broader competitive set.
- The paper is internally inconsistent about pretraining scale, mentioning `260B` tokens in the introduction but `270B` tokens in the detailed setup.
