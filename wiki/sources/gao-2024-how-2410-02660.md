## Summary

This paper studies how to extend a strong short-context base model into a practically useful long-context assistant through continued training and supervised fine-tuning, using realistic downstream evaluation rather than perplexity or needle-in-a-haystack tests. Starting from Llama-3-8B, the authors ablate data composition, sequence length, RoPE scaling, document masking, initialization, and SFT data choice, then assemble the best recipe into ProLong. The resulting `8B` model is trained on `40B` tokens, supports up to `512K` context, and achieves the best HELMET average among roughly `10B`-scale open models at `128K`. A central claim is that robust long-context performance depends on the interaction between continued pretraining, post-SFT evaluation, and carefully balanced short/long data mixtures rather than on long data alone.

## Problem & Motivation

Open long-context model development often relies on weak proxies such as perplexity or synthetic recall tests, which can hide failures on realistic tasks like retrieval-augmented generation, re-ranking, long-document QA, and summarization. The paper asks how to continue training a short-context pretrained LM so that it actually uses long context effectively while preserving short-context capabilities. The motivation is practical: long-context adaptation is expensive, design choices are poorly understood, and prior recipes can degrade base-model quality or overfit to simplistic benchmarks. The authors therefore build a development protocol around diverse downstream evaluations after SFT, then use it to determine what data, lengths, masking strategies, and instruction-tuning setups genuinely improve end-task long-context behavior.

## Method

- **Evaluation protocol**: model development is guided by HELMET-style downstream tasks instead of perplexity or NIAH, and ablations are evaluated after SFT because gains on RAG, re-ranking, QA, and summarization can appear only post-SFT.
- **Long-context continued training**: the final recipe uses `30%` code repositories, `30%` books, `3%` textbooks, and `37%` ShortMix, where ShortMix itself is `27%` FineWeb-Edu, `27%` FineWeb, `11%` Wikipedia, `11%` StackExchange, `8%` Tulu-v2, `8%` OpenWebMath, and `8%` ArXiv.
- **Length curriculum**: Stage 1 trains for `20B` tokens at `64K`; Stage 2 trains for another `20B` tokens at `512K`, mixing `512K` and `64K` long documents to balance availability and cost.
- **Model and positional scaling**: initialization is `[[llama-3-8b-instruct]]`; RoPE base is increased from the original `5 x 10^5` to `8 x 10^6` for `64K` and `1.28 x 10^8` for `512K` to support position extrapolation under continued training.
- **Optimization**: continued training uses AdamW with weight decay `0.1`, `beta_1 = 0.9`, `beta_2 = 0.95`, learning rate `1e-5`, warmup `10%`, cosine decay to `1e-6`, and batch sizes of `4M` then `8M` tokens; Stage 1 and Stage 2 require about `2.2K` and `12.2K` H100 hours respectively.
- **Attention and systems choices**: the recipe applies cross-document attention masking (`document masks`) and uses [[sequence-parallelism]] only when `512K` sequences require distributed attention.
- **SFT**: the final instruction-tuning stage uses only [[ultrachat]] for `1B` tokens with AdamW, learning rate `2e-5`, cosine decay to `2e-6`, warmup `5%`, and batch size `4M` tokens; synthetic long instruction data is tested but not adopted.

## Key Results

- On long-data source ablations, a `50/50` books-plus-code mix achieves the best average long-context score in Table 4: `54.6`, versus `53.8` for books only and `52.3` for code repos only.
- Training only on long data is suboptimal: the paper adopts a `60%` long / `40%` short ratio because more long data hurts short-context performance and lowers post-SFT long-context averages.
- Switching extra training from `64K` to `512K` helps even when evaluation stays at `64K`: recall improves from `95.0` to `98.5`, RAG from `56.4` to `56.9`, and re-rank from `28.0` to `32.9`.
- For SFT, adding synthetic long instruction data is harmful in this setup: average score drops from `55.7` at `0%` synthetic data to `54.1` at `1%`, `53.5` at `3%`, and `43.3` at `50%`.
- Final HELMET results at `128K` show `[[prolong]]` reaches `49.4` average, beating `[[llama-3-1-8b-instruct]]` at `46.5` and MegaBeam-Mistral at `45.4`, while using only `40B` long-context training tokens versus Llama-3.1's reported `800B`.
- The final model scales beyond `128K`: QA rises from `31.7` at `32K` to `49.7` at `512K`, and summarization rises from `40.4` to `42.1`.

## Limitations

- The study is limited to the `~10B` parameter regime and mostly to the Llama-3 family, so some conclusions may not transfer to larger or architecturally different models.
- The authors do not exhaustively ablate all optimization hyperparameters or alternative data mixtures because of compute constraints.
- Model development is guided by a fixed subset of long-context tasks, creating some risk of benchmark-specific tuning even though the authors validate on held-out HELMET tasks, NoCha, RULER, and InfinityBench.
- The recipe is computationally expensive: the `512K` stage alone costs roughly `12.2K` H100 hours, which limits accessibility for many open practitioners.
- Absolute performance is still uneven across tasks: for example, ProLong's HELMET re-rank score is only `22.5`, far below strong closed models such as GPT-4o (`50.0`) and Gemini-1.5-Pro (`59.7`).
