## Summary

The paper studies whether dense retrieval exhibits neural scaling laws analogous to those observed in large language models. It proposes contrastive perplexity, a continuous metric defined over one relevant passage and sampled negatives, to avoid the discontinuities of ranking metrics such as NDCG and MAP. Using 24 English BERT checkpoints from `0.6M` to `87M` non-embedding parameters and Chinese ERNIE variants on MS MARCO Passage Ranking and T2Ranking, the authors fit precise power-law relationships for both model size and annotation count. They further examine annotation quality via ICT, docT5query, and ChatGLM3-generated queries, then combine the fitted laws with annotation, training, and inference cost models to analyze budget allocation. The main claim is that dense retrieval scaling is predictable enough to guide experiment design and resource planning.

## Problem & Motivation

Dense retrieval models are increasingly central to first-stage retrieval, but their training depends on costly human relevance annotations and is usually evaluated with discrete ranking metrics that are too jagged for scaling-law analysis. The paper argues that this makes it hard to disentangle the effects of model size and data size, and therefore hard to predict when extra parameters or annotations are worth the cost. The goal is to build a smoother evaluation signal and derive empirical scaling curves that can support extrapolation, annotation planning, and budget-aware system design.

## Method

- **Dense retrieval setup**: encode query and passage into a shared vector space and score them by inner product, `` `s(q, p) = <f(q; theta), f(p; theta)>` ``.
- **Encoder family**: fine-tune pre-trained encoder-only Transformers with a projection layer, `` `v = W · Transformer(x; phi) + b` ``, standardizing output dimensionality to `768`.
- **Model scale sweep**: use `24` English BERT checkpoints from `0.6M` (`BERT-Tiny`) to `87M` (`BERT-Base`) non-embedding parameters, and Chinese ERNIE variants for T2Ranking.
- **Datasets**: MS MARCO Passage Ranking has `8.8M` passages, about `0.5M` training queries, and about `7,000` validation queries; T2Ranking has `300k+` queries and `2M+` unique passages.
- **Training loss**: optimize contrastive ranking loss with random negatives and in-batch negatives, `` `L(theta) = -(1/B) sum_i log( exp(s(q_i, p_i+)) / (exp(s(q_i, p_i+)) + sum_j exp(s(q_i, p_j-))) )` ``.
- **Training hyperparameters**: train every model for a fixed `10,000` steps and sample `256` negatives at each step, intentionally avoiding sophisticated hard-negative mining to reduce confounds in scaling analysis.
- **Evaluation metric**: define contrastive perplexity over `W = 256` sampled negatives, `` `L(W; theta) = -log( exp(s(q_i, p_i+)) / (exp(s(q_i, p_i+)) + sum_{j=1}^W exp(s(q_i, p_j-))) )` ``, and show it correlates strongly with standard IR metrics, especially recall@1000.
- **Scaling laws**: fit model-size scaling as `` `L(N) = (A / N)^alpha + delta_N` `` and data-size scaling as `` `L(D) = (B / D)^beta + delta_D` ``, where non-zero asymptotes capture incomplete binary annotations and false negatives.
- **Joint scaling + cost model**: on MS MARCO, fit `` `L(N, D) = [ (A / N)^(alpha / beta) + B / D ]^beta + delta` `` with `A ≈ 8.2e5`, `B ≈ 5.2e3`, `alpha ≈ 0.57`, `beta ≈ 1.39`, `delta ≈ 0.03`, then analyze total cost as `` `Z(N, D) = Z_data · D + Z_train · N + Z_infer · N` ``.
- **Budget assumptions**: use `Z_data ≈ 0.6` dollars per annotated pair, `Z_train ≈ 3.22e-8`, and `Z_infer ≈ 0.43`, assuming single-`A100 80G`, `25%` utilization, query length `30`, passage length `60`, and inference over `30e12` indexed web pages with `512` tokens each.

## Key Results

- **Model-size scaling** fits are strong: on MS MARCO, `` `A = 4.54e4` ``, `` `alpha = 0.46` ``, `` `delta_N = 0.02` ``, `` `R^2 = 0.990` ``; on T2Ranking, `` `A = 9.88e6` ``, `` `alpha = 0.53` ``, `` `delta_N = 0.14` ``, `` `R^2 = 0.999` ``.
- **Data-size scaling** is also close to log-linear: on MS MARCO, `` `B = 2.37e-2` ``, `` `beta = 0.19` ``, `` `delta_D = 0.01` ``, `` `R^2 = 0.971` ``; on T2Ranking, `` `B = 6.04e4` ``, `` `beta = 0.50` ``, `` `delta_D = 0.15` ``, `` `R^2 = 0.991` ``.
- **Annotation-quality scaling** persists across weak and synthetic supervision: ICT has the shallowest slope, docT5query is better, and ChatGLM3-generated queries yield the steepest slope among generated annotations.
- **Synthetic data finding**: the ChatGLM3 curve is steeper than the human-annotation curve, suggesting sufficiently large high-quality LLM-generated query data could outperform purely human-labeled training under this setup.
- **Budget analysis** predicts a regime change: without inference cost, the optimal model size can exceed `13B` parameters as budget rises; after adding corpus encoding cost, the optimum collapses to million-scale models.
- **Practical implication**: scaling curves derived from smaller runs can be extrapolated to estimate the value of more parameters or annotations before paying the full training and labeling cost.

## Limitations

- The experiments only cover BERT-series and ERNIE-style encoder models, with parameter scales topping out at `87M`, so the claimed laws are not validated for billion-parameter dense retrievers.
- Training is deliberately simplified to fixed `10,000` steps, random negatives, and in-batch negatives; stronger training recipes could change the fitted exponents.
- Contrastive perplexity is smoother than NDCG or MAP, but it is still a proxy metric built from sampled negatives rather than the end retrieval objective itself.
- Annotation-quality experiments and the joint budget-allocation fit are only reported on MS MARCO, so cross-dataset robustness is incomplete.
- The cost model uses rough assumptions about annotation price, GPU efficiency, token counts, and web-scale corpus size, so its recommended optima should be treated as approximate planning guidance rather than hard prescriptions.
