## Summary

MTRAG introduces a human-generated benchmark for end-to-end evaluation of multi-turn [[retrieval-augmented-generation]] systems. The benchmark contains `110` conversations spanning Wikipedia, finance, government, and technical-documentation corpora, averaging `7.7` turns and yielding `842` turn-level tasks. Its design explicitly stresses later-turn retrieval, non-standalone questions, passage diversity, and unanswerable cases, all of which are common in realistic conversational search but under-covered in prior benchmarks. The paper evaluates lexical, dense, and sparse retrievers plus `9` large models, shows that both retrieval and generation degrade noticeably on later turns and unanswerables, and complements the human benchmark with automatic metrics, human evaluation, and a synthetic companion set `mtRAG-S` for studying scalable evaluation paths.

## Problem & Motivation

Most existing RAG benchmarks are single-turn or keep retrieval fixed across a conversation, so they fail to stress the repeated retrieval, contextual interpretation, and answerability handling required in realistic multi-turn settings. The paper argues that a useful benchmark should expose changes in relevant passages across turns, include non-standalone follow-up questions, preserve long-form answer quality, and explicitly test unanswerable requests. mtRAG is proposed to fill this gap with human-authored conversations created while interacting with a live RAG system, making the benchmark suitable for evaluating the full retrieval-and-generation pipeline rather than isolated components.

## Method

- **Benchmark composition**: mtRAG contains `110` accepted conversations across `4` domains (`CLAPnq`, `FiQA`, `Govt`, `Cloud`), with `842` turn-level tasks and average conversation length `7.7` turns.
- **Corpus preparation**: documents are chunked into passages of `512` tokens with overlap stride `100`, then indexed with [[elser]] on [[elasticsearch]] for annotation-time retrieval and downstream experiments.
- **Conversation creation loop**: annotators use a custom chat application backed by `ELSERv1` retrieval and [[mixtral-8x7b-instruct]] generation; at each turn they write a question, inspect and edit retrieved passages, repair the response, and annotate dimensions.
- **Targeted benchmark properties**: questions are diversified by question type, multi-turn type, answerability, and domain; annotators are guided to produce `6-9` turns per conversation, passage diversity, and natural conversational flow.
- **Quality controls**: benchmark answers are optimized for FANC properties (faithfulness, appropriateness, naturalness, completeness); `92%` of turns require repair, and the pre-review edit score is `60.7` Rouge-L, indicating the tasks are non-trivial for the baseline agent.
- **Retrieval evaluation**: the paper compares `BM25`, `BGE-base 1.5`, and `Elser` under `2` strategies: last-turn retrieval and [[query-rewriting]] of the current turn into a standalone query. Metrics are Recall and nDCG at `@1`, `@3`, `@5`, and `@10`.
- **Generation evaluation**: `9` LLMs are tested with up to `N = 5` passages under three retrieval settings named in the paper: `reference`, `reference+RAG`, and `full RAG`; `full RAG` uses Elser with query rewrite.
- **Automatic metrics**: aggregate quality is measured with ``RB_alg = HM(Bert-Rec, Bert-K-Prec, Rouge-L)`` and an [[llm-as-a-judge]] score `RB_llm`; faithfulness is measured with `RL_F` from RAGAS.
- **Answerability conditioning**: for score `φ ∈ {RB_alg, RB_llm, RL_F}`, the metric is conditioned on answerability `A` and IDK prediction `IDK`, assigning `φ` when answerability is handled correctly, `0` for missed abstentions, and `1` for correct abstention on unanswerable items.
- **Human and synthetic extensions**: a human evaluation studies `159` tasks with three annotators each, and a synthetic companion benchmark `mtRAG-S` is generated with Mixtral `8x22B` for comparison.

## Key Results

- The benchmark contains `110` conversations, `842` tasks, `16.9` unique relevant passages per conversation on average, and `1.3` co-referential questions per conversation.
- On retrieval, Elser with query rewriting is best: `R@5 = 0.52` and `nDCG@5 = 0.48`, versus `BM25` query-rewrite `R@5 = 0.25` and `BGE-base 1.5` query-rewrite `R@5 = 0.37`.
- Retrieval difficulty rises sharply after the first turn: with Elser + rewrite, `R@5` drops from `0.89` on turn `1` to `0.47` on later turns; non-standalone later turns score `0.42`.
- Human-created reference answers remain much stronger than model outputs in generation: under the reference setting, the reference scores `RL_F = 0.86`, `RB_llm = 0.94`, and `RB_alg = 0.88`.
- Strong frontier models still lag substantially: under the same setting, GPT-4o reaches `RL_F = 0.69`, `RB_llm = 0.73`, and `RB_alg = 0.46`; Llama `3.1 405B` reaches `0.69`, `0.70`, and `0.47`.
- Human evaluation on `159` tasks shows the reference answer wins `59.3%` of pairwise comparisons, versus `47.8%` for GPT-4o and `47.4%` for Llama `3.1 405B`; reference FANC harmonic mean is `3.8` vs `3.6` for both frontier models.
- Automatic evaluation remains imperfect: weighted Spearman correlation with human win-rate is `0.33` for `RB_llm`, `0.24` for `RB_alg`, and only `0.01` for `RL_F`.
- The synthetic companion benchmark is easier and less diverse: `mtRAG-S` has `200` conversations and `1,181` tasks, but only `5.9` turns and `4.6` unique passages per conversation on average.

## Limitations

- The benchmark covers only `4` domains and `110` conversations, so it is broad enough to surface multi-turn failures but still limited as a universal coverage benchmark.
- Retrieval experiments may be biased toward Elser because the same retriever is used during data creation and passage indexing.
- The extracted markdown does not fully render the prose for the middle generation condition (`reference+RAG`), so some implementation detail is less explicit than for `reference` and `full RAG`.
- Human evaluation is careful but small (`159` tasks, `2` frontier models), which limits the granularity of conclusions about model ranking.
- The paper itself shows that current automatic metrics correlate only weakly to moderately with human judgment, so the benchmark still depends on costly human assessment for high-confidence comparison.
- The synthetic benchmark is intentionally approximate and does not yet match the human benchmark on passage diversity, turn depth, or unanswerable-case realism.
