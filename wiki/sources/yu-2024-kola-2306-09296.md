## Summary

KoLA proposes a world-knowledge benchmark for large language models that prioritizes careful benchmark design over merely broader task coverage. The benchmark organizes evaluation into a four-level cognitive taxonomy, mixes Wikipedia-based known data with continuously refreshed evolving corpora, and introduces a contrastive evaluation system that combines cross-task standardized scores with a self-contrast metric for knowledge-creating tasks. Across its first two seasons, KoLA evaluates 28 open-source and commercial models and reports several diagnostic patterns: larger unaligned models memorize more knowledge, alignment improves higher-level abilities but can hurt memorization, and commercial systems remain stronger overall. The benchmark is designed as an evolving diagnostic resource with quarterly updates.

## Problem & Motivation

The paper argues that contemporary LLM benchmarks often expand breadth without enough attention to diagnostic structure, fairness under undisclosed training data, or comparability across heterogeneous metrics. KoLA targets world knowledge because it is central to LLM usefulness, but the authors want deeper analysis than broad exam-style coverage: they explicitly model a hierarchy of knowledge abilities, distinguish known from newly emerging data, and redesign evaluation metrics so practitioners can compare models more directly and inspect where failures arise.

## Method

- **Ability taxonomy**: KoLA organizes world-knowledge evaluation into four levels inspired by Bloom-style cognition: [[knowledge-memorization]], [[knowledge-understanding]], [[knowledge-applying]], and [[knowledge-creating]]. The full suite contains `19` tasks split as `3 + 8 + 6 + 2`.
- **Known vs. evolving data**: the known source is Wikipedia, linked through [[wikidata5m]] to the 2019 Wikipedia dump; the evolving source crawls content from roughly the most recent `90` days and builds a new season every `3` months with at least `500` newly collected articles.
- **Task construction**: test sizes are typically `100` examples per task, but KoLA uses `300` examples for FewNERD, `199` for MAVEN-ERE, `49` for ETA, and `95` examples for each knowledge-creating task. The benchmark pairs known and evolving tasks within each level to compare memorized versus unseen knowledge.
- **Standardized overall scoring**: for model `m_j` on task `d_i`, KoLA computes `z_ij = (x_ij - mu(x_i1, ..., x_i|M|)) / sigma(x_i1, ..., x_i|M|)` and rescales to `s_ij = 100 * (z_ij - min(z)) / (max(z) - min(z))` so scores from heterogeneous tasks become more comparable.
- **Self-contrast for knowledge creation**: given context `C`, human continuation `R`, and annotated foreknowledge `K`, each model generates a free completion `T` and a knowledge-grounded completion `T_k`. The overall creating score is `x = avg(partial(T, R), partial(T, T_k), partial(T_k, R))`, where `partial` is Rouge-L F1.
- **Inference and normalization**: open-source models are deployed with PyTorch `2.0.0`, transformers `4.28.1`, Python `3.10.0`, CUDA `11.4`, and `8 x NVIDIA A100 SXM 80GB` GPUs. Task-specific adaptation uses `temperature` in `{0, 0.2, 1}`, `max_tokens = 20-256`, `max_train = 4-5`, and relaxed F1 with GPT2Tokenizer for open-ended answers.

## Key Results

- KoLA evaluates `28` models across the first two seasons; on the Season 2 overall leaderboard, `GPT-4` ranks first with average standardized score `2.33`, followed by `GPT-3.5-turbo` at `1.34` and `InstructGPT davinci v2` at `1.12`.
- For unaligned models, the ranking on [[knowledge-memorization]] has Spearman correlation `0.79` with model size, supporting the claim that larger base models memorize more seen knowledge.
- After instruction tuning, the correlation between higher-level ability and model size rises substantially; for [[knowledge-applying]], the paper reports a jump from `0.02` to `0.53`, while the size correlation for memorization falls to `0.34`, consistent with an alignment tax.
- The proposed [[self-contrast-metric]] shows Spearman correlation `0.61` with human-judged faithfulness on knowledge-creating outputs, and removing it from the aggregate creating metric causes a `32%` drop in correlation with human overall-quality judgments.
- Open-source models have average `z-score = -0.29`, below the overall average, and their relative rankings tend to decline from Season 1 to Season 2 versus continuously updated commercial systems.

## Limitations

- The evolving evaluation is annotation-heavy: each season requires at least `500` newly collected articles, and only one newly annotated evolving task per level is added, limiting coverage.
- Most non-evolving tasks remain Wikipedia-based and English-centric, so benchmark conclusions may underrepresent broader or multilingual world knowledge.
- The standardized score depends on the current model pool, and the handling of `-` / `N/A` results can distort overall rankings because missing task scores become `0` at level aggregation time.
- Knowledge-creating evaluation is still based on Rouge-L over paired generations rather than direct semantic verification, so it only approximates faithfulness and novelty.
- Several tasks are sensitive to context limits and instruction-following failures, meaning benchmark scores partly reflect engineering constraints rather than pure knowledge ability.
