## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023 in a volatile LLM-efficiency domain; re-verify against recent literature.

H2O (Heavy-Hitter Oracle) addresses the KV-cache memory bottleneck in autoregressive LLM decoding. The key empirical insight is that attention score matrices are over 95% sparse at inference time, and accumulated attention scores follow a power-law distribution — a small subset of tokens ("heavy hitters", H2) receive disproportionately large cumulative attention. H2O retains a fixed-size cache of these heavy hitters plus the most recent tokens, evicting the lowest-scoring token at each step via a greedy algorithm formalized as dynamic submodular maximization. With only 20% KV-cache budget, H2O achieves comparable task accuracy to full-cache models across OPT, LLaMA, and GPT-NeoX on diverse benchmarks, while improving generation throughput by up to 29× over DeepSpeed/Accelerate and 3× over FlexGen on OPT-6.7B/30B.

## Problem & Motivation

LLM deployment is bottlenecked not only by model parameter size but increasingly by the KV cache, which stores all past key/value embeddings to avoid recomputation during autoregressive generation. The cache grows linearly with sequence length and batch size: a 30B-parameter model with batch size 128 and sequence length 1024 requires roughly 180 GB of KV cache alone. Existing sparse attention approximations (Reformer, Sparse Transformer) were designed to reduce quadratic training cost but still require large caches; multi-query attention and gisting tokens require model modifications or expensive fine-tuning. A practical eviction policy that can be applied to pre-trained models without retraining was lacking.

## Method

**Core observation — attention sparsity and heavy hitters:**
- Inference-time attention matrices of pre-trained OPT models are >95% sparse across nearly all layers (threshold: 1% of the per-row max value).
- Accumulated per-token attention scores across all decoding steps follow a power-law distribution; the heavy-hitter set H2 is small but critical — removing it causes severe accuracy collapse.
- H2 tokens correlate strongly with high-frequency co-occurrence in the training corpus.

**H2O eviction policy (Algorithm 1):**
- Cache budget `k` is partitioned evenly between H2 tokens and the most recent tokens.
- At each decoding step `i`, the new token is added to candidate set `G_i = S_{i-1} ∪ {i}`.
- The score function is `F_score(T) = Σ_{s∈T} o_s` where `o_s` is the normalized attention score of token `s` accumulated over preceding steps.
- Eviction: `u = argmax_{v∈G_i} F_score(G_i \ {v})` — remove the token whose absence maximally preserves total score.
- Result: `S_i = G_i \ {u}`, maintaining `|S_i| = k`.

**Theoretical guarantee:**
- The paper formalizes the eviction problem as dynamic submodular maximization (Definition 4.1).
- Theorem 4.4 (informal): under mild submodularity assumptions, the greedy eviction gives `f(S̃_i) ≥ (1−α)(1−1/e) max_{|S|=k} f(S) − β` for small parameters α, β > 0, providing a near-optimal approximation ratio analogous to the classical submodular maximization guarantee.

**System implementation:**
- Built on FlexGen; does not swap evicted KV memory but fills in place with newly added KV to maintain I/O efficiency.
- Orthogonal to quantization: combining H2O with 4-bit KV quantization (OPT-30B) further improves accuracy-efficiency trade-off.
- Supports infinite-length streaming generation (sequence lengths up to 4 million tokens in testing) by combining H2 retention with position rolling, outperforming StreamLLM on perplexity at the same cache size.

## Key Results

- **Accuracy (20% KV budget):** On OPT-30B across COPA, OpenBookQA, PiQA, Winogrande, H2O scores 84/43/78.45/69.06 vs Full 85/43.2/78.51/70.24 — within ~1% of full-cache performance. "Local" (recency-only) baseline collapses to 48/25.2/55.82/49.17.
- **Sparse Transformer boost:** Adding H2 to strided/fixed Sparse Transformer baselines closes the ~35% accuracy gap vs full cache under 20% budget.
- **Throughput (T4 GPU, OPT-6.7B, 512+512 tokens):** H2O 51.7 tok/s vs Accelerate 15.5 / DeepSpeed 9.6 / FlexGen 16.8 → up to 29× speedup.
- **Throughput (T4, OPT-30B, 512+32):** H2O 12.7 tok/s vs FlexGen 8.1 tok/s (FlexGen requires CPU offloading; H2O keeps 728-sample batch in GPU).
- **Latency (A100, OPT-6.7B, 2048+2048, batch 24):** H2O 53.5 s vs FlexGen 99.5 s → 1.9× reduction.
- **Throughput (A100, OPT-6.7B, batch 64):** H2O 1161 tok/s; FlexGen OOM.
- **Infinite-length streaming:** H2O achieves lower perplexity than StreamLLM across cache sizes up to 4M-token input on PG-19.
- **Compatibility with quantization:** H2O + quantization on OPT-30B jointly outperforms either method alone on COPA, OpenBookQA, PiQA.
- **Diversity bonus:** H2O-generated text exhibits fewer repeated tokens compared to full-cache models.

## Limitations

- The heavy-hitter identification uses accumulated local attention scores as a proxy; this greedy heuristic is near-optimal only under submodularity assumptions that may not strictly hold in all LLM architectures.
- Fixed 50/50 split between H2 and recent tokens is a heuristic; optimal allocation likely varies by task, layer, and model family.
- Evaluated on OPT, LLaMA (1), and GPT-NeoX; generalization to more recent architectures with grouped-query attention (GQA) or sliding-window attention is not studied.
- The OPT models used are pre-trained on sequences up to length 2048; long-sequence (>2048) throughput benchmarks are extrapolations.
- Does not address the prompt-phase (pre-filling) bottleneck, only the autoregressive generation phase.
