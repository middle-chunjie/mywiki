## Summary

This paper introduces DR Tulu-8B, an open `8B` deep-research agent trained directly for long-form, citation-grounded report generation, and proposes Reinforcement Learning with Evolving Rubrics (RLER) as its core training method. Instead of relying on static, closed-book rewards or short-form RLVR proxies, RLER builds question-specific rubric buffers from search results and continually updates them using on-policy rollouts, so the verifier tracks newly explored evidence and emerging failure modes. DR Tulu combines a `16K`-example SFT cold start, online GRPO with asynchronous tool calls, and an MCP-based multi-tool backend. The resulting model substantially outperforms prior open deep-research systems, reaches `63.7` average score across four long-form benchmarks, and is far cheaper per query than proprietary deep-research systems while preserving citation quality.

## Problem & Motivation

Existing open deep-research systems are usually either prompt-engineered pipelines or models trained with Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards on short-form QA tasks that only approximate real deep research. The paper argues that this proxy breaks for long-form, open-ended tasks because answer quality is under-specified, depends on broad external knowledge, and requires faithful citation support rather than easily checkable exact answers. Static general rubrics and closed-book question-specific rubrics are also too vague or too stale to keep pace with a policy that is actively exploring the web and papers. The motivation behind RLER is therefore to make the verifier adaptive, search-grounded, and on-policy, so RL can optimize realistic long-form research behavior instead of gaming brittle surrogate rewards.

## Method

- **Deep-research formulation**: the policy `pi_theta` operates over action types `{think, tool, answer, cite}` and a tool set `T = {T_1, T_2, ...}`; a tool action executes `o_i = T_k(q_i; a_i)` and appends the observation to the next state.
- **Rubric reward**: for question `x`, answer `y`, and rubric set `R_x = {(r_{x,k}, w_{x,k})}`, the main score is `S(x, y) = (sum_k w_{x,k} * JUDGE(r_{x,k}, y)) / (sum_{k:w_{x,k}>0} w_{x,k})`, where the judge returns `0`, `0.5`, or `1` per rubric.
- **Persistent search-based rubrics**: before RL, the system runs `SEARCH(x)` and builds `R_x^persist = G_rubric(x, SEARCH(x))`, so each prompt starts with externally grounded rubric items rather than closed-book criteria.
- **Evolving rubrics**: at each step, the current policy samples rollouts `{y_i}_{i=1}^G`, and the rubric generator produces `R_x^new = G_rubric(x, {y_i}, R_x)` from the prompt, rollout traces, and existing rubric pool. The generator emits both positive rubrics (new strengths or uncovered knowledge) and negative rubrics (reward-hacking or failure patterns).
- **Rubric buffer management**: active rubrics with zero reward variance across rollouts are discarded; the remaining ones are ranked by reward standard deviation and only the top `K_max = 5` evolving rubrics are retained per prompt.
- **Auxiliary rewards**: RLER adds citation, search, and format rewards. The search reward caps at `1.0` after `3` valid searches; the format reward is `0.5 a(y) + 0.3 c(y) + 0.2 q(y)` for answer, citation, and query structure; citation reward combines per-claim support and citation-format validity.
- **SFT cold start**: DR Tulu first learns planning, tool use, and citation style from `16K` teacher-generated trajectories. Prompts come from SearchArena and Open-Scholar plus a moderate short-form QA mixture; GPT-5 generates full trajectories with mock thinking traces, and rejection sampling filters malformed or wrong examples.
- **RL data and optimizer**: online RL uses approximately `5K` new long-form prompts from SearchArena and OpenScholar plus `4K` prompts from RaR. Training uses GRPO with group size `8`, batch `32` prompts, `1` minibatch, `1` inner epoch, max prompt length `2048`, max response length `16384`, max packed length `18500`, max tool calls `10`, `lr = 5e-7`, AdamW betas `(0.9, 0.95)`, and KL penalty `0.001`.
- **Asynchronous rollout execution**: generation and tool latency are overlapped by sending tool calls immediately when triggered, putting the rollout to sleep while other rollouts continue. Tool outputs are masked from loss, and citation reward is disabled after `650` RL steps because it had converged and was slowing training.
- **Infrastructure**: the `dr-agent-lib` backend exposes MCP-based `google_search`, `web_browse`, and `paper_search`, with concurrency control, caching, and prompt-layer composability for both training and inference.

## Key Results

- On the four long-form benchmarks, `DR Tulu-8B (RL)` scores `86.8` on SQAv2, `50.2` on HealthBench, `74.3` on ResearchQA, and `43.4` on DeepResearchBench, for `63.7` average.
- The RL model improves over `DR Tulu-8B (SFT)` by `+14.5` on SQAv2, `+12.1` on HealthBench, `+5.8` on ResearchQA, `+4.4` on DRB, and `+9.8` on average (`63.7` vs. `53.9`).
- Relative to open deep-research baselines, DR Tulu beats Tongyi DeepResearch-30B-A3B by `13.7` average points (`63.7` vs. `50.0`), WebExplorer-8B by `19.3` (`44.4`), and WebThinker-32B-DPO by `34.8` (`28.9`).
- On SQAv2 fine-grained metrics, `DR Tulu-8B (RL)` reaches rubric `89.6`, answer `95.4`, citation precision `88.6`, and citation recall `73.7`, whereas open baselines generally provide no citations.
- Usage statistics on SQAv2 show DR Tulu averaging `1889.2` answer tokens, `35.8` citations, `4.3` tool calls, and about `USD 0.0019` per query, compared with `USD 1.8` for OpenAI Deep Research.
- In rubric ablations, evolving rubrics outperform initial search-based rubrics after `650` RL steps (`58.2` vs. `57.3` average), while general rubrics can underperform the SFT starting point.
- The model also generalizes to short-form QA: average score rises from `47.5` for `Qwen3-8B + Our Search` to `58.0` for `DR Tulu-8B (SFT)` and `62.4` for `DR Tulu-8B (RL)`.

## Limitations

- RL training is judge- and API-dependent: rubric generation and scoring rely on `GPT-4.1` / `GPT-4.1-mini`, and overall training speed remained bottlenecked by rollout-time API rate limits even when more compute was added.
- The paper explicitly reports a train-test mismatch: higher in-training reward does not always translate into better downstream benchmark scores because training rubrics, judges, and benchmark evaluators differ.
- The final `25`-day, `1900`-step RL run still had not saturated, so the reported checkpoint is not a clean compute-optimal endpoint.
- Many baseline numbers are reused from published leaderboards instead of fully rerun evaluations, and some expensive proprietary evaluations use `100`-sample subsets.
- Although DR Tulu is competitive overall, the authors note that stronger proprietary base models such as GPT-5 and Gemini 3 Pro still hold advantages on some specialized tasks and final-answer correctness dimensions.
