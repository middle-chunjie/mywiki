## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature.

The paper proposes a training-free self-verification framework for large language models that reranks chain-of-thought candidate answers by asking the same model to verify each candidate through a backward reasoning pass. Instead of learning a separate verifier, the method rewrites a candidate answer as an added condition, then checks whether the model can recover masked original conditions or judge the consistency of the resulting statement set. Across arithmetic, commonsense, and logical reasoning benchmarks, the approach improves both GPT-3 `code-davinci-001` and InstructGPT `code-davinci-002`, with especially strong gains on arithmetic tasks such as GSM8K and SingleEq. The work is an early inference-time reliability method for LLM reasoning that emphasizes interpretable verification scores over additional supervised verifier training.

## Problem & Motivation

Chain-of-thought prompting improves multi-step reasoning, but the paper argues that it is fragile because a single local mistake can corrupt the entire reasoning chain and final answer. Prior answer-verification approaches usually require training a separate verifier with extra annotations, which reduces portability across tasks and obscures why a score is assigned. The paper therefore asks whether an LLM can reuse its own reasoning ability to check candidate conclusions after generation, so that better answers can be selected without fine-tuning, gradient updates, or external verifiers. The central motivation is to obtain a more explainable and task-agnostic verification mechanism for few-shot reasoning.

## Method

- **Problem setup**: the input is decomposed as `X = {f_1, f_2, ..., f_R, q}`, where `f_r` are conditions and `q` is the question. Few-shot chain-of-thought demonstrations are packaged as `C = {(X_i, t_i, y_i)}_{i=1}^n`.
- **Forward reasoning**: the LLM first performs [[chain-of-thought-prompting]] and generates multiple candidate answers by stochastic decoding. In implementation, the paper uses `K = 5` candidate conclusions per question.
- **Candidate rewriting**: each candidate answer `y` is rewritten into a declarative conclusion `f_y` using an instruction like "Please change the questions and answers into complete declarative sentences [Q] The answer is [A]."
- **Backward verification**: the rewritten conclusion is appended back to the original problem, and the model solves a verification problem instead of the original task. This creates an inference-time reranking loop rather than a separate trained verifier.
- **True-False Item Verification (TFV)**: for general reasoning tasks, the model is asked whether the condition set plus `f_y` is jointly correct. The verification score is the count of `True` outputs across repeated samples.
- **Condition Mask Verification (CMV)**: for arithmetic tasks, the method masks one condition at a time, typically a numeric value extracted by regex, and asks the model to recover the masked value given the remaining conditions plus `f_y`.
- **Scoring rule**: forward generation factorizes as `P(y | C, X) = P(t_CoT | C, X) * P(y | C, X, t_CoT)`. The verification score for candidate `y` is computed over repeated backward checks, and the final answer is selected by `argmax_k Score_k`.
- **Sampling budget**: backward verification repeats each candidate `P = 10` times, with `max_tokens = 168` per decode and no top-k truncation. Each experiment is run `3` times and averaged.
- **Applicability**: CMV is used for arithmetic reasoning because masked numeric conditions give explicit targets, while TFV is used for commonsense and logical reasoning because exact condition recovery is harder there.

## Key Results

- On InstructGPT `code-davinci-002`, self-verification improves CoT on all `8` reported datasets: GSM8K `60.81 -> 65.14` (`+4.33`), SingleEq `91.01 -> 93.40` (`+2.39`), AddSub `82.78 -> 86.33` (`+3.55`), MultiArith `96.13 -> 99.15` (`+3.02`), AQUA-RAT `45.30 -> 47.95` (`+2.65`), SVAMP `75.87 -> 76.99` (`+1.12`), CSQA `77.42 -> 77.83` (`+0.41`), and Date Understanding `65.43 -> 66.57` (`+1.14`).
- On GPT-3 `code-davinci-001`, gains are smaller but still positive on all `8` datasets, including AQUA-RAT `18.90 -> 27.04` (`+8.14`) and CSQA `46.75 -> 47.68` (`+0.93`).
- The paper reports larger average gains on arithmetic reasoning than on commonsense or logical reasoning: `+1.67 / +2.84` for GPT-3 / InstructGPT arithmetic tasks versus `+0.62 / +0.78` on the other tasks.
- Self-verification still helps when combined with stronger forward reasoners: with self-consistency on `code-davinci-002`, GSM8K rises `78.00 -> 78.32`; with PAL on the same model, GSM8K rises `72.02 -> 72.89`.
- In the focused verification analysis, backward verification outperforms both random choice and plain CoT accuracy: GSM8K `58.9%` vs `35.7%` random and `53.4%` CoT, MultiArith `89.0%` vs `45.6%` random and `71.2%` CoT, and SingleEq `74.5%` vs `43.3%` random and `51.0%` CoT.

## Limitations

The method depends on the base model already producing at least one correct candidate answer, so it cannot rescue failures when all forward samples are wrong. Its prompts for rewriting, masking, and verification are manually designed, which may introduce prompt bias and dataset-specific heuristics. The paper also shows that benefits are much stronger on arithmetic tasks than on commonsense or logical reasoning, suggesting that the verification signal is weaker when exact masked targets are unavailable. In addition, the approach increases inference cost because it expands each example into `K * P` verification calls, and it verifies conclusions rather than directly evaluating whether the full intermediate reasoning trace is sound.
