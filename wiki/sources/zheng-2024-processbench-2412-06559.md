## Summary

ProcessBench is a benchmark for measuring the ability of language models to identify erroneous steps in mathematical reasoning. It contains 3,400 test cases drawn from GSM8K, MATH, OlympiadBench, and Omni-MATH, with solutions generated by 12 open-source LLMs and annotated by doctoral-level human experts who label the earliest erroneous step in each solution. Evaluation covers two model types: process reward models (PRMs) and general LLMs prompted as critic models. Key findings are that existing PRMs generalize poorly to Olympiad-level problems and are outperformed by strong critic models, while the best open-source critic (QwQ-32B-Preview) matches GPT-4o but trails o1-mini significantly.

## Problem & Motivation

Existing benchmarks for reasoning-process assessment either use problems too easy for modern LLMs (GSM8K/MATH-level) or annotate only final-answer correctness without step-level error locations, limiting fine-grained evaluation of intermediate reasoning quality. As LLMs approach or exceed human performance on many tasks, scalable automated oversight demands tools that can pinpoint exactly where reasoning goes wrong—both to supervise model outputs and to train better PRMs and critic models.

## Method

**Benchmark construction pipeline:**
- **Problem curation:** 400 test cases from GSM8K, 1,000 each from MATH, OlympiadBench, and Omni-MATH test sets; competition- and Olympiad-level difficulty dominates.
- **Solution generation:** 12 solution generators (Qwen2/2.5 series at 1.5B–72B, LLaMA-3/3.1 at 8B–70B); balanced sampling of solutions with correct vs. incorrect final answers.
- **Solution reformatting:** Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct re-segments solutions at logically complete paragraph boundaries to standardize step granularity; solutions with changed final answers (< 0.5%) are discarded.
- **Expert annotation:** Doctoral-level annotators identify the earliest erroneous step `i ∈ {-1, 0, …, n-1}` (label `-1` = all correct). Three annotators per solution; up to five if no initial consensus; solutions with no 3-way agreement among five discarded (~30% overall discard rate).
- **Error taxonomy (non-exclusive):** mathematical errors, logical errors, conceptual errors, completeness errors.

**Evaluation protocol:**
- For each subset compute accuracy on erroneous samples (err-acc), accuracy on correct samples (corr-acc), and their harmonic mean F1 as the primary metric; F1 balances over-critical and under-critical tendencies.
- **PRMs evaluated:** Math-Shepherd-PRM-7B, RLHFlow-PRM-Mistral-8B, RLHFlow-PRM-Deepseek-8B, Skywork-PRM-1.5B, Skywork-PRM-7B, Qwen2.5-Math-7B-PRM800K (authors' own fine-tune).
- **PRM inference:** step correctness → earliest erroneous step via scalar threshold (tuned on GSM8K F1) for Skywork PRMs; direct correctness labels for others.
- **Critic models evaluated:** Qwen2/2.5/2.5-Math/2.5-Coder series, LLaMA-3/3.1/3.3 series, QwQ-32B-Preview, GPT-4o-0806, o1-mini; prompted with a structured template to return `\boxed{i}` indicating earliest error index.
- Open-source models: majority voting over 8 samples (`p=0.9, t=0.7`); Qwen2.5-Math uses `p=0.8`; GPT-4o: greedy; o1-mini: single sample.
- Qwen2.5-Math-7B-PRM800K: fine-tunes on PRM800K with binary reward head at `\n\n` token positions; trained on 8× A100 80 GB.

## Key Results

- **Best open-source PRM** (Skywork-PRM-7B): avg F1 = 42.1; drops from 70.8 on GSM8K to 21.0 on Omni-MATH.
- **Authors' trained PRM** (Qwen2.5-Math-7B-PRM800K): avg F1 = 56.5; substantially outperforms all other PRMs with 50.7 / 44.3 on Olympiad / Omni-MATH—showing human-annotated PRM800K generalizes better than auto-labeled PRMs.
- **Best open-source critic** (QwQ-32B-Preview): avg F1 = 71.5; 88.0 GSM8K, 78.7 MATH, 57.8 OlympiadBench, 61.3 Omni-MATH—competitive with GPT-4o (61.9 avg).
- **Best overall** (o1-mini): avg F1 = 87.9; 93.2 / 88.9 / 87.2 / 82.4 across all four subsets—shows large gap between current open-source and reasoning-specialized proprietary models.
- **Process errors in correct solutions:** 3.5% (GSM8K), 18.8% (MATH), 32.2% (OlympiadBench), 51.8% (Omni-MATH)—first empirical evidence that harder problems yield far more process errors even when the final answer is correct, undermining rule-based RL that rewards only final answers.
- PRMs suffer steeper degradation on harder problems than critic models, suggesting their auto-labeled training data is brittle.
- Within critic models, performance scales consistently with model size within each family.

## Limitations

- Expert annotation of Olympiad-level problems remains difficult even for PhD mathematicians; some labels may be inaccurate, particularly for Omni-MATH.
- The ~30% solution discard rate means particularly hard problems are under-represented, potentially biasing the difficulty distribution.
- Evaluation covers only identifying the earliest error, not correcting it or explaining it in detail.
- Benchmark covers mathematical reasoning only; generalization to other domains (code, science) is untested.
- QwQ-32B-Preview is used both as a solution reformatter (Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct) and as a baseline model; while the reformatter and evaluator are different models, the reformatting step could introduce subtle biases.
