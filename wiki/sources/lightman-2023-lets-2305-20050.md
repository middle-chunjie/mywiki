## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature.

The paper compares process supervision and outcome supervision for training reward models that rank chain-of-thought solutions to hard math problems. Large-scale models are fine-tuned from GPT-4 and evaluated by best-of-N search over sampled MATH solutions, while smaller models are used for controlled ablations with synthetic supervision. The central design is a process-supervised reward model (PRM) that predicts whether each reasoning step is correct and scores a full solution by aggregating step-level probabilities. On a held-out MATH subset, the PRM reaches `78.2%` best-of-1860 accuracy, beating an outcome-supervised reward model (ORM) at `72.4%` and majority voting at `69.6%`. The paper also releases PRM800K and argues that active learning improves process-supervision data efficiency by about `2.6x`.

## Problem & Motivation

Large language models can produce long chain-of-thought solutions for mathematical reasoning, but a single wrong intermediate step is enough to derail the final answer. The paper asks how to train reward models that can reliably distinguish correct from flawed reasoning traces: by supervising only the final outcome, or by supervising each intermediate step. This matters both for search quality and for alignment, because outcome-only labels can reward spurious reasoning that happens to land on the right answer.

## Method

- **Base models**: large-scale generator and reward models are fine-tuned from `GPT-4`; small-scale models use a similar architecture pretrained with roughly `200x` less compute. All models receive an additional math-focused pretraining stage on `MathMix`, a corpus of roughly `1.5B` math-relevant tokens.
- **Generator formatting**: the generator is fine-tuned for `1` epoch on filtered correct MATH solutions so it emits newline-delimited step-by-step solutions, making each reasoning step easy to isolate and label.
- **Process-supervision data**: PRM800K contains about `800k` usable step-level labels across `75k` solutions to `12k` problems. Human labelers mark each step as `positive`, `negative`, or `neutral`; for incorrect solutions, supervision stops at the first incorrect step.
- **Outcome-supervised reward model (ORM)**: the ORM is trained on uniformly sampled solutions, typically `100` samples per problem at large scale, to predict whether the final answer is correct. At test time it uses the score at the final token as the solution score.
- **Process-supervised reward model (PRM)**: the PRM predicts a label token after each reasoning step. A full-solution score is computed as `s(x) = ∏_t p_t`, where `p_t` is the PRM probability that step `t` is correct; the best-performing setup treats neutral steps as positive during scoring.
- **Search-based evaluation**: each reward model ranks uniformly sampled solutions from the fixed generator, and the paper reports best-of-`N` accuracy by selecting the top-ranked candidate for each problem.
- **Active learning**: during data collection, the current PRM ranks sampled solutions and surfaces the most convincing wrong-answer traces to human labelers. In the small-scale synthetic setup, the selector uses `1000` samples per problem and keeps a mix of `80%` convincing wrong-answer solutions plus `20%` other convincing solutions.
- **Synthetic supervision ablations**: a large PRM supervises smaller reward models so the paper can compare process supervision, outcome supervision from a reward-model oracle, and outcome supervision from final-answer checking on matched datasets.

## Key Results

- On the held-out `500`-problem MATH subset, best-of-`1860` search reaches `78.2%` with the PRM versus `72.4%` with the ORM and `69.6%` with majority voting.
- On out-of-distribution STEM exams (`234` problems total), aggregate best-of-`100` accuracy is `72.9%` for the PRM, `63.8%` for the ORM, and `61.3%` for majority voting.
- In small-scale matched-data ablations, process supervision outperforms both forms of outcome supervision at every data scale examined, including the setting with up to `200` sampled solutions per problem.
- Active learning improves process-supervision data efficiency by approximately `2.6x` relative to uniform labeling.
- The released PRM800K dataset provides roughly `800k` filtered step-level labels, enabling much larger-scale process-supervision experiments than prior work.

## Limitations

- The headline large-scale ORM and PRM are not trained on directly comparable datasets: the PRM uses actively collected step labels, while the ORM uses a much larger uniformly sampled solution set.
- The study evaluates reward models only as rankers for best-of-`N` search over a fixed generator; it does not test reinforcement learning that updates the generator itself.
- Most experiments are in mathematical reasoning, so it remains unclear how strongly the gains from process supervision transfer to broader domains.
- Outcome supervision on MATH is especially vulnerable to false positives, because some incorrect chains of thought still reach the correct final answer.
- The paper acknowledges possible contamination between math-focused pretraining data and the MATH benchmark, even though it argues relative comparisons should still be informative.
