## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature.

This paper proposes LASER, a post-training intervention that replaces a selected Transformer weight matrix with its truncated SVD approximation, retaining only lower-order singular components. Instead of compressing a model uniformly, the method searches over matrix type, layer, and retained-rank fraction, and finds that severe rank reduction in later MLP layers can improve factual question answering and reasoning without any retraining. Across GPT-J, RoBERTa, and Llama2, the gains are strongest on infrequent facts and on robustness to paraphrased prompts, suggesting that higher-order components sometimes inject noisy or conflicting outputs. The paper also shows smaller but consistent gains on a Sokoban decision Transformer, positioning LASER as both a compression-style intervention and a probe of latent knowledge access in LLMs.

## Problem & Motivation

Transformer-based large language models are heavily over-parameterized, and prior pruning work mainly showed that many parameters can be removed with limited damage. This paper asks a stronger question: can selective post-hoc reduction actually improve reasoning and factual behavior rather than merely preserve it? The motivation is that some learned parameters, especially in specific layers, may encode noisy or conflicting responses that obscure weakly learned facts. If so, carefully removing higher-order components from those matrices could expose cleaner decision boundaries and make latent knowledge more accessible without additional data, fine-tuning, or new parameters.

## Method

- **Single-step LASER parameterization**: define an intervention by ``(τ, ℓ, ρ)``, where `τ ∈ {W_q, W_k, W_v, W_o, U_in, U_out}` chooses the weight-matrix type, `ℓ` is the layer index, and `ρ ∈ [0,1)` is the retained-rank fraction.
- **Truncated SVD update**: for a selected matrix `W = UΣV^T`, LASER replaces `W` with the rank-`r` approximation ``\hat{W} = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i^\downarrow(W) u_i v_i^T``, where `r = floor(ρ · rank_max(W))`.
- **Transformer formulation**: the paper analyzes attention with ``p(j|i) = exp(q_i^T k_j / sqrt(d)) / \sum_l exp(q_i^T k_l / sqrt(d))`` and residual MLP blocks with weights `U_in` and `U_out`; empirically, later-layer MLP matrices are the most helpful intervention targets.
- **Component interpretation**: lower-order components correspond to larger singular values and are kept; higher-order components correspond to smaller singular values and are removed as suspected noise sources.
- **Compositional LASER**: interventions can be combined as ``{(τ_i, ℓ_i, ρ_i)}_{i=1}^m``; the paper reports a greedy search that starts from larger `ℓ` and smaller `ρ` when composing multiple steps.
- **Search space and validation**: the broad evaluation restricts `τ` to `U_in` and `U_out`, searches all layers, and uses ``ρ ∈ {0.9, 0.8, 0.6, 0.2, 0.1, 0.05, 0.01}``; `20%` of each processed dataset is used for validation and `80%` for test.
- **Evaluation setup**: models include `GPT-J (6B)`, `RoBERTa`, and `Llama2 (7B)`; metrics are generation accuracy, classification accuracy, and log loss, with greedy decoding (`temperature = 0`) for open-ended QA tasks.

## Key Results

- On CounterFact, GPT-J single-step LASER improves held-out accuracy from `13.1%` to `24.0%`; the paper also reports top-1 accuracy rising from `13.3%` to `24.1%` in the main analysis narrative.
- On the same benchmark, composing interventions across layers raises GPT-J top-10 accuracy to `29.2%`, a further `+5.2` absolute gain over the best single-step intervention.
- LASER improves paraphrase robustness for already-correct CounterFact examples by roughly `24.8` percentage points.
- Table 1 shows broad gains across models and tasks, including GPT-J on FEVER `50.2% -> 56.2%`, GPT-J on BigBench-WikidataQA `51.8% -> 65.9%`, and Llama2 on BigBench-Epistemic Reasoning `44.8% -> 63.4%`.
- Bias-oriented and truthfulness settings also improve in several cases, including GPT-J on Bios Gender `70.9% -> 97.5%` and Llama2 on TruthfulQA `50.5% -> 56.2%`.
- The language-modeling objective degrades slightly: GPT-J perplexity on The Pile rises from `4.8` to `5.0` for MLP-input interventions and from `4.8` to `4.9` for MLP-output interventions.
- Outside text, a `6`-layer decision Transformer on `10 x 10` Sokoban improves from `50.67` to `53.0` accuracy and from `0.575` to `0.965` return after LASER.

## Limitations

- The method is not plug-and-play: strong results depend on validation-time search over `(τ, ℓ, ρ)`, and the best layer and reduction level vary by model and task.
- The paper's mechanism claim is still inferential; the "higher-order components add noise" story is supported by qualitative and cosine-similarity analyses rather than causal proof.
- Some gains trade off against fluency or calibration, since perplexity and sometimes log loss worsen even when accuracy rises.
- Evidence outside text is limited to a single Sokoban decision-Transformer setting, and the paper itself notes the effect may be text-specific or require sufficiently large models.
- Reporting details are not perfectly clean: the main text describes GPT-J as having `27` layers, while Appendix C later refers to `28` layers.
