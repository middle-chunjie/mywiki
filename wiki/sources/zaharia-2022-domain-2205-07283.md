## Problem & Motivation

CWI datasets are scarce, domain-specific, and multilingual â€” models trained on one domain (e.g., biblical text) degrade on another (biomedical). Similarly, cross-lingual generalization is hampered by language-specific surface features. Standard fine-tuning on each domain/language individually does not scale. The paper asks: can a single adversarial training strategy smooth cross-domain and cross-lingual distributional gaps to produce a more robust complexity predictor?

## Method

## Limitations

- French (no training data in CWI 2018) still achieves the lowest Pearson scores despite cross-lingual adaptation, indicating zero-shot cross-lingual generalization remains hard.
- Scientific/biomedical out-of-vocabulary tokens (e.g., "sitosterolemia", chemical formulae) fall outside Transformer vocabulary and degrade predictions.
- Models evaluated on only two CWI benchmark datasets; generalizability to other complexity prediction tasks is unverified.
- VAE and Transformer Decoder auxiliary tasks show inconsistent gains â€” their benefit is task- and split-specific; hyperparameter sensitivity is not thoroughly ablated.
