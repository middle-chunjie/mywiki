## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature.

This paper studies whether GPT-4 can act as a synthetic data augmenter for low-resource extractive machine reading comprehension rather than relying solely on human annotation. The proposed pipeline uses one-shot or two-shot in-context prompts to first generate new passage-like contexts and then generate question-answer pairs conditioned on those contexts. A round-trip filtration step keeps only examples whose regenerated answer matches the original synthetic answer. The authors fine-tune RoBERTa on CovidQA, PolicyQA, and TechQA with synthetic datasets ranging from `1x` to `10x` the original training size. Synthetic augmentation substantially improves CovidQA, modestly improves PolicyQA, and fails on TechQA, showing both the promise and brittleness of LLM-based augmentation in genuinely low-data domains.

## Problem & Motivation

Low-resource machine reading comprehension suffers from the scarcity and cost of human-labeled question-answer pairs, especially in specialized domains where unlabeled passages from generic sources such as Wikipedia are not a good fit. Prior QA augmentation methods often assume abundant external contexts or generate only questions from existing passages, which leaves a gap for domains where even suitable raw passages are limited. The paper asks whether a frontier large language model can generate both synthetic contexts and extractive QA pairs closely enough to the target distribution that downstream readers improve after fine-tuning.

## Method

- **Task setting**: low-resource extractive [[machine-reading-comprehension]] on CovidQA, PolicyQA, and TechQA, with original dataset sizes of `2,019`, `12,102`, and `1,808` QA pairs respectively.
- **Stage 1, context generation**: GPT-4 receives `k in {1, 2}` in-context examples of real training passages and generates new paragraph-length contexts intended to stay close to the source domain and reduce [[domain-shift]].
- **Stage 2, QA generation**: conditioned on each synthetic context, GPT-4 receives `k in {1, 2}` example QA pairs and generates a synthetic question-answer pair that mimics the original dataset style.
- **Synthetic scale**: for each dataset, the authors create augmented training sets with synthetic volume from `1x` up to `10x` the size of the original training data to probe scaling effects of [[synthetic-data-generation]] and [[data-augmentation]].
- **Round-trip filtration**: after generating a pair `(c, q, a)`, GPT-4 is asked to answer `q` again from `c` without being shown `a`; the example is kept only if `a_regen = a`, yielding the cycle-consistent `CC` variants summarized as [[round-trip-filtration]].
- **Reader model**: downstream experiments fine-tune [[roberta]] using the HuggingFace implementation as the extractive reader across all settings.
- **Training hyperparameters**: `learning_rate = 3e-5`, `batch_size = 16`, `epochs = 5`, hardware `= 1 x Nvidia V100`; evaluation uses Exact Match and F1.
- **Baseline comparator**: besides the original training set, the paper compares against a T5-based [[question-generation]] baseline trained on SQuAD that produces QA pairs from a paragraph.

## Key Results

- **CovidQA**: original trainset reaches `25.81` EM / `50.91` F1; the best configuration, one-shot with cycle-consistent filtering, reaches `31.90` EM / `58.66` F1, a gain of `+6.09` EM and `+7.75` F1.
- **PolicyQA**: original trainset reaches `30.56` EM / `58.15` F1; the best configuration is unfiltered one-shot augmentation at `32.18` EM / `59.61` F1, improving by `+1.62` EM and `+1.46` F1.
- **TechQA**: original trainset is `11.11` EM / `39.45` F1, but the strongest baseline is the T5 generator at `44.44` EM / `59.92` F1; GPT-4 augmentation never surpasses it, with best augmented scores of `22.22` EM and `44.73` F1.
- **Prompting pattern**: one-shot prompting is consistently stronger than two-shot on CovidQA and PolicyQA, suggesting added demonstrations do not automatically improve synthetic QA quality.
- **Filtering effect**: round-trip filtration helps most on CovidQA, where it gives the overall best system, but hurts PolicyQA and does not rescue TechQA, indicating a precision-recall tradeoff rather than a universally beneficial filter.

## Limitations

- The evaluation covers only three extractive QA datasets, and the TechQA test set is extremely small (`9` examples), so variance is high and generalization claims are limited.
- The study uses a single synthetic generator family (GPT-4) and a single reader backbone (RoBERTa), so it does not separate model-specific effects from method-level effects.
- Synthetic data quality is measured mainly through downstream EM/F1 and self-consistency, without a detailed human annotation study of factuality, answerability, or diversity.
- Round-trip filtration may improve precision by discarding inconsistent examples, but it also reduces recall and can remove useful training variety, as suggested by weaker PolicyQA and TechQA outcomes.
- The paper focuses on extractive reading comprehension; it does not test whether the same augmentation strategy transfers to abstractive QA, multi-hop QA, or other low-resource NLP tasks.
