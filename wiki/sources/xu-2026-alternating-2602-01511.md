## Summary

This paper proposes Rubric-ARM, a rubric-based reward modeling framework for non-verifiable large language model post-training. Instead of using a fixed rubric or a scalar reward head, Rubric-ARM treats rubric generation as a latent action and jointly optimizes a rubric generator and a rubric-conditioned judge with alternating reinforcement learning. The key idea is to stabilize learning by updating the judge first under cached rubrics, then updating the rubric generator against the improved judge, which the authors support with a variance analysis. On reward-model benchmarks, Rubric-ARM improves average white-box performance from `70.1` to `74.8`, reaches `76.2` with voting@5, and transfers to stronger downstream DPO, IterDPO, and GRPO policy post-training on instruction-following, preference, and creative-writing evaluations.

## Problem & Motivation

Standard reward models in non-verifiable domains often output a scalar score or pairwise preference without exposing the criteria behind the judgment. The paper argues that this is inadequate for open-ended tasks such as creative writing and instruction following, where quality is multi-dimensional and task-specific. Prior rubric-based methods improve interpretability but usually rely on static prompting or separately trained rubric generators and judges, which limits adaptation to the target preference distribution. Rubric-ARM is motivated by the idea that rubric quality and judging quality should co-evolve under a shared correctness objective, while the optimization schedule must explicitly control non-stationarity and early-stage variance.

## Method

- **Formulation**: for each preference example `(x, y^(1), y^(2), o)`, the rubric generator samples `r ~ π_r(. | x; θ_r)` and the judge predicts `(c, \hat{o}) ~ π_j(. | x, y^(1), y^(2), r; θ_j)`. The learning target is expected preference correctness `E[I[\hat{o} = o]]`.
- **Rubrics as latent actions**: Rubric-ARM treats the rubric as an unobserved textual action rather than a fixed prompt artifact, so rubric generation is optimized to maximize downstream judging accuracy instead of matching a static rubric corpus.
- **Stage I SFT warmup**: both modules are warm-started with synthetic rubric/judging trajectories from OpenRubrics sources including UltraFeedback, SkyWork, Magpie, and Synthetic Instruction Following, using standard next-token prediction.
- **Judge update with fixed generator**: with `θ_r` frozen, the judge maximizes `J_j(θ_j; θ_r)` under sampled rubrics. In practice, one rubric is cached per training instance, and the judge receives shaped reward `R_j = R_acc + R_fmt`, where `R_acc = I[\hat{o} = o]` and `R_fmt` enforces criterion-by-criterion explanations, justification, and a valid final decision.
- **Rubric-generator update with fixed judge**: with `θ_j` frozen, the rubric generator maximizes `J_r(θ_r; θ_j)` so that generated rubrics help the current judge recover the dataset preference. The paper uses a single greedy rollout with `t = 0` and reward `R_r = I[\hat{o} = o]`.
- **Alternating RL schedule**: at iteration `t`, the method samples rubrics `r_i^t`, updates the judge with `GRPO(θ_j^t; r_i^t, D)`, then updates the rubric generator with `GRPO(θ_r^t; θ_j^(t+1), D)`. The implementation runs `3` alternating iterations and always updates the judge before the generator.
- **Variance analysis**: the paper shows that judge updates under reused rubrics remove inter-rubric variance, while generator updates include both multiplicative reward noise and a cross-rubric inconsistency term `Var_r(p(r) u_r(r))`, explaining why judge-first optimization is more stable early in training.
- **Training setup**: both rubric generator and judge are initialized from `Qwen-3-8B`; generator/judge hyperparameters include `#generations = 6/7`, cutoff lengths `512/1024`, batch sizes `288/224`, learning rate `1e-6`, `β = 0.001`, `ε_high = 0.28`, `ε_low = 0.2`, and `1` epoch per phase.
- **Policy post-training use**: the trained modules supervise `Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct` for offline `DPO` / `IterDPO` and online `GRPO`. For online RL, rewards average both response orders to reduce positional bias under the same rubric.

## Key Results

- **Reward-model average**: Rubric-ARM reaches `74.8` average across the main reward-model suite, improving over Rubric-RM at `70.1`; voting@5 reaches `76.2` vs. `73.0`.
- **Representative benchmark wins**: Rubric-ARM scores `79.6` on RewardBench Chat-Hard, `70.8` on PPE-IFEval, `86.1` on InfoBench, `41.9` on RewardBench2 Precise-IF, and `89.4` on RewardBench2 Focus.
- **OOD writing evaluation**: on WritingPreferenceBench, Rubric-ARM achieves `63.2`, outperforming Rubric-RM at `60.3` and RM-R1-Qwen2.5-7B at `59.8`.
- **Ablations validate the schedule**: switching the optimization order drops the average from `74.8` to `72.4`; removing the format reward drops it to `72.6`. On RewardBench2 Precise-IF, switching order reduces `41.9 -> 24.4`.
- **Offline policy alignment**: with `Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct`, DPO / IterDPO via Rubric-ARM reaches `80.4 / 80.8` average on IFEval and `83.7 / 85.0` on InfoBench.
- **Preference and broad-task transfer**: DPO / IterDPO via Rubric-ARM reaches `51.7 / 53.4` average on Arena-Hard + AlpacaEval and `53.7 / 55.7` on WildBench.
- **Online RL**: GRPO with Rubric-ARM reward reaches `55.4` average, compared with `52.3` for RM-R1 reward and `46.8` for the base `Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct`.
- **Efficiency**: inference on `100` samples takes `33.50s`, faster than Rubric-RM at `105.12s` and substantially faster than several reasoning-style baselines (`170.76s` to `382.02s`).

## Limitations

- The paper is an `arXiv` preprint from `2026`, so the empirical claims have not yet been peer reviewed.
- The main training and transfer results are concentrated on Qwen-based `7B/8B` backbones and the OpenRubrics data pipeline; cross-family generalization is not thoroughly established.
- Rubric-ARM depends on synthetic warmup data plus a multi-stage RL pipeline, so its performance may inherit biases or coverage gaps from synthetic rubric/judge trajectories.
- The theory mainly justifies the update order through gradient-variance decomposition; it does not provide a full convergence guarantee or a formal notion of rubric quality.
- Although efficient for a two-module system, deployment still requires maintaining both a rubric generator and a judge rather than a single reward model.
