## Summary

The paper introduces YesBut, a benchmark for testing whether large vision-language and language models can understand humor created by juxtaposing contradictory two-panel comics. The dataset contains `348` captionless comics, each annotated with a literal description, a contradiction explanation, an underlying philosophy, and a title, enabling four tasks that span surface narration and deeper reasoning. The authors evaluate commercial and open models with automatic and human judgments, showing that even strong systems remain substantially below human performance, especially on abstract title matching and philosophical interpretation. The analysis argues that failures stem from both visual misreading and weak nonlinear narrative reasoning, making comic understanding a useful stress test for socially grounded multimodal intelligence.

## Problem & Motivation

Existing multimodal benchmarks mostly test object recognition, instruction following, or relatively direct reasoning, but they do not probe whether models can understand humor built from contradictions across panels. The paper targets captionless comics whose meaning depends on juxtaposition, social knowledge, and nonlinear narrative integration rather than single-image description alone. The motivation is that success on this task would indicate stronger understanding of human creative expression, social context, and deep semantics than standard visual question answering or captioning-style evaluations provide.

## Method

- **Benchmark construction**: build YesBut from `348` two-panel captionless comics, mostly from Anton Gudim's "YES, BUT" series, after scraping, deduplication, two-panel filtering, and offensive-content removal.
- **Annotation schema**: each sample includes `1` comic, `1` literal description, `1` contradiction explanation, `4` philosophy options, and `4` title options; average lengths are `80` words for literal descriptions, `31` for contradictions, `24` for philosophies, and `6` for titles.
- **Human-AI collaborative annotation**: `8` human annotators use GPT-4 to draft descriptions and contradictions, then refine them into gold labels; GPT-4 is also used to draft deeper philosophy/title content and hard negatives.
- **Quality control**: each comic goes through cross-verification with an inspector, optional third-annotator adjudication for ambiguous cases, and final author review to remove unclear or controversial samples.
- **Task design**: evaluate `4` tasks spanning increasing difficulty: literal description writing, contradiction generation, underlying philosophy selection, and title matching.
- **Model settings**: compare commercial VLMs and open models in zero-shot mode; GPT-4, ChatGPT, and Claude-3 use `temperature = 1.0`, while other models use default decoding settings.
- **Prompting protocol**: create `3` prompt variants per task and report averages to reduce prompt sensitivity.
- **Evaluation**: use `accuracy` for MCQ tasks, `BERTScore (recall)`, `ROUGE-2 (recall)`, and GPT-based `1-5` scoring for generation tasks, plus human evaluation from `3` judges on `30` random samples.
- **Analysis probes**: test whether oracle literal descriptions improve deep reasoning, and whether decomposing VLM inference into description-then-reasoning helps or hurts performance.

## Key Results

- Dataset scale: `348` comics with `1,392` philosophy candidates and `1,392` title candidates after multi-choice construction.
- Literal description: GPT-4 achieves the strongest automatic scores among VLMs with `88.32` BERTScore recall and `87.46` ROUGE-2 recall.
- Contradiction generation: GPT-4 reaches `87.64` BERTScore recall, `83.21` ROUGE-2 recall, and `4.03/5` GPT evaluation; Claude-3 is close but still below on this task.
- Deep reasoning: Claude-3 is best on underlying philosophy selection at `84.10%`, while LLaVA-1.6-34B is best on title matching at `63.31%`, both still far from ceiling.
- Text-only reasoning remains competitive: with generated descriptions as input, ChatGPT scores `75.86%` on philosophy selection and `49.52%` on title matching.
- Better literal understanding helps deeper reasoning: providing oracle descriptions substantially improves philosophy/title performance for both LLMs and VLMs.
- Naive decomposition is unreliable: augmenting VLMs with their own generated descriptions often hurts title accuracy, e.g. LLaVA-1.6-13B drops from `55.08%` to `48.76%`.

## Limitations

- The benchmark is relatively small at `348` samples, and comic interpretation remains partly subjective even after multi-stage review.
- The data focuses on one humor mechanism, juxtaposed contradiction, so results may not transfer to broader visual understanding or other humor genres.
- Many findings depend on prompt-based black-box evaluation of 2024-era models, so leaderboard-like rankings may shift quickly as models improve.
- The paper shows that generated descriptions can contaminate downstream reasoning, but it does not fully isolate whether the main cause is description noise, prompt length, or architecture limitations.
- Most comics come from a narrow source distribution, which may limit cultural and stylistic coverage.
