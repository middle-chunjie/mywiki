## Summary

PRISM is a large-scale human feedback dataset for studying pluralistic alignment in large language models. It links detailed participant profiles and stated preferences to live conversational ratings, collecting data from `1,500` participants across `75` countries over `8,011` conversations with `21` LLMs. The dataset is designed around three properties the authors argue are missing from standard alignment corpora: broader participation, census-representative sub-samples for the UK and US, and individualized feedback tied to pseudonymous participant metadata. Beyond releasing the dataset, the paper uses three case studies to show that prompt choice, model preference, and downstream welfare all vary with who is sampled and what conversational contexts are emphasized, making "generic human feedback" a poor abstraction for alignment.

## Problem & Motivation

The paper argues that mainstream human-feedback datasets for LLM alignment are too narrow along four axes: feedback format, domain coverage, participant diversity, and objective specification. Many datasets rely on binary pairwise choices, recruit from narrow crowd or tech communities, hide annotator context, and treat aggregated preferences as if they were universal. PRISM is motivated by the claim that alignment on subjective, controversial, and multicultural topics requires richer signals about who the raters are, what they value in the abstract, and how their in-situ judgments vary across contexts. The goal is therefore not just more data, but better-documented and more socially legible alignment data.

## Method

- **Two-stage data collection**: PRISM first collects a survey, then live conversations. The final release contains `1,500` participants from `75` countries, `8,011` conversations, `27,172` interactions, and `68,371` scored utterances. Participants were paid `GBP 9/hour`, the task averaged `70` minutes, and collection ran from `2023-11-22` to `2023-12-22`.
- **Survey stage**: each participant provides LLM familiarity/usage data, a self-written system string, a self-description, demographics, and stated attribute preferences on a visual analog scale over `0-100` (recorded numerically but not shown to participants).
- **Conversation stage**: participants choose among three conversation types: unguided, values guided, and controversy guided. On the opening turn, `4/21` models are sampled to respond to the same prompt; on later turns, `2` responses are sampled from the previously highest-rated model at non-deterministic temperature.
- **Interface and decoding controls**: commercial APIs use `max_tokens = 256`; HuggingFace models use `min_tokens = 10` and `max_tokens = 200` because the limit applies to new tokens. When defaults were too deterministic, the authors set `temperature = 1.0`. Failed generations were dropped after a `30s` timeout. A neutral system prompt soft-limited outputs to about `50` words.
- **Topic-diversity analysis**: opening prompts are embedded with `all-mpnet-base-v2` into `768` dimensions, reduced with UMAP to `d = 20`, then clustered with HDBSCAN using `min_cluster_size = 80` and `min_dist = 0`. Group-topic over-representation is measured as `over_rep(g,t) = (N_g,t / N_t) / b_g`, and topic prevalence is modeled with OLS over demographic and conversation-type covariates.
- **Model-preference aggregation**: for the balanced subset (`n = 1,246` participants; `n = 6,669` opening conversations), model battles are aggregated with Pairwise Rank Centrality. The method uses pairwise win probabilities `p_ij = wins(i,j) / battles(i,j)`, regularization `alpha = 1`, and tie threshold `t = 5`, then ranks models by the stationary distribution of the induced Markov chain.
- **Welfare analysis**: the paper simulates sampling schemes with representative and demographically restricted sub-populations, using `N in {10, 20, 50, 100}` and welfare metrics MEANRATING / MEANCHOICE to study how the feedback population changes downstream model welfare.

## Key Results

- PRISM contributes `1,500` participants from `75` countries, `8,011` conversations, `27,172` interactions, and `68,371` utterances linked to participant-level profiles.
- Topic clustering assigns about `70%` of opening prompts to `22` clusters, leaving about `30%` as outliers; after controlling for conversation type, about `11%` of demographic coefficients in topic regressions remain significant at `alpha = 99%`.
- Local prompt neighborhoods are still diverse: `84%` of fixed local regions meet or exceed the intersectional entropy expected under random sampling, yet interpersonal preference differences persist even when dialogue context is held nearly fixed.
- Model rankings are unstable under small or filtered samples: with only `100` people, collective preference over the top `10` models becomes noisy; the balanced subset for these analyses contains `1,246` participants and `6,669` opening conversations.
- Text-style correlates of score are measurable but limited in explanatory power: `text_length` has coefficient `+0.0271`, `if_enumerate +7.1981`, `if_question_labels +2.6179`, while `if_line_breaks -10.8285`, `if_refusal -9.0484`, `if_deanthro -2.3025`, and `if_self_identification -3.6354`; the regression has `R^2 = 0.056`.
- In welfare analysis, larger representative samples first-order stochastically dominate smaller or demographically restricted samples, and no single model is close to universally preferred: in the US analysis, the model maximizing `MEANCHOICE` reaches only `45%`.

## Limitations

- The dataset still relies on English-speaking Prolific crowdworkers, so it broadens participation without escaping crowdworker and English-language bias.
- The model pool is a late-2023 snapshot; newer systems such as Gemini, Claude 3, Command-R, Mixtral, and Llama 3 are absent.
- Free-form prompt choice, random model assignment, and stochastic decoding introduce realistic but substantial confounding factors for causal interpretation.
- Visual analog scales and cardinal ratings may violate measurement invariance, and participants may sometimes optimize for interesting or provocative conversations rather than truthful preference reporting.
- Sensitive conversations create residual privacy and harm risks despite pseudonymization, PII checks, consent procedures, and a low moderation-flag rate.
