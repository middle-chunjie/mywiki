## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature.

This paper asks whether instruction-following LLMs are actually competitive few-shot information extractors under in-context learning across NER, relation extraction, event detection, and event argument extraction. Across nine datasets and multiple low-resource regimes, the authors find that direct LLM prompting helps mainly in extremely low-shot settings and otherwise lags behind fine-tuned SLMs in accuracy, latency, and cost, especially on fine-grained label spaces. The paper then identifies a capability split: LLMs are weak on easy, often negative samples, but useful on hard cases requiring knowledge or reasoning. Based on this diagnosis, it proposes an adaptive filter-then-rerank pipeline in which an SLM filters and scores samples and an LLM reranks only hard cases with multiple-choice prompts.

## Problem & Motivation

Prior work on few-shot information extraction reported conflicting conclusions about whether LLMs are good IE solvers, largely because tasks, datasets, prompt designs, and low-resource settings were inconsistent across studies. This paper aims to answer the question under a unified benchmark spanning four IE tasks and nine datasets, while also testing whether LLMs and supervised SLMs have complementary strengths that can be combined rather than compared only as substitutes.

## Method

- **Unified benchmark**: evaluate `9` datasets across `4` IE tasks: [[named-entity-recognition]], [[relation-extraction]], [[event-detection]], and [[event-argument-extraction]]. Few-shot training uses `K`-shot sampling with `K ∈ {1, 5, 10, 20}` for most tasks and up to `100` for RE; test sets are down-sampled to `500` sentences for RE and `250` for other tasks.
- **SLM baselines**: compare vanilla fine-tuning, FSLS, KnowPrompt, PAIE, and UIE. Implementations use RoBERTa-large for FT / FSLS / KnowPrompt, BART-large for PAIE, and T5-large for UIE; the FT / FSLS setup uses `batch_size = 64`, `max_input_len = 192`, `training_steps = 500`, `lr = 5e-5`, `warmup_ratio = 0.1`, `weight_decay = 1e-5`, and `grad_clip = 1.0`.
- **Direct LLM prompting**: formulate ICL as `` `P_{E,I,f}(D,s) = [I; f(E(D,s)); f(s)]` ``, where `I` is the instruction, `E(D,s)` retrieves demonstrations, and `f` renders examples with text templates.
- **Demo retrieval**: demonstrations are selected by sentence-embedding similarity rather than random sampling; Efficient Prompt Retriever is evaluated but not adopted because simple embedding retrieval is competitive and simpler.
- **LLM inference setup**: open-source models use `max_input_len = 2048`, `batch_size = 1`, `max_output_len = 96`, and `temperature = 0`; OpenAI models use `max_input_len = 3600` (`7000` for CODEX on RE), `max_output_len = 32` for RE and `96` otherwise, and `temperature = 0`.
- **Difficulty estimation**: define a sample-level confidence score as `` `conf(x) = max_{l ∈ L} P_SLM(l | x; s)` ``, and treat low-confidence samples as hard samples.
- **Filter-then-rerank**: use the SLM as a filter and keep its prediction for easy samples with `` `conf(x) > τ` ``; for hard samples, send the top-`3` SLM labels plus `None` to an LLM reranker using `4`-shot multiple-choice prompts.
- **Prompt redesign for reranking**: convert IE predictions into MCQ options so the LLM solves a constrained candidate-selection problem rather than open-ended sentence-level extraction; the final system also uses chain-of-thought explanations during reranking.

## Key Results

- On direct few-shot IE, supervised SLMs clearly beat prompted LLMs once annotations are moderately available: on FewNERD `20-shot`, direct LLMs reach `55.9-57.8` F1 while SLM SoTA reaches `61.9`; on TACREV `100-shot`, direct LLMs reach `62.4-62.7` vs. SLM `72.6`; on ACE05 ED `20-shot`, direct LLMs reach `47.9-52.1` vs. SLM `65.8`.
- LLM latency is much worse than SLM latency: over `500` sentences, RoBERTa needs `2.8s` / `1.4s` / `6.6s` on FewNERD / TACREV / ACE05, while CODEX needs `179.4s` / `151.6s` / `171.7s` and LLaMA needs `1135.4s` / `1144.9s` / `733.4s`.
- The adaptive SLM+LLM pipeline gives an average `+2.4` F1 gain over the non-ensemble SLM baseline and `+2.1` over the ensembled SLM filter, showing that LLM reranking adds value beyond plain ensembling.
- With InstructGPT reranking, results improve to `63.3` on FewNERD `20-shot`, `75.4` on TACREV `100-shot`, and `67.3` on ACE05 `20-shot`; with an ensembled SLM filter, the best scores reach `63.7`, `76.8`, and `67.8`.
- Only a small fraction of samples are reranked (`0.5%-10%` depending on dataset), but reranking on those hard samples yields large local gains: `+8.8` F1 on FewNERD, `+17.7` on TACREV, and `+26.8` on ACE05 with GPT-4.
- Compared with direct ICL, the adaptive system cuts budget and latency by roughly `80%-90%` because it calls the LLM only on hard samples and uses shorter MCQ prompts.

## Limitations

- The direct-ICL conclusion is empirical rather than absolute; the authors acknowledge that an untested "lottery prompt" could exist, although their prompt-sensitivity analysis suggests it is unlikely to overturn the main conclusion.
- The adaptive reranker depends on the difficulty metric `` `conf(x)` ``, which assumes the SLM filter is reasonably calibrated; the paper explicitly notes this assumption is imperfect.
- LLM reranking is beneficial mainly on hard samples and can hurt easy samples, especially negative ones, because of false positives, hallucination, and span-boundary mismatch.
- The study covers `9` IE datasets and several LLM variants, but the final system is still evaluated on a subset of tasks for adaptive reranking rather than all four tasks end to end.
