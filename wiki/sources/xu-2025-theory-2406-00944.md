## Summary

This paper gives a token-level theory for why retrieval can help or hurt retrieval-augmented generation. It models RAG next-token prediction as a fusion between the pretrained knowledge distribution of a large language model and the distribution induced by retrieved texts, then decomposes the fusion into benefit and detriment terms defined by KL-divergence-based distribution differences. The paper further argues that the gap between these terms can be approximated by representation similarity, which yields a practical decoding rule. Based on that rule, the authors propose Tok-RAG, which runs pure-LLM and RAG decoding in parallel and chooses tokens step by step. Across open-domain QA, long-form QA, dialogue, code generation, slot filling, and language modeling, Tok-RAG improves robustness without extra training or utility evaluators.

## Problem & Motivation

RAG often improves factual coverage by injecting external evidence, but noisy or wrong retrieved passages can also override correct internal model knowledge. Prior robust-RAG methods usually depend on additional utility evaluators, filtering modules, prompt heuristics, or fine-tuning, so they explain little about when retrieval is intrinsically helpful versus harmful. This paper aims to formalize that trade-off directly in next-token prediction, derive a criterion for comparing benefit and detriment without extra supervision, and turn the theory into a decoding method that preserves useful retrieval while rejecting misleading retrieval.

## Method

- The paper models RAG token prediction as a latent-variable distribution fusion: `p(x_i | R, x_{1:i-1}) = ∫ p(x_i | R, x_{1:i-1}, z) p(z | R, x_{1:i-1}) dz`, where `z` ranges over latent concepts in the LLM and `z*` is induced by retrieved texts.
- Two assumptions support the derivation: `p(x | h, z*) > c_1 > 0` for all hidden states, and delimiter-state transitions satisfy `0 <= c_2 <= p(h^d | h, z) <= c_3`.
- The fusion weight is rewritten as `v(z) = log ( p(R, x_{1:i-1} | z) / p(R, x_{1:i-1} | z*) ) ≈ -[KL(p_R(r) || p(r | z)) - KL(p_R(r) || p(r | z*))]`.
- The first KL term `Ω = KL(p_R(r) || p(r | z))` is defined as benefit, because it measures how much retrieved texts add out-of-distribution knowledge beyond the LLM's internal distribution. The second term `Υ = KL(p_R(r) || p(r | z*))` is defined as detriment, because it captures conflict between retrieved knowledge and model knowledge.
- Theorem 1 bounds `D = ||p(x_i | R, x_{1:i-1}) - p_R(x_i | x_{1:i-1})||_1` by `||Φ||_1 ± sqrt(2Υ)`, with `Φ ≈ α ∫ p(x_i | R, x_{1:i-1}, z) exp(-(Ω - Υ)) p(z) dz`, tying smaller distribution gap to larger net benefit.
- Theorem 2 states that `Ω - Υ` is approximately positively correlated with `1 / D`, so similarity between the RAG distribution and the retrieved-text distribution can proxy the benefit-minus-detriment gap.
- Theorem 3 introduces `M = ||p(x_i | R, x_{1:i-1}) - p(x_i | x_{1:i-1})||_1`. Benefit wins when `1 / D > 1 / M`, detriment wins when `1 / D < 1 / M`, and the boundary is `1 / D = 1 / M`.
- For practice, the paper approximates retrieved-text token distribution with `Att = softmax(((h_i^{l*} W_q)(h_{1:m}^{l*} W_k)^T) / sqrt(d_k))`, `WordSim = softmax((x_i^{l'-l*} A)(rt_{1:m}^{l*} A)^T)`, and `p_R(x_i | x_{1:i-1}) = softmax(Att ⊙ WordSim)`.
- Token comparison then uses weighted embedding representations: `w_RAG`, `w_IR`, and `w_LLM`, with similarity scores `cos(w_RAG, w_IR)` and `cos(w_RAG, w_LLM)`. Tok-RAG selects the RAG token if `cos(w_RAG, w_IR) >= cos(w_RAG, w_LLM)`; otherwise it keeps the pure-LLM token.
- Implementation uses parallel pure-LLM and RAG decoding, greedy generation in the benefit-detriment experiment, `ColBERTv2` retrieval over `21,015,324` Wikipedia passages, and open models including `OPT-6.7B`, `LLaMA-2-7B`, and `Mistral-7B-v0.1`.

## Key Results

- On short-form QA with noisy retrieval, Tok-RAG is consistently strongest. On TriviaQA it scores `53.5 / 72.9 / 77.6 / 81.3 / 83.4 / 85.7` accuracy as the hard-negative ratio drops from `100%` to `0%`; on WebQ it reaches `32.9` at `100%` noise and `57.3` at `0%`; on SQuAD it reaches `12.8` at `100%` noise and `68.1` at `0%`.
- On long-form and generative tasks, Tok-RAG outperforms the strongest baselines without extra training: dialogue F1 `9.68`, Python CodeBLEU `27.44`, Java CodeBLEU `32.59`, T-REx accuracy `68.70`, WikiText-103 ROUGE `64.28`, and ELI5 ROUGE `17.59`.
- On token-level benefit-vs-detriment prediction, Tok-RAG beats hallucination-based baselines across models and datasets. For `LLaMA-2-7B`, it achieves `81.89/80.42` AUC/F1 on WikiText, `76.96/76.80` on ASQA, and `70.50/72.45` on NQ; for `Mistral-7B`, it reaches `75.85/74.11` on WikiText.
- The added runtime cost is moderate but real: compared with Standard RAG, Tok-RAG increases GPU memory from `17.50 GB` to `23.98 GB`, latency from `1.95 s` to `2.24 s`, and raises accuracy from `51.96` to `56.12`.

## Limitations

- The theory depends on strong structural assumptions about latent-variable inference and bounded transition probabilities; the paper does not directly validate whether those assumptions hold for real transformers.
- The practical criterion needs white-box access to hidden states, attention projections, embeddings, and logits, so the method is not immediately applicable to closed API-only models.
- Empirical validation is centered on one retrieval setup (`ColBERTv2` + Wikipedia) and mostly open `7B`-scale LLMs, leaving transfer to other retrievers, corpora, multilingual settings, or stronger frontier models unclear.
- Tok-RAG requires parallel pure-LLM and RAG decoding, which increases memory and latency even if the overhead is smaller than the gains reported.
