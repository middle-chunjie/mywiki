## Summary

The paper studies robustness for deep-learning-based source code authorship attribution under both adversarial-example attacks and automatic coding-style manipulation. It introduces RoPGen, a training framework that combines data augmentation with gradient augmentation to force models to learn coding-style patterns that are harder to imitate or hide. The method formalizes 23 coding-style attributes across lexical, syntactic, and semantic levels, defines automatic targeted imitation and untargeted hiding attacks, and then trains enhanced attribution models using author-style imitation, controlled perturbations, and shared-weight subnetworks. Across four C/C++/Java datasets and two attribution models, RoPGen reduces the average success rate of targeted attacks by 22.8% and untargeted attacks by 41.0% while often maintaining or improving attribution accuracy.

## Problem & Motivation

Existing source code authorship attribution methods are useful for software forensics, plagiarism analysis, and maintenance tasks, but they are brittle under adversarial pressure. Prior work had already shown vulnerability to adversarial examples and style manipulation, yet defenses were either absent or too attack-specific. This paper argues that robust attribution requires moving beyond hand-crafted features and learning coding-style patterns that remain stable under semantics-preserving rewrites. The authors therefore target black-box attacks that can automatically imitate another programmer's style or hide the true author's style, and seek a defense that works across multiple neural attribution architectures rather than one special-case model.

## Method

- **Coding-style representation**: the paper systematizes `23` coding-style attributes over `4` granularities: token (`#1-#5`), statement (`#6-#19`), basic block (`#20-#22`), and function (`#23`). These attributes cover lexical, syntactic, and semantic signals used as a starting point for robust attribution.
- **Targeted imitation attack**: given authors `A = {A_1, ..., A_δ}`, attacker source program `p`, and reference programs `R` from target author `A_t`, the attacker extracts style values from `p` and `R`, synthesizes target-style values, identifies discrepant attributes, and applies semantics-preserving code transformations so that `M(p') = A_t` while `p' != p`.
- **Discrepancy rules**: for numeric attributes, discrepancy is triggered when the absolute gap exceeds threshold `` `τ` ``; for non-numeric attributes, discrepancy means the value derived from `p` is not a subset of the synthesized target-style value set.
- **Untargeted hiding attack**: for each alternative author `A_d in A - {A_s}`, the attacker computes discrepant attributes and selects the author `A_u` whose imitation would yield the highest misattribution probability, then transforms `p` toward `A_u` while preserving semantics.
- **Step 1, data augmentation by imitation**: for each training pair `(p_k, q_k)` and target-author set `T`, RoPGen generates programs imitating the other `δ - 1` authors, producing `P_1` and extended training set `` `U = P ∪ P_1` ``.
- **Step 2, manipulated programs by perturbation**: RoPGen builds `U'` either from adversarial-example transformation traces, giving `` `|U'| = |E| × |P|` ``, or by perturbing each of `z` attributes for each training program, giving `` `|U'| = z × |P|` ``.
- **Step 3, gradient augmentation**: at each iteration the method samples `n` subnetworks with widths `` `w_j ∈ [α, 1]` ``, computes `` `L_std = l(N(θ, u), v)` ``, computes `` `L_subnet = Σ_j l(N(θ_wj, u', v'))` ``, and optimizes total loss `` `L_RoPGen = L_std + L_subnet` ``. The resulting gradient decomposition is `` `g_RoPGen = g_std + g_subnet` ``.
- **Architectures and implementation**: evaluation uses DL-CAIS and PbNN with stratified cross-validation (`κ = 8` for GCJ-C++, `κ = 10` for GitHub-C, GCJ-Java, GitHub-Java), srcML for semantics-preserving rewrites, and `φ = 3` when generating adversarial examples.
- **Example hyperparameters**: for RoPGen-enabled DL-CAIS on GCJ-C++, the selected setting is `` `batch size = 128` ``, `` `learning rate = 1e-4` ``, `` `RNN layers = 3` ``, `` `α = 0.8` ``, and `` `n = 3` `` subnetworks.

## Key Results

- Baseline attribution accuracy is already high on some datasets: DL-CAIS reaches `88.2`, `79.9`, `98.5`, and `88.4` on GCJ-C++, GitHub-C, GCJ-Java, and GitHub-Java; PbNN reaches `84.8`, `76.7`, `86.2`, and `95.4`.
- RoPGen-enabled DL-CAIS improves or preserves accuracy at `92.1`, `84.9`, `98.5`, and `90.0` on the four datasets; RoPGen-enabled PbNN reaches `67.6`, `79.7`, `83.6`, and `86.1`.
- On unsecured models, targeted coding-style imitation attacks are strong: DL-CAIS is broken at `43.9`, `24.3`, `17.7`, and `45.1`; PbNN is broken at `36.8`, `18.4`, `21.0`, and `74.6`.
- On RoPGen-enabled models, targeted coding-style imitation attack success drops sharply to `3.4`, `1.3`, `0.7`, and `0.3` for DL-CAIS, and `6.3`, `7.2`, `0.6`, and `18.0` for PbNN.
- Untargeted hiding attacks are even stronger on baseline models, reaching up to `95.0`; after RoPGen, untargeted hiding attack success becomes `15.0`, `12.4`, `10.9`, and `4.2` for DL-CAIS, and `35.0`, `11.6`, `25.0`, and `25.7` for PbNN.
- Averaged over models and datasets, RoPGen reduces targeted attack success by `22.8%` and untargeted attack success by `41.0%`.
- Attack generation by coding-style imitation is much cheaper than adversarial-example search on GCJ-C++ with DL-CAIS: about `1.5` seconds versus `2417` seconds per manipulated program.
- Ablation on GCJ-C++ shows the full method is materially stronger than partial variants: baseline DL-CAIS has `(22.2, 87.7, 43.9, 94.8)` for `(adv-targeted, adv-untargeted, imitation-targeted, hiding-untargeted)`, while RoPGen yields `(19.4, 58.3, 3.4, 15.0)`.

## Limitations

- The study focuses on **single-authorship attribution** and does not validate the method on multi-authorship settings.
- Evaluation covers only **two open-source, language-agnostic attribution models**; robustness on other architectures or language-specific systems remains open.
- The framework depends on **semantics-preserving transformations**, but the paper does not provide a rigorous formal proof that all transformations are sound.
- The attack evaluation centers on **automatic black-box style imitation/hiding** and does not test whether skilled human manual mimicry would be more effective.
- Robustness gains come with a **substantial training-time cost**: for example, PbNN training on GCJ-C++ grows from `65.5` seconds to `5876` seconds.
- Threat-model adequacy is acknowledged as unresolved; the paper explicitly notes that stronger or different attacker assumptions may change the conclusions.
