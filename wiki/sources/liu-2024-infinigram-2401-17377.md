## Summary

The paper revisits classical `n`-gram language modeling at modern pretraining scale and argues that it remains useful when both the datastore and the context length are expanded aggressively. It introduces the `∞`-gram LM, which chooses the longest suffix of the prompt observed in a trillion-token corpus, and an accompanying on-disk suffix-array engine, infini-gram, that serves arbitrary-`n` counting and next-token queries with sub-second latency. Across analyses on human and machine text, `∞`-gram achieves strong next-token agreement and exposes memorization and decoding artifacts in neural LMs. When interpolated with neural models, it substantially lowers perplexity, showing that large nonparametric counts can still complement contemporary LLMs.

## Problem & Motivation

Classical `n`-gram LMs are usually dismissed because they are trained on much smaller corpora than modern neural LMs and because fixed small `n` discards long-range context. This paper asks whether that conclusion is partly an artifact of scale and implementation. The authors argue that if `n`-gram methods are trained on 360B to 1.4T tokens and allowed to use the longest matching suffix instead of a fixed context window, they can become useful again for corpus analysis and as a complementary nonparametric component for neural language modeling. The practical obstacle is indexing arbitrary-length `n`-grams at this scale without precomputing an infeasible count table.

## Method

- **`n`-gram baseline**: estimate `P_n(w_i | w_{i-(n-1):i-1}) = cnt(w_{i-(n-1):i-1} w_i | D) / cnt(w_{i-(n-1):i-1} | D)` and avoid the fixed-small-`n` bottleneck by letting context length vary with the observed suffix.
- **`∞`-gram LM**: define the effective order as `n = max { n' in [1, i] | cnt(w_{i-(n'-1):i-1} | D) > 0 }`, then compute `P_∞(w_i | w_{1:i-1})` with that longest non-zero suffix. The paper backs off only until the denominator becomes positive, which keeps `P_∞(* | context)` a valid distribution without Katz-style discounting.
- **Interpolation with neural LMs**: combine models as `P(y | x) = λ P_∞(y | x) + (1 - λ) P_neural(y | x)`. For perplexity experiments, the authors use separate weights `λ_1` and `λ_2` for sparse vs. non-sparse `∞`-gram estimates, tuned on validation data.
- **Suffix-array index**: instead of explicit count tables, infini-gram stores tokenized data as a byte array with `2` bytes per token, plus a suffix array with `5` bytes per pointer for corpora in the `2B` to `500B` token range, for a total footprint of `7N` bytes. Documents are prefixed with a `4`-byte document ID and separated by `0xffff`.
- **Index construction and sharding**: build suffix arrays over sharded byte arrays so trillion-token corpora fit memory constraints. The RedPajama index (`1.4T` tokens) is reported as built in about `56` hours on one node with `80` CPUs and `512 GB` RAM.
- **Query algorithms**: count an `n`-gram with binary search over the suffix-array segment, giving `O(log N)` random-access complexity in their accounting; compute `∞`-gram with binary lifting and binary search over suffix length for `O(log L · log N)`, and amortize dense left-to-right evaluation to `O(log N)` per token on consecutive text.
- **On-disk serving optimizations**: keep both the byte array and suffix array memory-mapped on SSD, parallelize shard processing, and use memory prefetching to reduce random-access latency by about `5x`.
- **Evaluation setup**: analyze agreement on decontaminated Pile validation/test data, compare against multiple neural LM families, and also test time-shifted Wikipedia articles created after the cutoff of Pile and RedPajama.

## Key Results

- Human-written text: `∞`-gram agrees with the actual next token on `47%` of tokens overall; when effective `n >= 16`, agreement exceeds `75%`.
- Sparse `∞`-gram cases are especially predictive: they cover more than `50%` of tokens and reach `75%` overall agreement, exceeding `80%` when effective `n >= 14`.
- Latency remains practical at trillion-token scale: counting arbitrary `n`-grams on RedPajama takes about `18-20 ms`, `∞`-gram token probability takes `135 ms`, and full `∞`-gram next-token distribution takes `180 ms`.
- Interpolation consistently reduces perplexity on the Pile: LLaMA-2 `70B` improves from `4.65` to `4.20` on test with Pile only, and to `3.95` with `Pile + RPJ`, a `19%` relative gap reduction.
- Smaller or mismatched models benefit even more: GPT-2 `117M` test perplexity drops from `22.86` to `13.58` (`42%` improvement), while SILO on Enron Emails improves from `15.71` to `4.85`, closing `73%` of the gap.
- Time-shifted Wikipedia still shows gains after corpus cutoff: simple interpolation improves LLaMA-2 `13B` by `0-6%` across months, and a Random Forest gating scheme raises that to `3-20%`.

## Limitations

- The standalone `∞`-gram LM is too sparse for direct perplexity evaluation; the strongest results depend on interpolation with neural LMs rather than replacing them.
- Infrastructure cost is substantial: the method relies on 360B to 1.8T tokens, multi-terabyte on-disk indexes, and large CPU nodes for construction and serving.
- The analysis is heavily tied to English web-scale corpora such as the Pile and RedPajama, so cross-lingual and domain-shift behavior is only partially tested.
- Some interpretive claims are observational rather than causal, especially the hypothesis that periodic agreement fluctuations under greedy decoding come from Transformer positional embeddings.
- The authors explicitly note that interpolation can be harmful for open-ended generation because `∞`-gram may retrieve locally plausible but globally irrelevant tokens.
