## Summary

This paper introduces REALTIME QA, a dynamic open-domain question answering benchmark and evaluation platform built around weekly news quizzes. Instead of assuming answers are static, the task asks systems to respond to current-world questions whose answers can change from week to week. The benchmark publishes about `30` multiple-choice questions every Saturday, provides optional Google Custom Search evidence, and evaluates six real-time baselines spanning GPT-3, T5, RAG, and DPR-based systems. Across `1,470` real-time QA pairs collected from June 2022 to June 2023, the strongest system is open-book GPT-3 with fresh web retrieval, showing that up-to-date retrieval matters more than static parametric knowledge. The paper's main value is reframing QA evaluation around temporal freshness, retrieval latency, and uncertainty handling.

## Problem & Motivation

Static QA benchmarks usually assume that a question has the same answer regardless of when it is asked. That assumption fails for real-world information needs such as sports scores, political events, business news, or public-health developments, where the correct answer depends on current events. The paper targets this gap by defining a benchmark whose questions are generated from newly published news articles and whose evaluation window is explicitly time-bounded. This setup is meant to stress both temporal generalization and the ability of a QA system to incorporate newly retrieved evidence, while still supporting realistic downstream uses such as emergency response and rapidly changing news monitoring.

## Method

- **Weekly benchmark loop**: every Saturday at `3 am GMT`, the platform releases about `30` multiple-choice questions extracted from weekly quizzes on CNN, USA Today, and The Week, yielding about `120` questions per month.
- **Document collection**: each question is sent to Google Custom Search to retrieve top-`10` candidate web documents; the shared document pool used by baselines is mostly news articles from the three source sites.
- **Dynamic submission protocol**: model submissions are accepted until the next weekly release, so evaluation depends on what information was available during that window rather than on a frozen corpus.
- **Open-book retrieval variants**: the benchmark compares top-`5` documents from Google Custom Search with top-`5` Wikipedia passages from DPR. DPR uses `768`-dimensional dense vectors over `100`-word chunks from the December 2018 English Wikipedia dump.
- **Answer prediction models**: the open-book baselines pair retrieval with either RAG-sequence or GPT-3 (`text-davinci-002`); the closed-book baselines use T5-11B finetuned on Natural Questions and closed-book GPT-3 prompting.
- **RAG formulation**: the generative baseline marginalizes over retrieved documents with `P(y) = Σ_{z∈Z} P(z) ∏_{t=1}^{|y|} P(y_t | z, y_{≤t})`, where `|Z| = 5` and `P(z)` comes from retrieval scores.
- **Prompt design**: the GPT-3 open-book prompt prepends the title plus first two paragraphs of each top-`5` article and inserts temporal metadata such as `Article on November 2, 2021`; for DPR evidence, the prompt inserts `December 31, 2018` to reflect the dump date.
- **Evaluation settings**: the benchmark reports original multiple-choice accuracy, a NOTA setting where one option is replaced by `none of the above`, and free-generation metrics using exact match and token-level `F1`.
- **Implementation details**: when Google Custom Search returns fewer than `5` articles (about `10%` of cases), the system backfills the evidence set with DPR results; Appendix A reports RAG settings such as `n_docs = 5`, `retrieval_vector_size = 768`, `retrieval_batch_size = 8`, and `max_combined_length = 300`.

## Key Results

- The benchmark reports `1,470` real-time QA pairs from June 17, 2022 to June 2, 2023, plus `2,886` earlier QA pairs released for later model development and finetuning.
- Human performance on a `300`-question sample reaches `96.7%` accuracy with Google search.
- The best real-time baseline is open-book GPT-3 with Google Custom Search: `66.5` accuracy on original multiple choice, `58.4` on NOTA, `34.6` exact match, and `45.3` token `F1`.
- Fresh web retrieval matters substantially: GPT-3 with GCS beats GPT-3 with DPR on original multiple choice (`66.5` vs. `43.9`) and beats closed-book GPT-3 on generation exact match (`34.6` vs. `15.3`).
- RAG also benefits from fresh retrieval: GCS+RAG reaches `46.9` original accuracy versus `27.4` for DPR+RAG.
- Error analysis of the best generation system shows `52%` correct answers, `34%` retrieval errors, and `13%` reading-comprehension errors, making retrieval the dominant failure mode.
- Submission timing matters: performance stays strong for roughly the first `24` hours after question release, then degrades as temporal gaps between query time and retrieved evidence widen.
- Date insertion helps the open-book GPT-3 prompt: over the first six weeks, adding dates to both questions and articles yields `69.3` original accuracy versus `65.9` without any dates.

## Limitations

- The benchmark is English-only and largely English-news-centric, with source material restricted to CNN, USA Today, and The Week.
- Questions are released weekly rather than continuously, so the platform is only a coarse approximation to truly instantaneous QA.
- Retrieval quality remains the main bottleneck; when retrieved documents are stale or incomplete, even the best GPT-3 system often outputs outdated answers instead of abstaining.
- A non-trivial minority of questions are not strictly time-sensitive (`~10%` to `12%`), so the benchmark mixes genuinely temporal QA with event-triggered but stable facts.
- The paper presents strong baselines but not a fully solved uncertainty mechanism for saying that available evidence is insufficient.
