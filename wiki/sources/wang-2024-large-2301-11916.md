## Summary

The paper argues that in-context learning can be understood as approximate Bayesian inference over a latent concept variable that encodes task and format information. Under this view, good demonstrations are examples that most strongly induce the task-specific latent concept. The authors operationalize this with a two-stage pipeline: first learn task-specific concept tokens by prompt tuning a small language model, then rank candidate demonstrations by the posterior probability of those learned tokens and transfer the selected examples to larger models. Across eight text classification datasets and multiple GPT-family models, the method improves average 4-shot accuracy over uniform and similarity-based baselines, and it also yields gains on GSM8K. The main contribution is a practical bridge between latent-variable theory and real-world demonstration selection.

## Problem & Motivation

Few-shot in-context learning is highly sensitive to which demonstrations are shown, yet prior explanations of this behavior either rely on synthetic settings, unrealistic assumptions such as Hidden Markov data generation, or heuristics like semantic similarity that do not explain why some examples transfer well across models. This paper aims to close that gap by giving a more realistic latent-variable account of in-context learning and deriving a demonstration selection algorithm from it. The authors want a method that can be trained cheaply with a small LM but still produce demonstrations that generalize to larger LLMs without further tuning.

## Method

- **Latent-variable view of prompting**: the continuation distribution is approximated as `P_M(w_{t+1:T} | w_{1:t}) = \int_\Theta P_M(w_{t+1:T} | \theta) P_M(\theta | w_{1:t}) d\theta`, where `\theta` is a latent concept variable capturing task and prompt-format information.
- **Task causal structure**: the theory covers both `X -> Y <- \theta` and `Y -> X <- \theta` directions, choosing the direction that best matches the task; the Bayes-optimal classifier is recovered when the posterior over demonstrations concentrates on the task latent `\theta^d`.
- **Latent concept learning**: for each task `d`, add `c` new concept tokens `\hat{\theta}^d` to the LM vocabulary and freeze all original model parameters; only the new embeddings are optimized with loss `\mathcal{L}(\hat{\theta}^d) = \mathbb{E}[\ell(X, Y; \hat{\theta}^d)]`.
- **Direct vs. channel objective**: use `-\log P_M^d(Y | \hat{\theta}^d, X)` for `X -> Y <- \theta`, and `-\log P_M^d(X | \hat{\theta}^d, Y)` for `Y -> X <- \theta`.
- **Posterior approximation over tasks**: after training on a diverse task subset `\mathcal{S}`, approximate the task posterior with `\hat{P}_{M'}^d(\hat{\theta}^d | w_{1:t}) = P_{M'}^d(\hat{\theta}^d | w_{1:t}) / \sum_{t \in \mathcal{S}} P_{M'}^t(\hat{\theta}^t | w_{1:t})`.
- **Demonstration selection rule**: score each candidate example by `\hat{P}_{M'}^d(\hat{\theta}^d | X_i^d, Y_i^d)` and select the top `k` examples; under the independence approximation this greedily maximizes the posterior that the demonstrations imply the task concept.
- **Main hyperparameters**: default experiments use `k = 4` demonstrations and `c = 10` concept tokens per dataset; demonstrations are chosen from a random candidate pool of `100` training examples.
- **Training setup**: concept tokens are learned with GPT2-large using learning rate `1e-4`, batch size `16`, and `10k` optimization steps; per dataset they train on up to `16,384` examples and evaluate on up to `1,000` test examples over `5` random-seed runs.
- **Transfer protocol**: the same demonstrations selected by a small LM are directly reused on larger LLMs, motivated by the assumption that related models share sufficiently similar pretraining distributions.

## Key Results

- On the eight text-classification datasets, average 4-shot accuracy rises from `57.9` (uniform) and `61.4` (similarity baseline) to `65.0` with the proposed method, a reported `12.5%` relative gain over uniform selection.
- With GPT2-large as the evaluation model, average accuracy improves from `57.4` (uniform) and `59.7` (similar) to `64.8` (ours).
- With GPT3-curie (`6.7B`), average accuracy improves from `62.3` (uniform) and `66.8` (similar) to `69.2` (ours), supporting transfer from GPT2-large-selected demonstrations to larger GPT-family models.
- Dataset-level gains include GPT2-large on SST2 `77.1 -> 86.2`, FPB `51.3 -> 60.4`, COLA `62.7 -> 69.1`, and DBpedia `54.4 -> 56.5` when moving from uniform to the proposed method.
- On GSM8K with 4-shot inference, ChatGPT reaches `81.2` with demonstrations selected by the method using Llama 2 `7B`, compared with `76.5` for uniform and `78.1` for similarity-based selection.
- On GSM8K, Llama 2 `13B` improves from `17.0` (uniform) and `18.3` (similar) to `21.6` with demonstrations selected by the proposed method.
- The `k` ablation shows gains for `k = 2, 4, 8, 16`; the largest reported average improvement is at `k = 4`, where GPT3-ada rises from `56.8` to `63.4`.
- The `c` ablation shows `c = 10` concept tokens works best on average (`64.2`), outperforming `c = 5` (`56.6`), `c = 15` (`62.3`), and `c = 20` (`60.92`).

## Limitations

- The core theory assumes the LM approximates the true language distribution well enough for Bayesian arguments to hold; the paper itself notes this is only approximate in practice.
- Demonstration selection relies on an independence approximation over examples, which avoids `O(|\mathcal{D}|^k)` combinatorial search but may miss interaction effects between demonstrations.
- The learned concept-token mechanism is trained on a specific LM, so the latent-prefix classifier itself is not transferable across models; only the selected demonstrations are.
- The approach is primarily designed for classification tasks, with GSM8K included as an additional case study rather than the main theoretical target.
- Performance is not uniformly strong across all models and datasets: variance is high on EmoC, and transfer to some non-GPT models such as OPT and LLaMA is modest.
- Constructing the diverse task set `\mathcal{S}` is left largely to the user, making the method sensitive to upstream task coverage and task-set design.
