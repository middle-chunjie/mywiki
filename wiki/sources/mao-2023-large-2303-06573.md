## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature.

LLM4CS studies whether a prompted [[large-language-model]] can serve as a contextual search-intent interpreter for [[conversational-search]]. Given a multi-turn interaction, it generates multiple standalone rewrites and hypothetical responses, embeds them with ANCE, and aggregates them into a single intent vector for [[passage-retrieval]]. The paper compares three prompting variants, three aggregation strategies, and a tailored [[chain-of-thought-prompting]] design. Using `gpt-3.5-turbo-16k` in a few-shot setting, LLM4CS substantially outperforms strong [[query-rewriting]] and [[conversational-dense-retrieval]] baselines on CAsT-19/20/21, and on many metrics even exceeds retrieval based on human rewrites. The main claim is that hypothetical responses plus multi-sample aggregation provide a more robust representation of long-tail conversational search intent than relying on a single rewrite.

## Problem & Motivation

Conversational search queries are often elliptical, coreferential, and dependent on earlier system responses, so the current utterance alone under-specifies the user's real information need. Existing [[query-rewriting]] and [[conversational-dense-retrieval]] methods are trained on relatively limited datasets and degrade on longer, more diverse, and more response-dependent sessions such as later CAsT turns. The paper asks whether a prompted [[large-language-model]], without task-specific fine-tuning, can infer contextual search intent more robustly by generating richer textual interpretations of the current turn and then mapping them into an intent representation usable by a downstream retriever.

## Method

- **Task setup**: conversational passage retrieval takes current query `q^t` and history `C^t = (q^1, r^1, ..., q^{t-1}, r^{t-1})`, then retrieves passages relevant to the user's latent contextual intent.
- **LLM4CS framework**: prompt an LLM to act as a text-based intent interpreter, producing short rewrites `q_hat` and optionally hypothetical responses `r_hat`, then map them into a shared dense space with retriever `f(·)`.
- **Prompting variants**: REW generates only rewrites; RTR first generates a rewrite then conditions on it to generate hypothetical responses; RAR generates rewrite and response jointly in one prompt.
- **Aggregation methods**: MaxProb uses the highest-probability generation, e.g. `s = f(q_hat_1)` for REW or `s = (f(q_hat_1) + f(r_hat_11)) / 2` for RTR/RAR.
- **Self-consistency aggregation**: select the vector closest to the cluster center, e.g. for REW `q* = (1/N) Σ_i f(q_hat_i)` and `s = argmax_{f(q_hat_i)} f(q_hat_i)^T q*`; RTR/RAR then combine the selected rewrite and response vectors.
- **Mean aggregation**: average all generated vectors, e.g. REW uses `s = (1/N) Σ_i f(q_hat_i)` and RTR/RAR use `s = [Σ_i (f(q_hat_i) + Σ_j f(r_hat_ij))] / [N * (1 + M)]`.
- **Chain-of-thought prompting**: demonstrations include manually written rationales explaining how the current turn's search intent should be inferred from prior turns, then the model first emits CoT before rewrite/response generation.
- **Retriever and generation details**: the retriever is ANCE pretrained on MSMARCO; the LLM is `gpt-3.5-turbo-16k` with temperature `0.7`; demonstrations come from 3 randomly selected CAsT-22 conversations.
- **Hyperparameters**: REW uses `N = 5`; RTR uses `N = 1, M = 5`; RAR uses `N = 5, M = 1`; query/rewrite length is truncated to `64`, and passage/response length to `256`.
- **Evaluation**: benchmarks are CAsT-19, CAsT-20, and CAsT-21; metrics are `MRR`, `NDCG@3`, and `Recall@100`, with paired `t`-tests at `p < 0.05`.

## Key Results

- **Best overall system**: LLM4CS with `RAR + Mean + CoT` reaches `MRR 0.776 / NDCG@3 0.515 / R@100 0.372` on CAsT-19, `0.615 / 0.455 / 0.489` on CAsT-20, and `0.681 / 0.492 / 0.614` on CAsT-21.
- **Improvement over the second-best baseline**: relative gains on CAsT-20 are `+20.6% MRR`, `+30.0% NDCG@3`, `+32.9% R@100`; on CAsT-21 they are `+18.8%`, `+27.8%`, and `+26.6%`.
- **Versus human rewrites**: LLM4CS beats human-rewrite retrieval on most metrics, including all three metrics on CAsT-20 and `MRR` plus `R@100` on CAsT-21, while trailing slightly on CAsT-19 `R@100` (`0.372` vs `0.381`) and CAsT-21 `NDCG@3` (`0.492` vs `0.502`).
- **Prompting ablation**: adding hypothetical responses helps; even REW alone reaches `NDCG@3 0.380` on CAsT-20 and `0.465` on CAsT-21, already above the reported previous SOTA of `0.350` and `0.385`.
- **Aggregation ablation**: Mean and self-consistency consistently outperform MaxProb, indicating that multi-sample aggregation is more reliable than trusting the top LLM output.
- **Human evaluation**: LLM4CS rewrites are judged correct or acceptable on `85.5%` of CAsT-19, `89.4%` of CAsT-20, and `84.8%` of CAsT-21 turns; coreference errors are below `3%`, versus roughly `10%` for T5QR.

## Limitations

- The framework is latency-heavy because it may invoke the LLM multiple times per turn to generate several rewrites and hypothetical responses.
- Generation is not directly optimized with downstream ranking signals, so it inherits the classic gap between generation quality and retrieval utility.
- The main experiments depend on a specific stack, `gpt-3.5-turbo-16k` plus ANCE, so transfer to stronger or weaker LLMs/retrievers is not established here.
- Evaluation is centered on three CAsT benchmarks for conversational passage retrieval, leaving broader validation across other conversational IR settings and production constraints open.
- The tailored chain-of-thought demonstrations are manually designed, which may limit portability and add prompt-engineering overhead.
