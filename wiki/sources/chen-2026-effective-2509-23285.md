## Summary

This paper studies tool-integrated reasoning in settings where a model may call multiple external tools during a reasoning chain and often fails through underuse, overuse, or post-tool overthinking. The authors first analyze multi-step tool use through token-level information entropy and show that tool outputs induce characteristic entropy fluctuations, while lower-entropy correct trajectories often use fewer tools. They then propose Tool-Light, a post-training framework that combines entropy-guided trajectory sampling with a two-stage training pipeline: supervised fine-tuning followed by self-evolved direct preference optimization. Across 10 reasoning benchmarks, Tool-Light improves average performance, efficiency, and necessity of tool use, with the Qwen-based variant reaching an overall average of `58.0` and outperforming Tool-Star on both aggregate quality and tool-use effectiveness.

## Problem & Motivation

Existing tool-integrated reasoning systems often call tools too often, too rarely, or continue reasoning inefficiently after receiving tool outputs. Prior work frequently optimizes only one tool or only the tool-overuse side of the problem, leaving multi-tool settings and tool-underuse less resolved. This paper argues that effective tool use should jointly optimize correctness, necessity of calling tools, and efficiency of the resulting reasoning chain. The authors therefore look for a post-training strategy that can improve multi-tool behavior without requiring bespoke RL rewards for each tool combination. Their central hypothesis is that entropy patterns in reasoning traces expose where tool use destabilizes or clarifies reasoning, and that these patterns can guide both data construction and preference learning.

## Method

- **Problem formulation**: multi-tool TIR models the final answer as `y ~ P_theta(y | R_N) * prod_{i=1}^{N} P_theta(R_i | T_{C<i}, R_{<i}, I)`, where `R_i` is the reasoning content at step `i`, `T_{C<i}` are previous tool results, and `I` is the instruction.
- **Entropy analysis**: token entropy is computed as `H(i) = -sum_j P(y_{ji} | y_{<i}) log P(y_{ji} | y_{<i})`; the branch-selection signal uses average prefix entropy `H_avg(i) = (1 / i) sum_{j=1}^{i} H(j)`. The paper measures `H_avg(i)` over the first `10`, `20`, `30`, `40`, and `50` tokens of each reasoning step.
- **Source-data construction**: starting from Tool-Star SFT data `D_sft`, the authors fine-tune an instruct model `M_sft`, run inference without tools, and keep only failed items: `D_source = D_sft[y_label != y_inference]`. This biases training toward questions the model cannot already solve internally.
- **Dual sampling strategy**: vanilla rollout sampling builds `D_dpo^1`, while entropy-guided tree sampling builds `D_dpo^2` by branching at the top-`k` highest-entropy steps of a main chain. The paper states an ideal complexity drop from `O(mn)` to `O(n log m)` for `m` rollouts and average length `n`; Appendix B reports the final mixing ratio as `13:7` between the two data types.
- **Two-stage TIR training**: Tool-Light first performs SFT with `L_SFT(theta) = -sum_{(x, y) in D} log P_theta(y | x)`, then runs self-evolved DPO with `L_DPO = -E[log sigma(beta log (pi_theta(y_w | x) / pi_ref(y_w | x)) - beta log (pi_theta(y_l | x) / pi_ref(y_l | x))))]`.
- **Pre-aligned DPO stage**: in entropy-guided sampling, hard samples have `<= 50%` correct trajectories and easy samples `>= 50%`; in vanilla sampling the thresholds are `<= 40%` and `>= 70%`. Positive examples are shortest or lowest-entropy correct trajectories, negatives are longer incorrect ones, and the hard/easy ratio is `2:1`.
- **Self-evolved DPO alignment**: after the first DPO round, the model resamples its own data. In easy cases, positives are correct trajectories with fewer tool calls and lower entropy while negatives are incorrect trajectories with the most tool calls; in hard cases, positives are the longest correct chains and negatives are the shortest incorrect chains.
- **Implementation details**: training uses LoRA with rank `8`, `3` epochs, learning rate `7e-6`, and batch size `8`. Inference uses temperature `1` and caps each tool to `4` calls. The knowledge-intensive setup uses `E5-base-v2` with Wikipedia retrieval under [[flashrag]] settings, while math tasks use [[bing-web-search]] plus a web-browser mechanism.

## Key Results

- On the 10-dataset average, Tool-Light (Qwen) reaches `58.0`, exceeding Tool-Star's `56.6`, ReTool's `47.3`, Search-R1's `45.6`, ReCall's `43.2`, and prompting-based multi-TIR's `35.3`.
- The Qwen-based model achieves `33.3` on AIME24, `67.5` on AMC23, `87.4` on MATH, `92.0` on GSM8K, `57.7` on HotpotQA, `56.1` on 2WikiMultiHopQA, `25.0` on MuSiQue, and `58.7` on Bamboogle.
- The framework improves progressively across training stages: prompting `35.3`, SFT `56.6`, pre-aligned DPO `57.4`, and self-evolved DPO with `2` loops `58.0`.
- Tool-use metrics favor Tool-Light as well: the main system reports `Efficiency = 0.44` and `Necessity = 0.75`, outperforming ablations with random positive selection (`53.6`, `0.42`, `0.63`) or random negative selection (`53.9`, `0.41`, `0.74`).
- The self-evolution loop count matters: `1` loop gives `57.9`, but `3`, `4`, and `5` loops drop to `56.1`, `56.4`, and `54.1`, suggesting overfitting or exhaustion of useful preference pairs after the second round.

## Limitations

- The framework is validated with only two tool types, a code interpreter and a search tool, so its behavior for richer tool ecosystems is not directly established.
- The main gains depend heavily on the Qwen backbone; the Llama-based Tool-Light variant averages only `36.8`, indicating that the method is not uniformly robust across models.
- Entropy-guided sampling is motivated empirically rather than by a formal optimality argument, and the claimed complexity benefit is presented as an ideal-case estimate.
- Performance declines after more self-evolution loops, implying that the method can run out of useful positive-negative pairs and overfit to its own generated data.
- Evaluation is benchmark-centric and mostly offline; the paper does not study persistent agent memory, interactive environments, or real open-web tool failures in deployment conditions.
