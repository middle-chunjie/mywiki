## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature.

The paper proposes Fine-grained Retrieval Prompt Tuning (FRPT), a parameter-efficient fine-grained object retrieval method that freezes a pre-trained ResNet backbone and adapts it through input-space prompting plus lightweight feature adaptation. The discriminative perturbation prompt (DPP) learns a spatial projection map that exaggerates category-relevant image regions via content-aware inhomogeneous sampling, while the category-specific awareness head (CAH) uses channel-attention-guided instance normalization to suppress inter-species variation that is unhelpful for subcategory retrieval. Trained with cross-entropy alone, FRPT uses about `2.86M` learnable parameters instead of fully tuning `23.53M+` backbone parameters, yet reports new best Recall@K results on CUB-200-2011, Stanford Cars, and FGVC Aircraft, with especially strong gains in few-shot regimes.

## Problem & Motivation

Fine-grained object retrieval has to separate visually similar subcategories under small inter-class differences and large intra-class variance. Prior localization-based and metric-learning systems improved discrimination, but they typically kept fine-tuning the entire backbone on relatively small fine-grained datasets. The authors argue that this continual full-model adaptation hurts general visual generalization and leads to suboptimal solutions, especially in limited-data settings. Their goal is to retain the representation quality of a frozen large pre-trained vision model while adding minimal task-specific parameters that steer the model toward subcategory-sensitive retrieval.

## Method

- **Overall pipeline**: FRPT composes a discriminative perturbation prompt `DPP`, a frozen pre-trained backbone, and a category-specific awareness head `CAH`. Given image `I`, the prompt module produces a modified image `I_P`, the frozen backbone extracts semantic features `M_P`, and CAH outputs category-specific features `M_R` that are globally pooled for retrieval.
- **Content parsing for DPP**: low-level features `M_S \in \mathbb{R}^{C_S \times H_S \times W_S}` from the frozen backbone's first block are mapped to a discriminative projection map `A \in \mathbb{R}^{H_S \times W_S}`. Each location uses a shared content-aware kernel `W_k \in \mathbb{R}^{\sigma \times \sigma \times C_S}` with kernel size `\sigma = 31`, and the map is spatially normalized by softmax so `\sum_{i,j} A_{ij} = 1`.
- **Discriminative content modification**: the prompt image is generated by `I_P = S(I, A)`, where `S` is a content-aware inhomogeneous sampler. The horizontal and vertical mapping functions `M_x(x)` and `M_y(y)` are weighted by `A` and regularized with a Gaussian distance kernel, then a differentiable bilinear sampler interpolates the final pixel values. This effectively zooms and exaggerates discriminative regions instead of simply cropping the object.
- **Category-specific awareness head**: CAH mixes raw semantic features and instance-normalized features using channel attention: `M_R = W_C \cdot M_P + (1 - W_C) \cdot IN(M_P)`. The channel weights are `W_C = \Delta(W_L \delta(W_F g(M_P)))`, where the reduction ratio is `r = 8`. The design aims to remove species-level discrepancies while preserving subcategory-specific evidence.
- **Optimization objective**: the network is trained only with cross-entropy, `L = -\log P(y \mid C(g(M_R) \mid \theta))`. Gradients update only DPP and CAH; the backbone remains frozen throughout training.
- **Backbone and training setup**: experiments use ImageNet-pretrained `ResNet-50` and `ResNet-101`. Images are resized to `256 x 256`, cropped to `224 x 224`, and trained with SGD using learning rate `1e-3`, momentum `0.9`, weight decay `1e-4`, batch size `32`, `500` epochs, and exponential decay factor `0.9` every `50` epochs. Data augmentation includes random crop/erase, horizontal flip, and color jitter.
- **Evaluation protocol**: retrieval is measured with cosine-distance `Recall@K` on CUB-200-2011, Stanford Cars, and FGVC Aircraft, plus few-shot variants using `10-shot` and `5-shot` training subsets on CUB.

## Key Results

- On the ablation benchmark, full FRPT (`DPP + PT + CAH`) reaches `74.3` Recall@1 on CUB and `91.1` on Cars, versus frozen pre-training at `44.9 / 58.9` and full fine-tuning at `69.5 / 84.4`.
- The full model uses only `2.86M` learnable parameters, roughly `12%` of the `23.53M` parameters updated by full fine-tuning, yet beats fine-tuning by `+4.8` points on CUB and `+6.7` on Cars in Table 1.
- Against prior SOTA methods, FRPT reports CUB `74.3 / 83.7 / 89.8 / 94.3`, Stanford Cars `91.1 / 95.1 / 97.3 / 98.6`, and FGVC Aircraft `77.6 / 85.7 / 91.4 / 95.6` for `Recall@{1,2,4,8}`.
- With a larger frozen `ResNet-101`, FRPT still keeps about `2.9M` trainable parameters and improves Recall@1 to `75.6` on CUB, `90.4` on Cars, and `76.2` on Aircraft, while corresponding full fine-tuning gives `70.9`, `85.1`, and `69.7`.
- In few-shot CUB experiments, FRPT outperforms fine-tuning for both backbones: with `ResNet-50`, `66.6` vs `63.1` in `10-shot` and `62.8` vs `59.7` in `5-shot`; with `ResNet-101`, `68.9` vs `64.7` in `10-shot` and `65.2` vs `61.5` in `5-shot`.
- Learnable DPP also beats fixed prompt baselines on CUB Recall@1: `74.3` for DPP, versus `67.6` for bounding-box cropping and `63.7` for CAM-based prompting.

## Limitations

- The evidence is confined to three fine-grained visual retrieval datasets and ResNet backbones; the paper does not test whether the approach transfers to transformer backbones, large-scale open-world retrieval, or non-vision modalities.
- The central claim that freezing avoids suboptimal solutions is plausible but mostly supported empirically rather than through direct optimization analysis; no diagnostic study compares feature drift or representation collapse during full fine-tuning.
- DPP introduces a global content-aware sampling step with large-kernel spatial weighting, but the paper does not quantify training/inference latency, memory overhead, or sensitivity to the Gaussian regularizer and kernel size.
- The method is trained for `500` epochs on relatively small datasets and relies on classification supervision, so its robustness under noisier labels, unseen meta-categories, or cross-dataset retrieval is unresolved.
