## Summary

The paper argues that generative retrieval (GR) can be understood not merely as an alternative to dense retrieval, but as a special case of [[multi-vector-retrieval]]. By analyzing the decoder cross-attention and prediction head of encoder-decoder GR models, the authors show that GR and MVDR both score a query-document pair through token-level interactions weighted by an alignment matrix. The key difference is not the overall scoring form, but how document representations and alignments are instantiated: GR uses lightweight document token embeddings plus a dense learned document-to-query alignment, whereas MVDR uses contextualized document vectors with sparse query-to-document alignment. Experiments on NQ320K and MS MARCO support this view, showing shared term-matching behavior, improved GR performance with stronger document encoding, and a persistent reranking gap that favors MVDR's alignment direction.

## Problem & Motivation

Generative retrieval has become an important end-to-end retrieval paradigm, but its internal relevance computation remains poorly understood relative to conventional dense retrieval. Earlier work had shown that GR with atomic identifiers can mimic single-vector dense retrieval, yet this only explains a narrow case and largely ignores the decoder's deeper interactions for multi-token identifiers. The paper targets this gap by asking how general GR systems relate to dense retrieval when the decoder, attention structure, and identifier design are taken seriously. The motivation is practical as well as conceptual: if GR shares a common relevance-computation substrate with state-of-the-art MVDR, then techniques for improving document encoding, token alignment, and inference may transfer across paradigms, and current weaknesses of GR can be diagnosed more precisely.

## Method

- The paper adopts the unified MVDR scoring form `rel(d, q) = (1 / Z) sum(D^T Q ⊙ A) = (1 / Z) Σ_{i,j} d_i^T q_j A_ij`, where `D` and `Q` are document/query token vectors and `A` is an alignment matrix controlling which token pairs contribute.
- For generative retrieval, the analyzed architecture is an encoder-decoder Transformer where the encoder processes query `q` and the decoder autoregressively predicts identifier tokens `d_1, ..., d_M`.
- At decoding step `i`, cross-attention is written as `α_i = softmax(Q^T W \hat d_{i-1})`, where `W = W_K^T W_Q`, and the cross-attention output is `h_i = W_V Q α_i`.
- The token-level training loss is standard autoregressive cross-entropy: `L_i(d, q) = -log(exp(e_{d_i}^T h_i) / Σ_{v∈V} exp(e_v^T h_i))`, with `e_v` taken from the decoder embedding table.
- Under teacher forcing, the paper treats `e_{d_i}^T h_i` as the relevance contribution at position `i` and derives `rel(d, q) = Σ_i e_{d_i}^T h_i = sum(\tilde E_d^T Q ⊙ A)`, where `\tilde e_{d_i}^T = e_{d_i}^T W_V` and `A = [α_1, ..., α_M]^T`.
- This derivation shows GR as a special case of [[multi-vector-retrieval]] with document-side vectors coming from token embeddings rather than fully contextualized document encoders.
- The comparison emphasizes two structural differences. First, GR uses simple embedding-based document encoding `\tilde E_d`, while MVDR uses contextualized token vectors `D`. Second, GR uses a dense learned [[document-to-query-alignment]], whereas MVDR typically uses sparse [[document-to-query-alignment]] such as ColBERT's max-over-document-token rule.
- The paper incorporates two GR extensions into the same framework. PAWA upgrades static embeddings by generating position-aware token projections with `e_{i,v} = E_{i,v} d'_i`, yielding `rel(d, q) = sum(\tilde D'^T \tilde E_d^T Q ⊙ A)`. NP-decoding replaces embedding-table document vectors with contextualized token vectors and recovers `rel(d, q) = sum(D^T Q ⊙ A)`.
- For alignment analysis, the paper contrasts MVDR's sparse column-wise top-1 alignment `A_ij = 1[d_i^T q_j = max_i d_i^T q_j]` against GR's row-wise softmax attention, and argues both contain low-rank structure. In GR, `A = softmax(D^T W Q)` is shown to admit a rank-one approximation bound `||A - R|| ≤ 4γ||W||`.
- Experimental settings are standardized around T5 variants. T5-ColBERT uses in-batch negatives with `batch_size = 256` and `epochs = 5`; T5-SEAL uses Hugging Face training with `learning_rate = 1e-3`, `batch_size = 256`, and query-to-span targets of length `10`. Reranking uses BM25 top-100 candidates plus the gold document.

## Key Results

- On reranking (Table 5), MVDR with query-to-document alignment outperforms GR: on NQ320K it reaches `R@1 = 61.3`, `R@10 = 91.9`, `MRR@10 = 72.0`, versus GR's `47.4`, `87.0`, `60.5`; on MS MARCO it reaches `46.5`, `84.5`, `58.9`, versus GR's `35.3`, `77.1`, `48.3`.
- The hybrid MVDR variant with document-to-query alignment lands between the two paradigms, with NQ320K `R@1 = 53.2`, `R@10 = 90.1`, `MRR@10 = 65.7`, supporting the claim that alignment direction materially affects performance.
- In end-to-end retrieval on NQ320K (Table 6), stronger GR document encoding helps substantially: DSI improves from `R@1/R@10 = 55.2/67.4` to DSI-PAWA `60.2/80.2`, and T5-GENRE improves from `53.7/64.7` to T5-GENRE-NP `62.2/78.8`.
- Despite those gains, T5-ColBERT remains stronger on NQ320K end-to-end retrieval with `R@1 = 61.1`, `R@10 = 88.4`, compared with T5-SEAL's `44.7` and `75.5`; on MS MARCO the same models reach `44.2/77.8/MRR@10 55.3` versus `24.1/57.1/34.0`.
- The alignment analysis finds measurable lexical behavior in both paradigms: MVDR's hard exact-match rate averages `11.4%`, and both MVDR and GR show higher soft exact-match rates for more important tokens.
- The low-rank experiment reports that the observed approximation error `||A - R||` for GR's alignment matrix is much smaller than `||W||`, empirically supporting the proposed low-rank characterization.

## Limitations

- The theoretical connection is derived for the training objective and does not fully explain how relevance computation transfers to generative inference with beam or greedy decoding.
- The derivation simplifies the decoder by abstracting away multi-layer nonlinear interactions in cross-attention, so some behavior of practical GR systems is intentionally collapsed into a cleaner analytical form.
- The paper studies representative T5-based GR and MVDR models, but not the full space of identifier designs, architectural variants, or corpus-update regimes.
- Stronger document encoding narrows the gap for GR, yet Recall@10 remains substantially below T5-ColBERT in the reported end-to-end results, indicating that the proposed common framework does not erase practical inference disadvantages.
- The reported ACM DOI is a placeholder rather than a finalized identifier, which slightly limits bibliographic completeness.
