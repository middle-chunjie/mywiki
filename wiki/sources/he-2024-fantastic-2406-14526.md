## Summary

The paper studies how modern text-to-image and text-to-video systems reproduce copyrighted characters, even when users do not explicitly name them. It introduces a two-part evaluation framework over `|D| = 50` characters: `DETECT = sum_{C in D} d(C, f_{p,m}(C))` measures whether a generated image is recognized as a target character, while `CONS = (1 / |D|) sum_{C in D} c(s(C), f_{p,m}(C))` measures preservation of user intent via character-level attributes. The authors then build indirect anchors from GPT-4-generated descriptions and keywords, rank them by embedding similarity or corpus co-occurrence, and test mitigation via prompt rewriting and negative prompts across open and closed models. The central result is that indirect anchoring remains surprisingly effective, while combined rewriting plus negative prompting materially reduces risk without collapsing intent consistency.

## Problem & Motivation

Copyrighted characters create both a legal and technical failure mode for generative models. Legally, infringement decisions often depend on substantial similarity rather than verbatim copying, so a model can create liability even without reproducing exact training images. Technically, characters are abstract recurring motifs rather than single datapoints, which makes them harder to block with conventional deduplication or near-memorization defenses. The paper asks two concrete questions: whether users can easily trigger copyrighted-character generation unintentionally through generic descriptions, and whether currently deployed mitigations such as prompt rewriting actually work once indirect prompts are considered.

## Method

- **Evaluation target**: study `|D| = 50` popular characters from 18 studios and franchises spanning U.S. and international properties, covering animation, superhero films, and video games.
- **Copyright metric**: define `d(C, I) in {0, 1}` for whether image `I = f_{p,m}(C)` is recognized as character `C`, then aggregate `DETECT(f, p, m) = sum_{C in D} d(C, f_{p,m}(C))`; lower is better.
- **Intent metric**: extract attribute description `s(C)` for each character and compute `CONS(f, p, m) = (1 / |D|) sum_{C in D} c(s(C), f_{p,m}(C))`, where `c` is the VQAScore probability of answering "Yes" to whether the image shows the requested generic traits.
- **Automatic evaluators**: use GPT-4V as the copyrighted-character detector after ablations against Claude Sonnet and LLaVA-34B; on 200 human-labeled images it reaches `82.5%` accuracy and Cohen's kappa `0.648`. The `CONS` evaluator achieves AUROC `0.82` against human binary judgments.
- **Indirect-anchor generation**: for each character, use GPT-4 to synthesize candidate prompts, including `n = 50` keywords and descriptions of roughly `60` words, chosen to stay under the common `77`-token Stable Diffusion text limit while remaining visually descriptive.
- **EmbeddingSim ranking**: encode the character name `C` and each candidate `w_i` with the model text encoder `g`, score by cosine similarity `s_{w_i} = g(C) dot g(w_i) / (||g(C)|| ||g(w_i)||)`, and sort descending.
- **Co-Occurrence ranking**: rank keywords by corpus co-occurrence counts between the character name and candidate word in LAION-2B, C4, OpenWebText, and The Pile; this estimates whether a nominally generic word is strongly associated with a particular character in likely training data.
- **LM-ranked baseline**: compare the rerankers against greedy LM-produced top-`k` keywords, keeping `k` fixed so retrieval methods are compared at equal budget.
- **Mitigations**: simulate DALL-E-style `prompt-rewriting` with a GPT-4-based rewriting pipeline, then test `negative-prompt` variants containing "Copyrighted character", the target name, and top-`k` keywords from LM, EmbeddingSim, Co-Occurrence, or a combined set. Negative prompts are injected through `classifier-free-guidance`.
- **Generation setup**: use `50` denoising steps and `guidance_scale = 3` for Playground v2.5, Stable Diffusion XL, and PixArt-alpha; DeepFloyd IF uses its standard 3-stage stack; VideoFusion uses default settings to generate `16` frames, with first/middle/last frames evaluated.
- **Evaluation protocol**: run each intervention `3` times per setting and report mean plus standard deviation for both `DETECT` and `CONS`; the main analysis centers on Playground v2.5, with transfer checks to DALL-E 3 and VideoFusion.

## Key Results

- Character-name prompts alone generate about `~60%` of the 50 copyrighted characters on tested systems; on Playground v2.5, the no-mitigation baseline is `DETECT = 30.33 +- 1.89` with `CONS = 0.75 +- 0.01`.
- Indirect anchoring is strong: `60`-word descriptions generate `~48%` of characters, and the top-ranked description by embedding similarity succeeds on `24` characters versus `16` for the bottom-ranked description.
- Co-occurrence ranking works best for keywords: `5` LAION keywords nearly match the performance of `60`-word descriptions, and top-`20` LAION or embedding-ranked keywords can exceed description-based prompting.
- Prompt rewriting alone is insufficient on Playground v2.5, reducing `DETECT` only from `30.33` to `14.33` while slightly increasing `CONS` from `0.75` to `0.80`.
- Adding negative prompts is materially helpful when the target name is included: on Playground v2.5, rewritten prompt plus target name plus `5` EmbeddingSim and `5` Co-Occurrence keywords lowers `DETECT` to `4.33 +- 0.47` while preserving `CONS = 0.81 +- 0.00`.
- The combined mitigation transfers across models: `DETECT` drops from `33.00` to `1.67` on Stable Diffusion XL, `33.67` to `2.00` on DeepFloyd IF, and `28.33` to `11.33` on VideoFusion.
- The proposed GPT-4V detector outperforms tested alternatives in evaluator ablations: `82.5%` accuracy / `0.65` kappa without naming the character in the prompt, versus `71%` / `0.43` for Claude Sonnet and `78%` / `0.56` for LLaVA-34B.
- Their intent-detection oracle appears feasible: an LLM-based detector reaches `95.14%` accuracy with `93.68%` TPR and `3.32%` FPR, while a retriever-based detector reaches `93.28%` accuracy.

## Limitations

- The evaluation focuses on `50` popular characters, so coverage is still narrow relative to the long tail of copyrighted entities and jurisdictions.
- `DETECT` is only a proxy for legal infringement; the paper explicitly notes that copyright sufficiency remains jurisdiction-dependent and cannot be reduced to a single metric.
- GPT-4V judging is imperfect despite reasonable agreement with humans, so absolute counts may contain evaluator noise.
- The mitigation pipeline assumes an oracle that identifies the intended copyrighted character from user input; this is partially validated but not deployed end-to-end in the paper's main experiments.
- The work studies inference-time defenses only and does not test training-time unlearning, dataset filtering, or stronger optimization-based attacks for finding indirect anchors.
- DALL-E 3 does not expose custom negative prompts, so the strongest mitigation is demonstrated mainly on open models rather than on the most policy-relevant proprietary deployment.
