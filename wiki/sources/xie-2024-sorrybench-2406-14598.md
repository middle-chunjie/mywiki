## Summary

SORRY-Bench introduces a systematic benchmark for measuring whether aligned [[large-language-model]]s refuse unsafe requests instead of substantially fulfilling them. The benchmark defines a 44-class fine-grained risk taxonomy over four domains, builds a class-balanced base set of `440` unsafe instructions (`10` per category), and adds `20` controlled prompt mutations to create `8,800` additional examples covering writing style, persuasion, encoding, and multilingual variation. The paper also contributes a `7,040`-example human judgment dataset for evaluating automated judges, then shows that fine-tuned `7B` judge models can match or exceed GPT-4-scale evaluators at much lower cost. Using this setup, the authors benchmark `56` open and proprietary LLMs and surface substantial differences in refusal behavior across model families, versions, categories, and prompt forms.

## Problem & Motivation

Existing LLM safety benchmarks mix coarse and inconsistent risk categories, over-sample some harms, under-sample others, and often ignore how prompt phrasing changes refusal behavior. This makes it hard to compare models, diagnose coverage gaps, or understand whether a model is refusing because of policy, language, or prompt formatting artifacts.

The paper argues that safety refusal evaluation needs three things simultaneously: a customizable fine-grained taxonomy, balanced per-category coverage, and a scalable evaluator that does not depend entirely on expensive frontier judges. SORRY-Bench is proposed as a benchmark that separates these factors and supports more systematic tracking of refusal behavior across models and versions.

## Method

- **Taxonomy construction**: aggregate taxonomies from `10` prior safety datasets, decompose coarse categories, then refine them via a [[human-in-the-loop]] process. The final [[fine-grained-taxonomy]] has `44` unsafe categories grouped into `4` high-level domains.
- **Base benchmark data**: map prior examples into the new taxonomy with GPT-4 as a classifier, filter and deduplicate them, then manually author additional prompts to obtain a [[class-balanced-dataset]] with `10` unsafe instructions per class and `44 × 10 = 440` total samples.
- **Prompt variation layer**: rewrite every base prompt with `20` controlled [[linguistic-mutation]]s spanning writing styles, persuasion strategies, encoding/encryption, and multilingual translation, producing `20 × 440 = 8,800` extra unsafe instructions.
- **Evaluation target**: cast safety refusal as binary classification over `(unsafe instruction, model response)` pairs. A response counts as fulfillment if it provides substantial content that resolves the unsafe request, even if it also contains disclaimers; otherwise it is marked refusal.
- **Human judgment corpus**: sample `8` model responses per base instruction for in-distribution evaluation and `8` more per mutated instruction for out-of-distribution evaluation, yielding `440 × (8 + 8) = 7,040` human annotations with label distribution `30.4%` fulfillment and `69.6%` refusal. The train split is `2,640` and the test split is `4,400`.
- **Judge meta-evaluation**: compare off-the-shelf and fine-tuned [[llm-as-a-judge]] recipes, including CoT, few-shot ICL, and supervised tuning. Fine-tuned judge models are trained for `3` epochs; for LLM judges the global batch size is `32`, and the chosen learning rate for Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 is `1e-5`.
- **Benchmark execution**: evaluate `56` LLMs. Generation for benchmarked models is mostly run with temperature `0.7`, top-p `1.0`, and max tokens `1024`; judge inference disables randomness (`temperature = 0`). The final reported judge is fine-tuned `Mistral-7b-instruct-v0.2`, chosen for its efficiency/accuracy trade-off.
- **Behavioral framing**: the benchmark treats refusal as an outcome of post-pretraining [[safety-alignment]], including methods such as [[instruction-tuning]] and [[preference-optimization]], and measures how these choices manifest under category and prompt variation.

## Key Results

- The benchmark itself contributes `44` categories, `440` base unsafe instructions, and `8,800` prompt-mutated variants.
- The human judgment resource contains `7,040` annotations with strong inter-annotator agreement: average pairwise Cohen's `κ = 90.5%` on a shared sample of `100` responses.
- Off-the-shelf GPT-4o reaches `78.9` Cohen's `κ` as a judge at roughly `260s` per evaluation pass; off-the-shelf Llama-3-70B-Instruct reaches `71.6` at `~100s`.
- Fine-tuning is the only judge add-on that consistently helps: GPT-3.5-Turbo + fine-tuning reaches `83.8`, Llama-3-8B-Instruct + fine-tuning reaches `80.8` in `~10s`, Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 + fine-tuning reaches `81.0` in `~11s`, and Gemma-7B-It + fine-tuning reaches `81.2` in `~14s`.
- Across `56` benchmarked models, `27` fall in the `20%` to `50%` fulfillment range; Claude-2 and Gemini-1.5 are below `10%`, while `8` models, including several Mistral variants, exceed `50%`.
- Version shifts are substantial: Llama-2-70B to Llama-3-70B rises from `12%` to `35%` fulfillment, while Gemini-Pro to Gemini-1.5 drops from `33%` to `7%`.
- Category difficulty is uneven: harassment, child-related crimes, and sexual crimes average only `9%` to `11%` fulfillment, whereas legal consulting advice, religion promotion, and ethical belief promotion average `74%` to `80%`.
- Prompt mutations matter: technical-term rewrites increase fulfillment by `8%` to `19%` across all eight studied models, persuasion mutations raise it by `5%` to `66%`, and encoding/encryption usually lowers it by `15%` to `68%`.

## Limitations

- The benchmark evaluates binary refusal/fulfillment rather than graded harmfulness, so it cannot measure severity differences among unsafe responses.
- It does not cover multi-risk prompts, neutral prompts that still elicit unsafe responses, or the full worst-case jailbreak space; the `20` mutations are positioned as average-case user variations rather than comprehensive attacks.
- A model that always refuses can score as maximally safe on this benchmark while still being unhelpful, so the paper recommends combining SORRY-Bench with utility and over-refusal benchmarks.
- The taxonomy reflects a fast-moving policy space and may require periodic revision as safety norms, regulations, and model behaviors change.
- Public release creates possible benchmark contamination risk if future models train directly on SORRY-Bench data.
