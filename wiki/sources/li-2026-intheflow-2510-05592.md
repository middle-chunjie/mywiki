## Summary

This paper proposes AgentFlow, a trainable multi-turn agentic system that separates planning, execution, verification, and answer generation instead of using a single monolithic tool-using policy. Its central technical claim is that the planner should be optimized on-policy inside the live interaction loop, where states evolve with tool outputs and verifier feedback. To make that feasible, the paper introduces Flow-GRPO, which broadcasts one verifiable trajectory-level reward to every turn and applies group-normalized advantages for stable planner updates under sparse rewards. With a Qwen2.5-7B backbone, the tuned system reports average gains of `14.9%` on search tasks, `14.0%` on agentic tasks, `14.5%` on math, and `4.1%` on science over strong specialized baselines, and it also beats GPT-4o on all four domains.

## Problem & Motivation

The paper targets a gap between two existing paradigms. Monolithic tool-integrated reasoning models can be trained with outcome-based reinforcement learning, but they interleave all reasoning and tool use inside a single full-context policy, which becomes brittle as horizons lengthen, toolsets diversify, and feedback changes the state distribution. Training-free agentic systems avoid this by decomposing work across specialized modules, but they usually rely on handcrafted orchestration or offline traces and therefore do not learn from live multi-turn successes and failures. AgentFlow is motivated by the need for a trainable agentic system that can coordinate tools over long horizons while still learning directly from trajectory-level outcomes.

## Method

- **Agentic decomposition**: AgentFlow contains four modules, Action Planner `\mathcal{P}`, Tool Executor `\mathcal{E}`, Execution Verifier `\mathcal{V}`, and Solution Generator `\mathcal{G}`, which interact through a shared memory `M` and toolset `K`.
- **Planner state and action**: at turn `t`, the planner observes `(q, K, M^t)` and emits an action `a^t ~ \pi_\theta(a^t | q, K, M^t)` that specifies a sub-goal, a tool choice, and the context to use.
- **Execution loop**: the executor returns `e^t ~ \mathcal{E}(e^t | a^t, K)` and the verifier emits a binary stop/continue signal `v^t ~ \mathcal{V}(v^t | q, e^t, M^t)`, with memory updated by `M^{t+1} = f_mem(M^t, a^t, e^t, v^t)`.
- **Termination and generation**: the loop stops when `v^t = 1` or the maximum turn budget is reached, after which the generator produces the final answer `o ~ \mathcal{G}(o | q, M^T)`.
- **Flow-GRPO reward design**: instead of assigning heuristic intermediate rewards, the method uses one final outcome reward for every turn, `r = R(a^t) = \bar{R}(o, q, y*)`, where `\bar{R}` is a binary correctness judgment.
- **Optimization objective**: training samples `G = 8` on-policy rollouts per example and optimizes a PPO-style clipped objective over token-level ratios `\rho_{i,j}^t = \pi_\theta / \pi_{\theta_old}`, plus a KL penalty to a fixed reference model.
- **Advantage normalization**: each rollout gets a group-normalized advantage `A_i^t = (\bar{R}(o_i, q, y*) - mean) / std`, which is constant across turns within that rollout and reduces variance under sparse rewards.
- **Backbone and tools**: in the main setup, planner, executor, verifier, and generator all use Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, together with five tools: Base Generator, Python Coder, Google Search, Wikipedia Search, and Web Search.
- **Tool retrieval details**: the Wikipedia Search and Web Search tools chunk retrieved text into roughly `200`-word segments with `20`-word overlap, rank chunks using `text-embedding-3-small`, and summarize the top `10` chunks from up to the first `1M` words.
- **Training hyperparameters**: planner learning rate `1e-6`, sampling temperature `0.5`, KL coefficient `\beta = 0.001`, planner max output length `2048`, batch size `32`, `8` rollouts per sample, training-time max turns `T_max = 3`, tool timeout `500` seconds, and tool-side LLM temperature `0.0`; training runs on `8` NVIDIA A100 GPUs.
- **Reward adjudication**: the final-outcome reward is provided by an LLM judge, instantiated as GPT-4o, using semantic, numeric, and option-level equivalence criteria.

## Key Results

- On search-intensive benchmarks, AgentFlow with Flow-GRPO reaches `57.3` average accuracy, beating the untuned AgentFlow system at `47.2` and GPT-4o at `49.1`.
- On the agentic benchmark GAIA, the tuned system reaches `33.1`, compared with `17.2` for the frozen AgentFlow setup and `17.3` for GPT-4o.
- On mathematical reasoning, it reaches `51.5` average across AIME24, AMC23, and GameOf24, versus `37.0` for ToRL and `35.1` for GPT-4o.
- On scientific reasoning, it reaches `63.5` average on GPQA and MedQA, outperforming TIR at `59.4` and GPT-4o at `45.5`.
- Relative to the strongest specialized baselines, the paper reports average gains of `14.9%` on search, `14.0%` on agentic tasks, `14.5%` on math, and `4.1%` on science.
- In the planner-training ablation, Flow-GRPO improves the frozen Qwen2.5-7B planner from `38.5` to `55.7` average across six representative tasks, a gain of `17.2`.
- Tool-calling errors fall during training, with the paper reporting reductions of up to `28.4%` on GAIA.
- Tool selection becomes more task-specific after tuning: on 2Wiki, Google Search usage rises by `42.0%`, while on MedQA Google Search drops from `66.2%` to `10.9%` as the planner shifts toward Web Search (`0` to `19.5%`) and Wikipedia Search (`0` to `59.8%`).
- Increasing the inference turn budget from `3` to `10` keeps improving reported outcomes across 2Wiki, GameOf24, AIME24, and GAIA, indicating that the trained planner benefits from longer test-time horizons.

## Limitations

- Only the planner is trainable; the executor, verifier, and generator remain fixed Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct modules, so the paper does not study end-to-end joint optimization of the full system.
- The training reward depends on a proprietary GPT-4o judge and synchronous external tools, which introduces infrastructure dependence and may make reproduction costly or brittle.
- Training rollouts are capped at `T_max = 3`, so the optimization objective is learned on relatively short trajectories even though the paper later studies larger inference-time turn budgets.
- The reward signal is binary and trajectory-level, which simplifies optimization but may still blur fine-grained distinctions between partially useful and clearly harmful intermediate actions.
- Evaluation covers ten text-centric benchmarks, but the paper does not test multimodal, embodied, or continuously changing real-world environments where module coordination may be harder.
