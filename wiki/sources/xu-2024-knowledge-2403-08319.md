## Summary

This survey systematizes how large language models behave when contextual evidence and parametric memory disagree. It defines three conflict families, [[context-memory-conflict]], [[inter-context-conflict]], and [[intra-memory-conflict]], then reviews their causes, behavioral signatures, and mitigation strategies. The paper argues that conflict handling cannot be reduced to "trust context" or "trust memory" because the right choice depends on whether the conflict is caused by updated evidence, misinformation, inconsistent retrieved documents, or instability already stored in model parameters. Its main contribution is a unified taxonomy over recent LLM-era work, a synthesis of available datasets and quantitative findings, and a research agenda for conflict resolution in real-world [[retrieval-augmented-generation]] and tool-using systems.

## Problem & Motivation

LLMs combine [[parametric-knowledge]] stored in model weights with [[contextual-knowledge]] supplied through prompts, dialogue history, retrieved documents, and tools. These sources do not always agree. The paper argues that such [[knowledge-conflict]] directly affects trustworthiness, robustness, factuality, and user-facing reliability. Existing literature had discussed fragments of the problem, but there was no dedicated survey organizing the field across conflict types, causal mechanisms, model behaviors, datasets, and mitigation strategies. The motivation is therefore to give researchers a common vocabulary and map for studying when LLMs should follow context, resist it, separate conflicting sources, or synthesize them into a more truthful answer.

## Method

- **Survey scope**: the authors explicitly frame the paper as a survey rather than a new model paper; it introduces no new architecture, loss, or training hyperparameters, and instead synthesizes literature mainly collected after `2021` from Google Scholar, ACL Anthology, and citation chasing.
- **Core taxonomy**: the review is organized around `3` conflict types: [[context-memory-conflict]] between external context and model memory, [[inter-context-conflict]] among multiple contextual sources, and [[intra-memory-conflict]] within the model's own stored knowledge.
- **Per-conflict template**: each conflict type is analyzed with the same structure, `causes -> model behaviors -> solutions`, which makes results across otherwise heterogeneous papers directly comparable.
- **Cause decomposition**: for context-memory conflicts, the survey separates `2` main causes, [[temporal-misalignment]] and [[misinformation-pollution]]; for intra-memory conflicts, it highlights `3` causes, training-corpus bias, stochastic decoding, and [[knowledge-editing]] side effects.
- **Solution taxonomy**: mitigation methods are split into `pre-hoc` versus `post-hoc`, and then grouped by objective such as faithfulness to context, discriminating misinformation, disentangling sources, and improving factuality.
- **Dataset synthesis**: Table 1 compiles `11` representative evaluation datasets spanning `5` context-memory, `5` inter-context, and `1` intra-memory settings, and records how conflicts are constructed via human annotation, entity substitution, or LLM generation.
- **Quantitative synthesis**: the appendix aggregates reported numbers from prior work rather than running new experiments, providing a cautious cross-paper comparison of conflict impact and mitigation effectiveness.

## Key Results

- The survey consolidates the field into `3` conflict categories and `11` representative datasets, giving a reusable map of evaluation settings across context-memory, inter-context, and intra-memory conflict.
- Appendix Table 2 reports that LLMs can generate contradictory content with probability between `15.7%` and `22.9%`, and that even GPT-4 can show an inconsistency rate of `32%` on FaVIQ in one cited study.
- For intra-memory conflict, surveyed work reports consistency of only about `50%` to `60%` for earlier masked or encoder-decoder LMs under paraphrase-style probes, showing that internal factual stability is far from solved.
- Appendix Table 3 reports strong but heterogeneous mitigation gains: CAD improves GPT-Neo `20B` by `54.4%` on MemoTrap and `128%` on NQ-SWAP, while one discriminator-based method reaches about `80%` F1 and improves downstream model performance by more than `5%`.
- Prompt-based misinformation defenses in the surveyed literature improve accuracy by more than `10%`, and one conflict-detection method achieves over `80%` F1 on contextual conflict detection.

## Limitations

- The paper is a survey, so its quantitative conclusions are secondary syntheses from heterogeneous papers, datasets, models, and objectives rather than apples-to-apples experiments.
- The authors explicitly note possible incompleteness because the topic is rapidly expanding and recent or peripheral work may have been missed.
- Most reviewed evidence is still centered on English text and QA-style evaluations, leaving downstream, multilingual, and multimodal conflict settings underexplored.
- Much of the surveyed literature studies artificially constructed conflicts, so transfer to real-world retrieved or interactive settings remains uncertain.
