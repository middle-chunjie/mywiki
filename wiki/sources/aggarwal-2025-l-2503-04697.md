## Summary

The paper introduces Length Controlled Policy Optimization (LCPO), a reinforcement-learning method for training reasoning language models that obey prompt-specified token budgets while preserving answer accuracy. Starting from DeepScaleR-1.5B-Preview, the authors train L1-Exact with reward `I(y = y_gold) - alpha * |n_gold - n_y|` and then train L1-Max with a clipped maximum-budget objective, so one model family can support either exact or ceiling-style compute control. Across math benchmarks and out-of-domain tasks, L1 achieves a smoother accuracy-versus-budget frontier than budget-forcing baselines such as S1, especially at short budgets. A notable secondary finding is that LCPO-trained long-CoT models also become strong short-reasoning models, with the 1.5B L1 variants reported to match or exceed GPT-4o under comparable reasoning lengths on the paper's short-CoT evaluation.

## Problem & Motivation

Reasoning LMs often improve with longer chain-of-thought traces, but their output length is usually uncontrolled, so users cannot reliably trade off accuracy against test-time compute. Existing prompt-only or budget-forcing approaches either ignore the requested budget or truncate generation in a brittle way that damages reasoning quality. The paper targets a stricter setting: given a user-requested token budget, train a reasoning model to stay near that budget while still optimizing for correct final answers. This makes token length an explicit control knob for deployment cost and latency.

## Method

- **Prompt conditioning**: each training prompt is augmented as `x_i_new = Concat(x_i, "Think for n_gold tokens.")`, where `n_gold ~ U(100, 4000)`.
- **Training data**: LCPO uses the DeepScaleR-Preview-Dataset of `40K` math question-answer pairs and does not require gold chain-of-thought traces.
- **L1-Exact objective**: the exact-length model optimizes `r(y, y_gold, n_gold) = I(y = y_gold) - alpha * |n_gold - n_y|`, coupling answer correctness with adherence to a requested token count.
- **L1-Max objective**: the maximum-budget model uses `r(y, y_gold, n_gold) = I(y = y_gold) * clip(alpha * (n_gold - n_y) + delta, 0, 1)` with `delta = 0.5`, softly penalizing budget overruns while still preferring correct answers.
- **RL algorithm**: experiments use [[group-relative-policy-optimization]] with `alpha = 0.0003`, `lr = 1e-6`, batch size `128`, `700` RL steps for L1-Exact, then `120` more steps for L1-Max.
- **Base model**: L1 starts from [[deepscaler-1.5b-preview]], a `1.5B` reasoning model derived from [[deepseek-r1-distill-qwen-1.5b]]; training uses `4K` context length and evaluation uses `8K`, down from the original `24K`.
- **Inference control modes**: L1-Exact targets exact budgets, while L1-Max handles both exact requests and "at most N tokens" style prompts under a shared dual-objective recipe.
- **Evaluation**: results are reported at budgets `{512, 1024, 2048, 3600}` with `16` random seeds and temperature `0.6`.

## Key Results

- On math reasoning tasks, L1 improves over [[s1]] by roughly `100-150%` relative and `20-25` absolute points at the `512` and `1024` token budgets.
- L1-Exact stays about `1%` below the unconstrained [[deepscaler-1.5b-preview]] `4K` baseline, while L1-Max matches that baseline and can use up to `2x` fewer tokens by adapting to problem difficulty.
- Length adherence is strong in-domain: mean error is about `3%` on math datasets; on OOD datasets the error rises to roughly `20-40%`, which is still substantially better than uncontrolled prompting.
- L1-Max keeps soft budget violation rates between `0.3%` and `2.3%`, averaging about `1.3%` across the tested budgets.
- In the short-CoT evaluation, L1-Max reaches `47.8` average accuracy versus `45.6` for [[gpt-4o]] and `41.0` for Qwen-2.5-1.5B-Instruct at comparable generation lengths.
- Additional training improves adherence: extending L1-Exact yields RMSE reductions such as `18.44 -> 10.04` on math tasks, though high-budget performance drops slightly.
- Generalization checks on AIME2025 show a smaller drop for L1-Exact (`-2.3`) than for several baselines, including DeepScaleR-24K (`-12.9`), supporting the paper's anti-memorization claim.

## Limitations

- The model is only trained with requested lengths up to `4000` tokens, and the authors explicitly note that it may not generalize to much longer requested budgets.
- Training uses `4K` context and evaluation `8K` because of compute limits, so claims about longer-context scaling are extrapolations rather than direct evidence.
- Length precision degrades notably on OOD knowledge-heavy tasks such as MMLU, where long reasoning is often less useful.
- The paper fixes `alpha = 0.0003` and reports limited hyperparameter search, so the current trade-off between accuracy and adherence is probably not fully optimized.
- Comparisons against supervised fine-tuning use a particular relabel-and-train setup; stronger SFT baselines or hybrid objectives are not explored deeply.
