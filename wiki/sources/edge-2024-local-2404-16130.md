## Summary

The paper introduces GraphRAG, a graph-indexed retrieval-augmented generation pipeline for answering global, corpus-level questions that conventional vector RAG handles poorly. Instead of retrieving a few locally relevant passages, GraphRAG uses an LLM to extract entities, relations, and claims from document chunks, aggregates them into a knowledge graph, partitions that graph with hierarchical community detection, and precomputes community summaries. Query answering then becomes a map-reduce summarization problem over those summaries. On two corpora in the 1M-token range, GraphRAG consistently beats vector RAG on comprehensiveness and diversity for global sensemaking questions, while also reducing query-time context requirements relative to source-text summarization. The paper also proposes an adaptive, persona-driven benchmarking setup plus claim-based validation for this evaluation regime.

## Problem & Motivation

Conventional vector RAG is effective when answers are localized in a small number of passages, but it breaks down on global questions such as dataset-wide themes, trends, or cross-document patterns. The paper argues that these are better viewed as [[query-focused-summarization]] problems over an entire corpus rather than retrieval problems over a few relevant chunks. Prior QFS methods, however, do not scale well to the corpus sizes commonly indexed by RAG systems. GraphRAG is motivated by the need for a corpus-scale indexing and summarization strategy that preserves global structure, supports sensemaking, and remains efficient enough for interactive question answering.

## Method

- **Chunking and extraction**: split source documents into chunks of roughly `600` tokens with `100`-token overlap, then prompt an LLM to extract entities, relationships, and optional claims from each chunk.
- **Entity/relationship prompts**: each entity record includes name, type, and description; each relationship record includes source, target, description, and a strength score. Prompt tailoring is done with domain-specific few-shot exemplars.
- **Self-reflection for recall**: after an initial extraction pass, the model can perform iterative [[self-reflection]] to "glean" missed entities; the appendix reports higher extracted-entity counts at larger chunk sizes when reflection is used.
- **Graph construction**: aggregate extracted instances into a [[knowledge-graph]] where duplicate relationships increase edge weights; the paper uses exact string matching for entity matching, then summarizes aggregated node/edge descriptions.
- **Hierarchical communities**: apply hierarchical Leiden [[community-detection]] recursively until leaf communities are reached; each level yields a mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive partition used for summary generation.
- **Community summary generation**: within an `8k`-token context window, prioritize leaf-community elements by descending combined node degree; for higher levels, replace oversized element sets with lower-level summaries to fit the context budget.
- **Global answering**: shuffle and chunk community summaries, generate map-stage partial answers in parallel, assign each a helpfulness score in `[0, 100]`, filter answers with `score = 0`, then reduce the highest-scoring partial answers into a final answer.
- **Adaptive benchmarking**: generate corpus-specific global questions by prompting for `K = 5` personas, `N = 5` tasks per persona, and `M = 5` questions per persona-task pair, yielding `K * N * M = 125` questions per dataset.
- **Claim-based validation**: for Experiment 2, decompose answers into factual claims and cluster them with agglomerative clustering using `distance = 1 - ROUGE-L` to quantify comprehensiveness and diversity.

## Key Results

- On global sensemaking questions, GraphRAG-style global methods beat vector RAG (`SS`) on comprehensiveness with win rates of `72-83%` for Podcast transcripts and `72-80%` for News articles (`p < .001`).
- On diversity, global methods beat vector RAG with win rates of `75-82%` on Podcast transcripts (`p < .001`) and `62-71%` on News articles (`p < .01`).
- The indexed graphs contain `8,564` nodes and `20,691` edges for the Podcast dataset, and `15,754` nodes and `19,520` edges for the News dataset.
- Compared with source-text map-reduce summarization (`TS`), low-level GraphRAG summaries (`C3`) require `26-33%` fewer context tokens per query; root-level summaries (`C0`) require more than `97%` fewer tokens.
- In claim-based evaluation, all global conditions (`C0-C3`) and `TS` exceed vector RAG on average claims. For example, News claims rise from `25.23` (`SS`) to `34.18` (`C0`), and Podcast claims rise from `26.50` (`SS`) to `32.46` (`C2`).
- Agreement between majority-vote LLM judgments and claim-based labels reaches `78%` for comprehensiveness and `69-70%` for diversity on non-tied comparisons.

## Limitations

- The evaluation uses only two corpora, each around the `1M`-token scale, so generalization to other domains and corpus structures remains unclear.
- There is no gold-standard answer set for the global questions; the paper relies on [[llm-as-a-judge]] comparisons plus indirect claim-based validation.
- Entity reconciliation uses exact string matching, which can leave duplicate entities unresolved in the graph.
- GraphRAG does not consistently beat vector RAG on empowerment or directness, and the authors note that missing quotes, citations, or specific examples may hurt those criteria.
- The paper does not directly benchmark fabrication or hallucination rates, leaving factuality analysis for future work.
