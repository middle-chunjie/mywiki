## Summary

The paper studies text embedding inversion as a privacy attack and replaces autoregressive reconstruction with a conditional masked diffusion language model. Instead of repeatedly querying the target encoder to score correction candidates, the method starts from a fully masked 32-token sequence and denoises all positions in parallel, conditioning on the target embedding through adaptive layer normalization. This makes inference encoder-agnostic and reduces the attack to a small fixed number of decoder passes. On three embedding models with different architectures and dimensions, the approach reaches up to `81.3%` token accuracy using a `78M`-parameter trainable decoder and only `8` denoising passes, showing that production embeddings should be treated as sensitive representations rather than anonymized text surrogates.

## Problem & Motivation

Embedding-based retrieval systems often assume dense vectors are safer to store, cache, or transmit than the original text. Prior inversion methods such as [[vec2text]] already challenged that assumption, but they depend on autoregressive decoding plus iterative correction, which makes attack cost grow with the number of correction rounds and can accumulate left-to-right generation errors. This paper targets a more practical black-box setting: recover text from a target embedding without re-querying the encoder during inference and without requiring encoder-specific alignment. The motivation is both algorithmic and security-oriented: make inversion more parallel, less architecture-dependent, and more realistic as a threat model for deployed embedding APIs.

## Method

- Formulates inversion as conditional generation: given embedding `e = f(x)`, recover `x_hat = argmax_x' p_theta(x' | e)` with a masked diffusion decoder rather than an autoregressive seq2seq model.
- Uses an absorbing-state forward process where each token survives with probability `alpha_t = exp(-lambda t)` and is otherwise replaced by `[MASK]`; the paper sets `lambda = 5.0`.
- Trains the reverse model to predict original masked tokens with categorical logits `p_theta(x_0,i | x_t, t, e) = softmax(z_i)` and a Rao-Blackwellized ELBO weighted by `1 / t` to emphasize low-noise reconstruction.
- Projects the target embedding to hidden size `D_h = 768` through a two-layer MLP and injects it into every transformer block via adaptive layer normalization.
- Uses an `8`-layer Transformer backbone with `12` attention heads, FFN size `4D_h = 3072`, and tied input/output embeddings over a vocabulary of `|V| = 50257`.
- Computes per-layer conditioning terms from both timestep and embedding, combining them as `gamma^(l) = gamma_t^(l) + gamma_c^(l)` and `beta^(l) = beta_t^(l) + beta_c^(l)` before applying AdaLN.
- Evaluates four decoding strategies: sequential greedy unmasking, Euler sampling, Euler sampling with confidence-based remasking, and two-stage decoding that refines a greedy hypothesis.
- Training uses `2M` multilingual `[[mc4]]` samples truncated to `32` tokens, GPT-2 tokenization, batch size `400`, `200K` steps, AdamW with learning rate `1e-4`, and EMA decay `0.9999`.
- The full model has about `270M` parameters because of embeddings, but only `78M` are trainable; inference requires no access to the target encoder once the conditioning embedding is given.

## Key Results

- On `32`-token reconstruction, sequential greedy decoding reaches `81.3%` token accuracy for `[[qwen3-embedding]]`, `78.8%` for [[embeddinggemma-300m]], and `76.0%` for [[jina-embeddings-v3]].
- The best Qwen3-Embedding checkpoint appears at `72.5K` steps with validation loss `1.317`; EmbeddingGemma peaks at `49.5K` steps with loss `1.55`, and jina-embeddings-v3 at `62.5K` with loss `1.60`.
- Across ten languages, sequential greedy decoding yields the strongest average cosine similarity among tested decoders: `0.715` for jina-embeddings-v3, `0.585` for Qwen3-Embedding, and `0.621` for EmbeddingGemma.
- Confidence-based remasking improves Euler decoding from `65.2%` to `67.8%` token accuracy when the remask probability is `0.05`; larger values such as `0.20` drop performance to `63.7%`.
- Two-stage decoding attains the best exact-match score reported in the paper at `13.1%`, while unconditional or random baselines collapse to `2.1%` and `0.02%` token accuracy respectively.
- Compared with prior inversion pipelines discussed in the paper, the proposed method needs only `8` decoder forward passes and no iterative re-embedding of the current hypothesis.

## Limitations

The paper only studies fixed-length `32`-token sequences, so its security conclusions may not transfer directly to paragraph- or document-level inversion. Results are reported against three embedding encoders, but there is no head-to-head evaluation against strong contemporary baselines such as Zero2Text under identical settings, so relative competitiveness is partly inferential. The method still requires a model trained separately for each target embedding space, which weakens the practical claim of universal encoder agnosticism. The paper also emphasizes token accuracy and cosine similarity more than semantic fidelity or human evaluation, leaving reconstruction quality for paraphrastic or multilingual outputs underexplored.
