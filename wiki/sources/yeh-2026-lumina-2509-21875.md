## Summary

Lumina is a training-free hallucination detector for retrieval-augmented generation that scores each response by contrasting two signals: how strongly the model uses retrieved evidence and how strongly it relies on internal parametric knowledge. External-context utilization is measured by the distributional shift between next-token distributions under relevant versus random documents, implemented with MMD over token embeddings; internal-knowledge utilization is measured by an information-processing-rate statistic derived from layerwise logit-lens trajectories. The paper also adds a statistical validation framework for both measurements rather than only correlating them with hallucination labels. Across RAGTruth and HalluRAG, and across Llama2, Llama3, and Mistral backbones, Lumina usually beats prior utilization-based detectors and remains reasonably robust when retrieval quality or detector-generator model matching is relaxed.

## Problem & Motivation

RAG reduces hallucination by supplying retrieved evidence, but it does not guarantee grounded generation: models may still ignore relevant documents and answer from stale or conflicting parametric knowledge. Prior RAG hallucination detectors such as ReDeEP and related semantic-entropy variants try to quantify external-context use and internal-knowledge use, but they depend on model-specific choices of attention heads and layers, so they require substantial tuning and often fail to transfer across datasets or architectures. Lumina targets a more model-agnostic alternative: define utilization signals at the distribution level, avoid hand-picking internal components, and validate that the signals really behave like external-context utilization and internal-knowledge utilization instead of only serving as ad hoc correlates of hallucination.

## Method

- **Hallucination score**: for token `a_t`, Lumina defines `H_t = lambda * I(a_t | q, d, a_<t) - (1 - lambda) * E(a_t | q, d, a_<t)` and response score `H_r = (1 / T) * sum_t H_t`; the default is `lambda = 0.5`.
- **External-context utilization**: compare the next-token distribution under retrieved documents `d` versus random documents `d'`, with `P(E_v) = p_theta(v | q, d, a_<t)` and `Q(E_v) = p_theta(v | q, d', a_<t)`, then set `E = Delta(P, Q)`.
- **Distribution distance**: instantiate `Delta` with squared maximum mean discrepancy, `MMD_k^2(P, Q) = E[k(A, A')] + E[k(B, B')] - 2E[k(A, B)]`, where `A, A' ~ P` and `B, B' ~ Q`; over vocabulary embeddings this becomes the double-sum form in Eq. 6.
- **Kernel choice**: the default kernel is cosine, `k_cos(E_u, E_v) = 0.5 * (1 + <E_u, E_v> / (||E_u||_2 ||E_v||_2))`; the appendix also tests `RBF_sigma` for `sigma in {0.5, 0.7, 1, 2, 3}`.
- **Practical approximation**: exact MMD over the full vocabulary is approximated with the top `100` tokens to keep computation cheap; random documents `d'` are taken from other data points or from irrelevant datastore documents.
- **Internal-knowledge utilization**: define an information-processing rate over `L` transformer layers using logit-lens projections `f(h) = Softmax(LayerNorm(h) W)`, where `x_t,1 = argmax_v p_theta(v | x_<t)`.
- **Information-processing-rate formula**: `R(x_<t) = [sum_{l=1}^{L-1} (1 - min(f(h_{t,l})[x_t,1] / p_theta(x_t,1 | x_<t), 1)) * l] / [sum_{l'=1}^{L-1} l' / H(f(h_{t,l'}))]`; larger values mean later layers contribute more unresolved processing before the final prediction stabilizes.
- **Calibration for sampled tokens**: because the generated token is not always the top-probability token, the calibrated score is `I(a_t | q, d, a_<t) = [p_theta(a_t | q, d, a_<t) / p_theta(a_t,1 | q, d, a_<t)] * R^1(q, d, a_<t)`.
- **Validation framework**: the paper tests four implications `H1-H4`, e.g. retrieved-document generations should have higher external-context utilization than no-document generations, and no-document generations should have higher internal-knowledge utilization than retrieved generations.
- **Evaluation setup**: experiments use `RAGTruth` and `HalluRAG`, four open-source detector backbones (`Llama2-7B`, `Llama2-13B`, `Llama3-8B`, `Mistral-7B`), and threshold-free metrics `AUROC`, `AUPRC`, and `PCC`; the method needs only two forward passes and no detector training.

## Key Results

- **Validation succeeds statistically**: all four hypotheses `H1-H4` pass on all four tested LLMs; for example, Llama2-7B shows `79.85` on `H1`, `27.67` on `H2`, `101.20` on `H3`, and `15.36` on `H4`, all reported as statistically significant.
- **RAGTruth gains over ReDeEP**: Lumina reaches `0.7646/0.7491` `AUROC/AUPRC` on Llama2-7B vs. ReDeEP's `0.7273/0.6971`, and `0.8569/0.8436` on Llama2-13B vs. `0.8055/0.7792`.
- **Large HalluRAG gains**: on HalluRAG, Lumina reports `0.9153` AUROC for Llama2-7B, `0.9166` for Llama2-13B, and `0.9899` for Mistral-7B; the Mistral-7B AUPRC is `0.9431`, far above ReDeEP's `0.3516`.
- **Competitive with supervised detection**: against SAPLMA, Lumina is better on several settings, e.g. Llama2-7B on RAGTruth `0.7646` vs. `0.6508` AUROC and on HalluRAG `0.9153` vs. `0.8813`.
- **Robustness to assumption relaxation**: adding up to `30%` context noise only modestly degrades performance for most models, and proxy-detector settings remain viable; the paper explicitly notes that the same detector/generator model is not always required.
- **Low inference cost**: on responses around `150` tokens, computing both scores takes under `1` second on a single `NVIDIA H100`, while avoiding multi-sample generation or supervised training.

## Limitations

- The theoretical setup assumes retrieved documents are relevant, correct, and sufficient; the empirical relaxation study helps, but poor retrieval quality still produces false positives and unstable score interpretation.
- The internal-knowledge score requires access to layerwise hidden states and unembedding/logit-lens projections, so the method is harder to apply to closed APIs than black-box detectors.
- The MMD computation is approximated with only the top `100` tokens, which may miss long-tail distribution changes and makes the practical metric only an approximation to the formal definition.
- The best `lambda` is not universal: the appendix finds `lambda = 0.2` is best for some backbones even though `0.5` is used as the default.
- HalluRAG does not include Llama3-8B generations, and most reported failures come from summarization or low-quality retrieval cases, so coverage across tasks and data regimes remains incomplete.
