## Summary

The paper proposes OPRO, a framework that uses a large language model itself as an optimizer by describing an optimization problem in natural language and iteratively appending prior solution-score pairs to a meta-prompt. The method is first tested on synthetic linear regression and traveling salesman problems, then applied to prompt optimization for reasoning benchmarks. OPRO generates multiple candidates per step, evaluates them externally, and feeds the resulting trajectory back into the next round to balance exploration and exploitation without gradients. On prompt optimization, the best discovered instructions outperform strong human-written baselines, including gains of up to 8 percentage points on GSM8K and over 5 points on most BBH tasks, while also transferring to related math benchmarks.

## Problem & Motivation

Derivative-based optimization is powerful but inapplicable when gradients are unavailable, objectives are exposed only through black-box evaluation, or the search space is discrete. The paper asks whether an LLM can serve as a generic optimizer if the task is specified in natural language instead of through a handcrafted solver. This is especially relevant for prompt optimization, where the search space is large, discrete, and highly sensitive to wording, and where the best instruction is often model-specific and task-specific. The authors therefore frame optimization as iterative prompting over a trajectory of previous solutions and scores, aiming to test whether LLMs can infer useful search directions directly from context.

## Method

- **Core loop (OPRO)**: at optimization step `t`, the optimizer LLM reads a meta-prompt containing the task description plus prior solution-score pairs, proposes new candidates, and the evaluated candidates are appended to the optimization trajectory for the next step.
- **Meta-prompt design**: the prompt contains `(1)` a natural-language optimization problem description, `(2)` prior solutions with scores sorted by score, and `(3)` optional meta-instructions that constrain style or output format.
- **Stability mechanism**: instead of one candidate, OPRO samples up to `8` new solutions per step to reduce variance and discover promising directions faster.
- **Exploration vs. exploitation**: optimizer sampling temperature is the main control knob; the default is `T = 1.0`, with ablations over `T ∈ {0.0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0}`.
- **Linear regression setup**: optimize one-dimensional coefficients `w` and `b` in `y = w_true x + b_true + ε` using `50` synthetic points, `5` random starting pairs, and the best `20` historical `(w, b)` pairs in the meta-prompt; the analytic formula is hidden so the task remains black-box.
- **TSP setup**: start from `5` random tours, generate up to `8` tours per step, sample node coordinates from `[-100, 100]`, and compute optimality gap against a Gurobi oracle, where insertion-based heuristics such as farthest insertion are used as non-LLM baselines.
- **Prompt optimization setup**: optimize instructions inserted at `Q_begin`, `Q_end`, or `A_begin`; use `3.5%` of GSM8K training examples or `20%` of BBH examples for scoring, keep the best `20` prior instructions and `3` exemplars in the meta-prompt, evaluate with scorer decoding temperature `0`, and use optimizer temperature `1.0`.
- **Ablation findings baked into the method**: ordering previous instructions from low to high score, exposing the scores explicitly, and sampling `8` candidates per step yield the best trade-off between optimization stability and search budget.

## Key Results

- On GSM8K with PaLM 2-L as scorer, OPRO with PaLM 2-L-IT as optimizer reaches `80.2%` test accuracy, beating `Let's think step by step.` at `71.8%` and `Let's solve the problem.` at `60.8%`.
- On the same GSM8K setup, PaLM 2-L as optimizer reaches `79.9%`, `gpt-3.5-turbo` reaches `78.5%`, and `gpt-4` reaches `74.5%`.
- On BBH, OPRO improves over `Let's think step by step.` by more than `5%` on `19/23` tasks with PaLM 2-L as scorer and on `15/23` tasks with text-bison as scorer.
- On linear regression with `(w_true, b_true) = (15, 14)`, `gpt-4` reaches the optimum in `4.0 ± 1.5` steps while exploring only `17.2 ± 5.1` unique pairs, versus `5.8 ± 2.6` steps for text-bison and `7.6 ± 4.5` for `gpt-3.5-turbo`.
- On TSP with `n = 10`, all LLMs achieve `0.0%` optimality gap, but `gpt-4` reaches optimum in `9.6 ± 3.0` steps versus `40.4 ± 5.6` for text-bison and `46.8 ± 9.3` for `gpt-3.5-turbo`.
- On TSP with `n = 20`, `gpt-4` still achieves `1.4 ± 0.6%` optimality gap, compared with `4.4 ± 2.5%` for `gpt-3.5-turbo` and `30.4 ± 10.6%` for text-bison.
- On TSP with `n = 50`, `gpt-4` degrades to `11.0 ± 2.6%` gap but remains far better than `gpt-3.5-turbo` at `133.0 ± 6.8%` and text-bison at `219.8 ± 13.7%`.
- Instructions optimized on GSM8K transfer to related math datasets, reaching `95.3% / 54.3%` on MultiArith / AQuA with PaLM 2-L as scorer.

## Limitations

OPRO is not competitive with specialized gradient-based optimizers or dedicated combinatorial solvers; the mathematical optimization experiments are proof-of-concept rather than state-of-the-art attempts. Performance degrades sharply as problem size grows, partly because prompt length limits make large optimization states hard to serialize into context. The method is sensitive to initialization, instruction wording, and temperature, and the paper documents failure modes where the LLM hallucinates objective values, repeats old candidates, or gets stuck on bumpy landscapes such as Rosenbrock-like valleys. Prompt optimization also still overfits: training accuracy can exceed validation or test accuracy by roughly `5%` to `20%`, and the approach requires a non-trivial labeled training subset to compute the optimization signal.
