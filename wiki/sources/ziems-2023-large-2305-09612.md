## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature.

This paper proposes LLM-URL, a training-free retrieval method that prompts large language models (GPT-3 `text-davinci-003`) to directly generate Wikipedia URLs as document identifiers for open-domain question answering (ODQA). Rather than training a dedicated retriever, the method exploits LLMs' parametric knowledge of web document structure via in-context demonstrations (zero-shot or few-shot with `d=10` examples). Retrieved documents are broken into passages and re-ranked with BM25 or DPR to produce a final passage set for a reader. Across three ODQA benchmarks (WebQ, NQ, TriviaQA), LLM-URL achieves document Recall@1 of 76.8–79.7% (WebQ), 61.7–62.6% (NQ), and 71.3–73.5% (TriviaQA), outperforming Contriever and BM25 by up to 24.9% relative. The work demonstrates that LLMs implicitly encode URL-to-document mappings, acting as built-in autoregressive search engines without task-specific training.

## Problem & Motivation

Document retrieval for knowledge-intensive NLP tasks historically relies on dual-encoder dense retrievers (DPR, Contriever) or sparse methods (BM25). These models encode questions and documents independently, allowing only shallow token interactions and potentially losing fine-grained information in a single dense vector. Autoregressive search engines (DSI, SEAL) overcome this by generating document identifiers, but their training cost scales sharply with corpus size and they cannot generalize to new tasks without retraining. A lightweight alternative that leverages existing LLM knowledge without corpus-specific training is desirable.

## Method

- **Core idea**: Treat URL generation as document retrieval. Prompt an LLM to generate `m` Wikipedia URLs in response to a question, then fetch those pages directly via HTTP GET requests. No index or fine-tuning required.
- **Prompt structure**: `"Which m Wikipedia URLs would have the answer? <question> https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki"` — the URL prefix is appended to prime the model for valid Wikipedia URL format.
- **Zero-shot setting**: prompt contains only the instruction + question.
- **Few-shot setting**: `d=10` in-context demonstrations of `(question, URL list)` pairs sampled from the training set are prepended.
- **Model**: GPT-3 `text-davinci-003`, greedy decoding, temperature `0`.
- **URL extraction**: regex extracts all valid URLs from the generated token sequence; malformed URLs and non-Wikipedia domains are discarded.
- **Passage filtering**: each fetched document `d ∈ D_f` is chunked into passages; a scorer (BM25 default, or DPR) ranks passages by relevance to question `q`; top-`n` passages `P_n` are passed to a reader.
- **Reader**: InstructGPT (GPT-3) prompted with `P_n` and `q` for answer generation.
- **Validity**: ~68%+ of generated URLs point to real Wikipedia pages when generating up to `m=10` URLs; validity rate decreases as `m` grows.

## Key Results

- **Document Recall@1** (LLM-URL few-shot vs. best baseline): WebQ 79.7% vs. 63.8% (Contriever, +24.9%); NQ 62.6% vs. 55.5% (Google API, +12.8%); TriviaQA 73.5% vs. 63.0% (BM25, +16.7%).
- **Document Recall@10** (few-shot): WebQ 89.9%, NQ 83.9%, TriviaQA 86.8%.
- **Passage Recall@1** (few-shot, BM25 ranker): WebQ 22.3%, NQ 25.5%, TriviaQA 49.1% — slight improvement over BM25/Contriever baselines.
- **Zero-shot QA EM** (few-shot + InstructGPT reader): WebQ 29.0% (+16.9% over best non-NDI baseline), TriviaQA 60.7% (+2.3%), NQ 27.3% (worse than DSI+FiD 31.4% and SEAL+FiD 43.6%).
- **Entity frequency analysis**: Recall@1 is substantially higher for common Wikipedia entities (top-1M) than uncommon ones, mirroring closed-book LLM behavior.
- **DSI/SEAL comparison (NQ, Recall@1/10)**: zero-shot LLM-URL 24.0/60.6% vs. SEAL 26.3/74.5% — LLM-URL lags on NQ passage retrieval against explicitly trained NDIs.

## Limitations

- **Parametric knowledge staleness**: LLM cannot retrieve documents about events after its training cutoff; adding new knowledge would require model retraining, unlike retrieve-then-read systems that can swap corpora.
- **Hallucination**: generates syntactically invalid or non-existent URLs; on average `m=10` generations yield only ~6–7 valid documents.
- **Common-entity bias**: retrieval performance drops sharply for uncommon entities, as the LLM's parametric knowledge is biased toward frequently occurring Wikipedia topics.
- **Latency and cost**: depends on very large LLMs, live HTTP requests, and document processing, making real-time deployment impractical.
- **Corpus scope**: restricted to Wikipedia URLs for fair comparison; generalization to other corpora (non-Wikipedia) is untested.
