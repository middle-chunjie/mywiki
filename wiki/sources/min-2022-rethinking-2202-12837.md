## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2022; re-verify against recent literature.

This paper asks what demonstrations actually contribute in in-context learning, and shows that the answer is not primarily the ground-truth input-label mapping. Across 12 decoder-only language models from `774M` to `175B`, evaluated on `26` classification and multi-choice datasets, replacing gold labels inside demonstrations with random labels usually causes only a small degradation. The authors argue that demonstrations help mainly by specifying the input distribution, label space, and sequence format rather than by teaching a new mapping at inference time. They further show that meta-trained models such as MetaICL rely even less on correct pairings and even more on easier prompt cues, sharpening the claim that much of in-context learning is retrieval and formatting of pretrained knowledge rather than true test-time task induction.

## Problem & Motivation

In-context learning is usually described as learning a new task from a few labeled demonstrations placed in the prompt, but prior work offered limited evidence about which part of those demonstrations is actually responsible for the gain over zero-shot inference. This paper tests a core assumption: whether large language models need the correct input-label correspondences in the prompt at all. The motivation is both scientific and practical. If gains mainly come from label-space hints, in-domain inputs, or prompt format, then current few-shot prompting may be less like on-the-fly supervised learning and more like activating priors already stored during pretraining.

## Method

- **Model set**: evaluate `12` decoder-only dense LMs using direct and channel inference variants, spanning GPT-2 Large / MetaICL at `774M`, GPT-J at `6B`, fairseq LMs at `6.7B` and `13B`, and GPT-3 at `175B`.
- **Task setup**: use `26` low-resource datasets across sentiment analysis, paraphrase detection, NLI, hate speech detection, QA, and sentence completion. Default demonstrations use `k = 16` examples, sampled uniformly from training data with `5` random seeds; fairseq `13B` and GPT-3 use a `6`-dataset subset with `3` seeds.
- **Prediction formulations**: zero-shot uses `` `argmax_y P(y | x)` ``; standard demonstrations use `` `argmax_y P(y | x_1,y_1,...,x_k,y_k,x)` ``; random-label demonstrations replace each `y_i` with a uniformly sampled `` `\tilde{y}_i ∈ C` `` and reuse the same prompting interface.
- **Metrics and prompt formatting**: report Macro-F1 on classification and Accuracy on multi-choice tasks; minimal templates are the default, with manual templates included as an ablation.
- **Correctness ablation**: create demonstrations with `` `a%` `` correct labels by mixing `` `k × a / 100` `` gold pairs and `` `k × (1 - a / 100)` `` incorrect pairs, testing whether accuracy depends smoothly on true pairings.
- **Input-distribution ablation**: replace in-domain demonstration inputs with out-of-distribution text sampled from CC-News while preserving label space and prompt format, isolating the effect of input distribution.
- **Label-space ablation**: replace task labels with random English words, enforcing `` `|C_rand| = |C|` ``, to measure whether knowledge of the output label space matters independently of correct pairings.
- **Format ablation**: test demonstrations with no labels and labels only, removing input-label pairing structure while retaining only one side of the prompt evidence.
- **Meta-training analysis**: compare MetaICL, initialized from GPT-2 Large and meta-trained with an in-context-learning objective, against non-meta-trained LMs to study whether explicit meta-training changes which prompt signals the model exploits.

## Key Results

- Replacing gold labels with random labels causes only `0-5%` absolute degradation overall; the average drop is `2.6%` on classification and `1.7%` on multi-choice tasks.
- MetaICL is especially insensitive to correct pairings, with only `0.1-0.9%` absolute drop from gold to random labels.
- When varying label correctness, always-incorrect labels still preserve `92%` of the in-context-learning gain for MetaICL classification, `100%` for MetaICL multi-choice, and `97%` for GPT-J multi-choice; GPT-J classification is the main exception with nearly `10%` absolute loss.
- Across different numbers of demonstrations, the gold-vs-random gap stays small at `0.8-1.6%`, and performance largely saturates once `k >= 8`.
- Replacing in-domain inputs with OOD text drops performance by `3-16%` absolute for Channel MetaICL, Direct GPT-J, and Channel GPT-J, showing that input distribution matters substantially.
- Swapping the true label space for random English words hurts direct models by `5-16%` absolute, but channel models only by `0-2%`, indicating asymmetric dependence on output-space cues.
- With the right prompt format, the model can retain up to `95%` and `82%` of the original gain in Direct MetaICL for classification and multi-choice respectively, and `82%/87%/86%/75%` in four channel-model settings using only inputs or only label-like cues.

## Limitations

- The study covers only classification and multi-choice benchmarks; extension to open-set generation is explicitly left unresolved because preserving output distribution while removing input-output correspondence is hard.
- Conclusions are mostly macro-averaged over datasets, so individual dataset-model pairs can deviate sharply; the appendix reports an extreme case of nearly `14%` absolute gap on Financial Phrasebank with GPT-J.
- The central interpretation assumes tasks whose input-label associations are already represented in pretraining; unfamiliar or synthetic tasks may rely much more on genuine demonstration supervision.
- The analysis is empirical rather than mechanistic: it shows which prompt factors correlate with gains, but it does not prove the internal causal process by which a model recovers or ignores a task mapping.
