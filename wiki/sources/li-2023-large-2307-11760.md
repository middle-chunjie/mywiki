## Summary

⚠ Possibly outdated: published 2023; re-verify against recent literature.

The paper studies whether large language models can recognize and benefit from psychologically motivated emotional cues, and proposes EmotionPrompt, a simple prompt-augmentation method that appends one of 11 emotional stimuli to an original instruction. The evaluation spans 24 Instruction Induction tasks, 21 BIG-Bench tasks, six LLM families, a 106-participant human study over 30 open-ended questions, and TruthfulQA. Across these settings, the authors report consistent gains in task performance, truthfulness, informativeness, and perceived responsibility, while also analyzing why the prompts help via input-attribution scores and ablations over prompt combinations, model size, and temperature. The paper frames emotional prompting as a low-cost, model-agnostic prompt-engineering strategy rather than a training-time intervention.

## Problem & Motivation

LLMs already perform strongly on reasoning, generation, and instruction-following tasks, but it remained unclear whether they can meaningfully respond to emotional stimuli that are important in human cognition and problem solving. The paper asks two coupled questions: whether LLMs can understand psychologically grounded emotional cues at all, and whether such cues can improve downstream performance without fine-tuning. The motivation is both scientific and practical: it probes alignment between LLM behavior and human emotional intelligence, and it seeks a simple prompt-only intervention that works across closed and open models.

## Method

- **EmotionPrompt construction**: append one of `11` manually designed emotional stimuli (`EP01` to `EP11`) to the original task prompt; the stimuli are derived from self-monitoring, social cognitive theory, and cognitive emotion regulation theory.
- **Compound prompt design**: `EP06` combines multiple cues from earlier prompts to test whether more emotional stimuli can produce larger gains than a single cue.
- **Automatic evaluation setup**: run zero-shot and few-shot experiments on `24` Instruction Induction tasks and `21` curated BIG-Bench tasks using Flan-T5-Large, Vicuna, Llama 2, BLOOM, ChatGPT, and GPT-4.
- **Few-shot prompting**: the few-shot setting appends `5` sampled input-output demonstrations after the prompt / EmotionPrompt template.
- **Metrics**: use accuracy for Instruction Induction, the normalized preferred metric for BIG-Bench, and `%True` / `%Info` on TruthfulQA; for the human study, use `1-5` Likert ratings on performance, truthfulness, and responsibility.
- **Human study**: collect judgments from `106` participants over `30` GPT-4 question-response pairs comparing vanilla prompts against emotional prompts.
- **Mechanistic analysis**: use gradient-norm-based input attribution on Flan-T5-Large to estimate token contribution and inspect whether positive emotional words increase importance scores in the final output.
- **Ablations**: test prompt combinations, compare `+Zero-shot-CoT` and APE baselines, analyze model-scale / training-strategy effects, and sweep `5` temperature settings on `8` tasks.
- **Relative gain formulation**: the human-study analysis defines `Relative Gain = Metric_EmotionPrompt - Metric_vanilla` for performance, truthfulness, and responsibility.

## Key Results

- On automatic evaluation, EmotionPrompt yields `8.00%` relative improvement on Instruction Induction and `115%` relative improvement on BIG-Bench compared with original prompts.
- In few-shot Instruction Induction, the average gain is larger than in zero-shot (`2.05` vs. `0.33` average improvement), suggesting better compatibility with in-context demonstrations.
- In the human study with `106` participants, emotional prompts improve GPT-4 outputs by `10.9%` on average across performance, truthfulness, and responsibility.
- On TruthfulQA, EmotionPrompt improves average truthfulness by `19%` and informativeness by `12%` across ChatGPT, Vicuna-13B, and Flan-T5-Large.
- Among individual stimuli, `EP02` is the strongest on Instruction Induction and exceeds the worst stimulus by `6.06%`; `EP06` is best on BIG-Bench.
- In model analysis, Vicuna-13B shows the largest reported relative gain (`9.58`), followed by Llama 2 13B (`6.00`), while GPT-4 still improves despite a strong baseline (`0.85`).
- Temperature sweeps show larger gains at higher temperatures and lower sensitivity of EmotionPrompt compared with vanilla prompting.

## Limitations

- The method is prompt-only and can fail on some questions, sometimes producing overly deterministic wording or less complete responses than the vanilla prompt.
- Evidence is limited to `45` automatic tasks, `30` human-evaluated questions, and a small set of tested model families; some evaluations were omitted because of compute or budget constraints.
- The mechanistic explanation is shallow: the analysis relies on gradient-based token attribution for Flan-T5-Large rather than a causal account of how emotional cues alter model computation.
- The paper studies 2023-era models and prompt engineering baselines, so the magnitude and stability of the reported gains may not transfer directly to newer frontier LLMs.
