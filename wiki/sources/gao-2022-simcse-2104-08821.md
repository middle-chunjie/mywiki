## Summary

âš  Possibly outdated: published 2022; re-verify against recent literature.

SimCSE proposes an unusually simple recipe for sentence representation learning: run the same sentence through a pre-trained encoder twice, rely on independent dropout masks to create a positive pair, and contrast it against other examples in the mini-batch. The paper also introduces a supervised variant that uses NLI entailment pairs as positives and contradiction pairs as hard negatives. Starting from BERT or RoBERTa checkpoints, SimCSE substantially improves average Spearman correlation on standard STS benchmarks while offering an analysis of why it works: contrastive learning improves representation-space uniformity, supervised pairs improve alignment, and the resulting objective alleviates the anisotropy of pre-trained embedding spaces without requiring complex textual augmentation.

## Problem & Motivation

Universal sentence embeddings were still lagging behind strong task-specific systems, and prior unsupervised objectives such as next-sentence prediction or discrete text augmentation produced limited gains. The paper asks whether a much simpler contrastive setup, built directly on pre-trained encoders, can learn stronger sentence representations without elaborate augmentation pipelines. It further argues that sentence embedding quality should be understood not only through benchmark scores but also through the geometry of the embedding space, especially the trade-off between positive-pair alignment and global uniformity.

## Method

- **Base contrastive objective**: for paired sentences `(x_i, x_i^+)`, optimize `` `ell_i = -log(exp(sim(h_i, h_i^+) / tau) / sum_j exp(sim(h_i, h_j^+) / tau))` `` with cosine similarity and temperature `` `tau` ``.
- **Unsupervised SimCSE**: set `` `x_i^+ = x_i` `` and encode the same sentence twice with independently sampled dropout masks, yielding `` `h_i^z = f_theta(x_i, z)` `` and `` `h_i^z' = f_theta(x_i, z')` ``. Standard Transformer dropout alone is used as augmentation, with default probability `` `p = 0.1` ``.
- **Supervised SimCSE**: use NLI entailment pairs as positives and extend each training tuple to `` `(x_i, x_i^+, x_i^-)` ``, where `` `x_i^-` `` is a contradiction hypothesis. The loss becomes `` `-log(exp(sim(h_i, h_i^+) / tau) / sum_j (exp(sim(h_i, h_j^+) / tau) + exp(sim(h_i, h_j^-) / tau)))` ``.
- **Hard-negative weighting**: an ablation adds a factor `` `alpha^(1_i^j)` `` to emphasize the matched contradiction example; the best setting is `` `alpha = 1` ``, so the final model keeps the simple hard-negative formulation.
- **Encoders and pooling**: initialize from uncased BERT or cased RoBERTa checkpoints; use the `[CLS]` representation with an MLP during training, and for unsupervised SimCSE remove the MLP at test time because it improves STS performance.
- **Training data**: unsupervised SimCSE uses `` `10^6` `` random English Wikipedia sentences; supervised SimCSE uses SNLI and MNLI entailment pairs, plus contradiction pairs as hard negatives.
- **Key hyperparameters**: the supervised models train for `` `3` `` epochs; grid search covers batch size `` `{64, 128, 256, 512}` `` and learning rate `` `{1e-5, 3e-5, 5e-5}` ``. Best configurations include BERT-base `` `batch = 64, lr = 3e-5` ``, BERT-large `` `64, 1e-5` ``, RoBERTa-base `` `512, 1e-5` ``, and RoBERTa-large `` `512, 3e-5` ``.
- **Objective analysis**: the paper studies `` `l_align = E ||f(x) - f(x^+)||^2` `` and `` `l_uniform = log E exp(-2 ||f(x) - f(y)||^2)` ``, and argues that the contrastive term flattens the singular-value spectrum of the embedding matrix, mitigating anisotropy.
- **Evaluation protocol**: all STS results are reported with cosine similarity and Spearman correlation under the paper's unified "all" aggregation setting; Appendix D shows `` `tau = 0.05` `` is the best temperature for supervised SimCSE on STS-B dev.

## Key Results

- Unsupervised SimCSE-BERT-base reaches `76.25` average Spearman on 7 STS tasks, improving the previous best unsupervised baseline (`72.05` for CT-BERT-base) by `4.20` points.
- Supervised SimCSE-BERT-base reaches `81.57` average STS, outperforming CT-SBERT-base (`79.39`) by `2.18` points.
- The best overall model, supervised SimCSE-RoBERTa-large, reaches `83.76` average STS and `86.70` on STS-B.
- On STS-B dev, unsupervised BERT-base SimCSE scores `82.5`, compared with `74.2` without dropout and only `43.6` when the same dropout mask is reused across the positive pair.
- For supervised training data, NLI entailment pairs outperform QQP (`81.8`), Flickr30k (`81.4`), and ParaNMT (`78.7`); using SNLI+MNLI entailment plus contradiction hard negatives raises STS-B dev from `84.9` to `86.2`.
- Temperature tuning matters: Appendix D shows supervised SimCSE peaks at `86.2` on STS-B dev with cosine similarity and `` `tau = 0.05` ``, while `` `tau = 1` `` drops to `64.0`.

## Limitations

- The method is evaluated primarily on English STS and a standard set of transfer tasks, so its cross-lingual and domain-transfer behavior is not established here.
- Unsupervised SimCSE depends on a pre-trained encoder with already good alignment; the paper explicitly notes that the pre-trained checkpoint is crucial for avoiding degenerate behavior.
- The supervised variant relies on NLI data quality and structure, which may limit applicability in settings without entailment/contradiction annotations.
- The paper's main gains are on sentence similarity benchmarks; even with an auxiliary MLM loss, transfer-task improvements are less consistent than the STS gains.
- The analysis links contrastive learning to anisotropy reduction, but the theoretical argument is still an approximation around normalized embeddings and large-negative asymptotics rather than a full general proof.
